Chapter 1

...Buy a puppet for your daughter, signore, buy one. You won't find better puppets than mine!

The patrician twitched an irritated eyebrow. The bodyguard read him correctly and stepped toward the bearded, fat, and seemingly dishevelled as a sick crow plebeian, lowering his hand to the hilt of his broadsword. Nothing good awaited the plebeian, but the inevitable was halted by a demanding little girl's voice:

— I want this puppet, Papa! She's interesting!

The patrician regarded the wooden figurine with its disproportionately long nose with some doubt. The dishevelled fat man, hearing the daughter's voice, had nimbly and obsequiously thrust it forward, grinning wide with a gap-toothed maw that seemed capable of swallowing the patrician whole.

The marionette was rather crudely made — its little arms and legs were uneven in thickness, the nose — one's attention kept returning to the nose again and again: it was long and sharp as a stiletto blade.

From the nose, one's gaze was seized by the marionette's eyes. And it was precisely these that were the most unusual thing about the entire puppet. The marionette's eyes seemed alive and, at the same time, luminous.

"What a little freak!" — the patrician mentally shuddered.

— Papa! I want her! I want her! — the daughter would not relent. Ever since his wife had perished, the patrician had spoiled their child, who had become for him the embodiment of his departed love.

With a heavy sigh he gestured with the fingers of his raised hand. The bodyguard nodded and produced a purse from inside his coat.

— How much for this wooden thing of yours?

— Oh, not much. A mere ten silver coins, my lord...

— HOW MUCH??? — the bodyguard nearly lost the power of speech at the seller's audacity. — I'll give you ten copper coins for it. And thank the patrician for his great mercy! This pile of firewood isn't worth even a single copper!

— But my labour... My hard work... — the fat man began to whine.

The bodyguard yanked the marionette from the greasy fingers, tossed a few coins on the ground, and wiping the toy with his cloak — handed it to its new owner. The girl immediately hugged the puppet and pressed it to herself. The patrician sighed deeply, imagining how he would flinch for a while — stumbling upon the little freak in the darkness of his daughter's room.

...Deep in the catacombs of the Eternal City, a Servant of Shadows, member of the Guild of Soul Catchers and Senior Malefic of the Lesser Circle, Kraabas del Baarbos opened a jadeite casket with an intricate carving on its pale green lid. The carving depicted a marionette with a long nose, holding someone's heart in an outstretched hand.

Inside the casket lay eight rings. Pale blue, of translucent stone — each ring rested in its own recess.

Kraabas closed his eyes and began to chant a long litany. In the ancient burial niches came a rustling — old bones stirring, sensing the churning of the dark essence of being that had answered the malefic's song. The temperature in the tiny chamber was dropping rapidly.

With a thin chime like a snapping string — one of the rings leapt up and slipped itself onto Kraabas's little finger. From beneath the ring seeped a single small drop of blood. The malefic didn't even raise an eyebrow. This was far from his first commission. He knew that seven more such drops awaited him.

Kraabas was never interested in his clients' motives. Nevertheless — he always learned them. Such was fate.

The patrician was guilty of his wife's death. Born a Pauletti. The motto of the Elder House of Pauletti read: "The Paulettis always pay."

It was hard to say whether this originally referred to debts or to revenge, but since none of the descendants knew the right answer either, the Paulettis — just to be safe — paid all accounts.

The patrician could not explain the death of young Giulietta.

One of the rulers of the Eternal City, of course, could not fail to know the consequences. He surrounded himself with guards, turned his home into a fortress...

Another ring took its place.

Through the ventilation shaft, from the surface, came the toll of the watch bell, announcing the onset of the Dark Hour. From this moment — the watch was forbidden to ring, and common folk — to step outside. It was believed that after this toll — all the undead and evil creatures emerged onto the streets. Was it so? Old guardsmen told many tales, if you treated them properly at the tavern.

Kraabas, however, knew the answer without them.

Another ring took its place on his finger. The malefic closed his eyes.

Wavering, like a twilight vision, like a hallucination — before his eyes appeared a child's room. Drawings on the finest rice paper, hung on the walls, toys scattered across the furs on the floor.

Another ring slid onto a finger. And another...

When all the rings had taken their places — the malefic lightly hopped off the chair and walked across the furs. The room was faintly lit by the eyes of the malefic's new body.

He walked back and forth across the soft fur. Then, grabbing the edge of the blanket with tiny hands, he easily threw himself onto the bed. The girl lay hugging an old toy of her mother's. A doll depicting some unknown creature. Perhaps it had once been a little bear?

Be that as it may, Kraabas knew something more about this little beast.

The puppet, whose body he was presently controlling, approached the toy. Grasped the body with one hand and the head with the other, and with a deft twist pulled them in opposite directions.

In the fragile wooden hands — there turned out to be unexpectedly great strength. The head separated easily from the furry torso, revealing the hilt of a small but remarkably sharp stiletto. The marionette pulled it from the body. In the puppet's hands, the dagger looked like a hand-and-a-half sword.

Be that as it may, Kraabas had been warned about this toy. An old gift to a little girl from her father. A girl growing up in an atmosphere of eternal conspiracies and intrigues. The beloved daughter of the head of a Great House. He did not know that Kraabas was already acquainted with her. Very well acquainted...

Kraabas glanced once more at the girl. Just one movement... But the client had given specific instructions regarding the granddaughter. The puppet turned away and in a single leap was on the floor.

To the unknowing, the patrician's house might have seemed an absolute labyrinth. However, for the malefic there was but one path. A distinct, scarlet, pulsing ribbon of life, visible only to his dark sight — led him to the far study. The patrician was still awake. Sitting at his desk, writing something, cross-referencing with an enormous, thick ledger.

The marionette silently crossed the room and latched onto the back leg of the chair. A guard peered through the door. Seeing nothing of interest, he pulled the door shut.

The marionette pulled itself up and was on the back of the chair.

At some point — the patrician sensed something amiss and turned his head.

The last thing he saw in his life were two eyes burning with an otherworldly, icy blue fire, on an uneven, motionless, roughly carved wooden face. Then the thin stiletto blade slipped unimpeded into his ear, piercing the brain and delivering a most potent poison into his body.

In the next instant, the puppet's body was engulfed in the same blue flame and crumbled into weightless silvery ash... Though even the ash never reached the floor.

In the empty study sat only the dead body of the patrician, from whose ear protruded the hilt of the very dagger with which his wife had been murdered. A different contract — different rules.

Deep in the catacombs, in the malefic's hands, the long-nosed wooden body appeared in a flash of fire.

The head turned on its thin neck. The eyes shifted from blue to red. The painted mouth opened, revealing an endless chasm of oozing flesh. The stench of stale, bad blood wafted forth.

— That was a tasty soul, slave. I think I'll give you another year of life. Would have given three — but you denied me the young life to feast upon.

The marionette's body went lifeless once more.

The soul-sold sorcerer shuddered and came out of his trance. With a chime, the rings fell into the casket and rolled to their places.

The slave lifted his master and with the proper litany placed him in a reliquary fashioned from the wood of a hanged man's gallows.

Another year lay ahead. How he would pay for the next one — Kraabas did not think about. He was used to it. And his profession remained in demand always and in all ages.

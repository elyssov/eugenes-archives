Title: Diary of the End of the World

Author: Eugene Lyssovsky

Chapter 1

Day -1 of the new era. September 16, 20.. Friday.

The psychologist said that to manage stress I should keep a diary. Utter nonsense, but let's give it a try. So — hello, diary. We're driving to the dacha. Brought a bucket of kebab meat; people are coming on Saturday. The traffic is killing me. The Minsk highway is a nightmare. Almost 200 kilometres to go.

The wife will sleep the entire way. Again.

Brought my Saiga along — been promising Vano for ages I'd take him to the forest, to my "secret shooting range."

Right, I'm done navel-gazing. Time to move out.

Day 1.

Diary, hi. Once again, like an idiot, writing random crap. Alex apparently won't make it. Got called in urgently to work, at the Ministry of Defence. Vano and Svetka are coming. Irka finished making salads and is sprawled in the hammock. I just got back from the supermarket — brought another pack of Coca-Cola, toilet paper... Do I really have to list everything here?

Fine. What to write about? Ah. Shprot stole a piece of kebab. Currently growling and gnawing on it under the porch. Can't be arsed to get up and take it away. From the direction of the Minsk highway — non-stop car horns. A continuous roar. Are they seeing Putin off again? He doesn't usually come out this far. Oh, it stopped. Must have passed.

Day 3.

This is fucked. This is fucked. This is fucked.

The psychologist was right. It does get easier when you write.

The burns hurt less now. Didn't know that happens. Like lying on a beach too long in the sun. Still incredibly sleepy. Need to go check on Ira.

Day 5.

Sitting in the cellar. Eating raw but well-marinated kebab. Barely talking. Yesterday I ran to the car and the fridge. Dragged everything I could down to the cellar. There was black rain. I've heard that's very dangerous, so at the first drops I fled back to the cellar. Grabbed the first-aid kit from the car too. Mixed iodine with Shishkin Les mineral water. Drank it. Absolutely disgusting, but I've heard it's the only way to protect yourself from radiation. All the vodka was gone long before that.

Day 8.

More rain again. Three times in a row. The ash seems to have washed off. Going outside is terrifying, but we can't sit in the cellar forever.

Food's run out. Well, there's rubbish like crisps left. As I understand it — nothing from the garden is safe to eat either. Worse — water is running out.

Day 9.

Went to the water pump. No water. Then went to the neighbours' — they have something like an artesian well on their property. So now we have water. Loads of water. Ira has more or less come to her senses. She was helping me. We carried water, poured it everywhere — I read that's how decontamination is done. Cleaned up the house. It barely suffered, just the paint darkened in a few spots.

Day 10.

Slept in the house. For the first time. Got a proper night's sleep. Feeling great. Burns completely healed, hair isn't falling out. Basically — looks like we got lucky. I saw the mushroom cloud from the direction of Vyazma. They must have dropped one there. There were many from the Moscow direction. Well, more like flashes. Lots of them.

Day 11.

Walked through the neighbours' houses — found almost nobody, except old Grandpa Opanas. The old man is deaf and senile. Oh, and I found a body. Those were the new people — they'd built themselves a huge brick house on the edge of the village. Apparently he got caught in the black rain. Horrific sight: blisters, blood, pulled-out hair. Ugh, already feeling sick again.

Day 12.

Decided to drive to the supermarket. The food we scrounged from the neighbours is almost gone. Irka is perfectly fine. Cracking jokes, teasing me. By the way, while we're driving — I'll charge the mobile. You never know. Although there's no electricity in the networks.

The car started! Hurray for Ford!

Irka had the idea to turn on the radio — on every channel, the same thing: "Attention! This is the Civil Defence headquarters! Citizens — if you can hear this signal, proceed to the nearest evacuation point. Evacuation points within range of this broadcast are located at:..." Then a list follows. When it finishes — the whole message repeats. We've decided to head for Gagarin. We'll still stop at the supermarket on the way though. Remembered "Mad Max" — loaded the Saiga just in case.

Day 14.

Loads of events. In front of the supermarket — there was a police patrol. The moment we appeared — two men with assault rifles took aim at us. Ordered us out of the car. Demanded documents. Searched the vehicle. Took the Saiga. While all this was happening — right before our eyes, some soldiers in camouflage were loading boxes from the shop into a truck.

After the check — the police told us to park the car and wait for evacuation with them. One of them went to the car and spoke to someone on the radio.

A couple of hours later — a real APC arrived. They helped us climb inside. Incredibly uncomfortable. Nevertheless — as soon as we got onto the highway, it became clear that nothing but an APC could have passed. Hundreds of cars abandoned right on the road.

Took about an hour and a half to reach Gagarin, going slow. On site — found loads of military. They'd set up a tent camp on the outskirts of town. That's where they put us, though they ordered us to report to the local military registration office in the morning. The camp has electricity, light, hot water. Literally from the front door, they checked us with some instruments, said we'd caught surprisingly little, then confiscated all our clothes and sent us to wash. Finally washed properly. At the exit from the showers — the camp quartermaster issued old, washed-out uniforms. Soviet-era, I think. Well, almost all civilians here are dressed the same. Must be coming from the reserves.

Day 15.

At the registration office, they spent a long time finding out who could do what. Learned more about the military. I gather it's one of the Moscow Region units that they didn't waste a bomb on. Drivers or a logistics regiment or whatever they're called. Basically — not real warriors. But — they work in good coordination. All civilians were given some sort of assignments. Me, upon learning I'm a manager — they ordered to report to Warrant Officer Sitnov. Turns out he's in charge of supply. Accordingly, our job is to "carry the round things, roll the square things," as he told us. Got back to camp past midnight, but collected two truckloads of food, medicine and even gas masks from one of the hardware shops from the surrounding village shops. Along the way — picked up thirty-odd civilians. People are starting to come alive and crawl out.

Day 17.

Today four people were killed before my eyes. Here's what happened: we, accompanied by police, arrived at the warehouses near Artyomki. And found six looters there. The police demanded they surrender immediately. In response, they opened fire. Shotguns, by the sound of it. One of ours caught buckshot in the shoulder. Meanwhile, the warrant officer and two regular soldiers (not volunteers like us) went round the flanks "through the gardens." The looters were distracted by the police, who laid down harassing automatic fire.

The result: our lads popped out behind the looters and with three barrels dropped four of them. The other two turned out to be merely wounded. Weapons confiscated, patched up, loaded into the truck bed. The entire time we were clearing the warehouse, they said nothing. Only when we were already handing over to the guys from Mozhaisk (another collection point there) did one of the looters start asking to see "the boss." I ran for the warrant officer. The wounded man started begging the sergeant to save their families. Claimed they weren't stealing for themselves but for several families left in a village called Sudakovo. Sitnov brought a map, the wounded man showed him his village, stuck in some godforsaken middle of nowhere (though we'd driven through Vlasovo and Baranovo a few times back in the day... Before the war...).

Then, reckoning this fell within Mozhaisk's area of responsibility, he radioed the local camp. I was smoking nearby, so I heard him recommend they send not a truck but a BMP and an armed platoon. Because "it could be a rat trap."

Day 18.

Today, under martial law, the remaining looters were shot. "For looting and resisting the armed forces of the Russian Federation in wartime." Ours wasn't the only unit, of course, so looters had accumulated. Ten were led to the wall. Four were wounded. No theatrics, nothing. Led out, the garrison commander's order was read, machine guns blazed. Businesslike.

Also — today the reconnaissance returned from Moscow. Well, more precisely: some guys in a special APC (they called it an RKhM in conversation, I think) drove toward Moscow a couple of days ago. The mission was to see if anything remained of Kubinka and how bad things generally were.

Turned out — very bad. Still loads of fires burning. Not everything has burned yet. Approaching the MKAD on the main roads is simply impossible. If in our backwoods the cars are still relatively thinly spread, by Kubinka the road is one solid traffic jam. And even an APC doesn't have enough power to push cars off the road. So they went round.

They managed to approach the MKAD around Rublyovka. Contamination is already quite moderate, but judging by what they saw, no one in the city survived. Continuous fires, not a single building of any height left. Maybe some people are in shelters. It was decided to send a couple of tanks with blades into the city as bulldozers to push cars onto the verges. I volunteered for this expedition.

Day 20.

Set out. Moving briskly. Tanks in front, behind them our eight APCs. We're sitting in the first two. The rest are empty — for evacuating civilians. The operation is fully coordinated with the Moscow Region Civil Defence headquarters. As I understand it, the HQ is in some bunker and maintains radio contact with all surviving units. Generally, I've noticed we live one day at a time. I still haven't even properly found out what this was, how the war started — nothing.

Yes, as I already wrote — the operation is synchronised. We enter the city from the west, guys from the Dmitrov area from the north. And so on. The idea is to pull survivors from the outskirts. The centre, from what I gathered from the commanders' conversations, has already been written off wholesale. Well, judging by those same conversations, there's nobody to rescue there.

The chem-suit — or officially, the OZK — is wildly uncomfortable. Even given that the gas masks are still in their bags for now — we're relying on the APCs' filtered ventilation. I've still got 20/20 vision, thank God. How people with glasses survive — I can't even imagine! Specs definitely don't fit inside a gas mask!

Irka, by the way, has proven herself in the kitchen. I always said — marry only plump girls: they are, in themselves, a walking advertisement for their culinary talents! As a result, she's now the assistant to the camp's head cook.

Anyway, more later — writing in the three-fingered OZK gloves is wildly uncomfortable.

Day 23.

Returned from Moscow. Don't really feel like writing. Captain Kharlamov, who commanded the raid, ordered 200 grams issued to everyone upon return. Sound decision.

Day 24.

Must drink less. Drink less. Much less. Very much. Oh, my head!

But — there's amazing news: the President went on air.

Nothing specific, unfortunately. The traditional "Hold on! Stay strong! We shall endure! We shall prevail! We shall stand firm!"

The glow over Moscow is noticeably dimmer now. Today I can finally organise my thoughts from our rescue expedition.

First off — the trip was worth it. We brought out almost fifteen hundred people. Obviously not on four APCs. Had to call for reinforcements. All these people had been hiding in old Soviet-era shelters.

But overall, what I saw in the city — chills you to the bone. Practically everything that stuck up more than two metres above the ground has been sheared off. Almost two dozen warheads hit the city, according to the captain. Very visible indeed.

The ground is uneven. Feels like there was an earthquake. As I understand it, some of the warheads were earth-penetrating. For destroying bunkers. We could only drive deeper into the city as far as Poklonnaya Gora. Beyond that — no way. Literally a ploughed field.

The outskirts are rough too. The entire Molodyozhnaya district is solid broken brick from the local khrushchyovkas. Slightly better in Krylatskoe. That's where we had the biggest haul of survivors, as it turned out — the whole district is riddled with bomb shelters.

Every street is covered in remains. Some — rotting chunks of corpses. Some — shadows. There, it depended on your luck. The outskirts suffered less than the centre, so more horrors were preserved. Barely held it together and didn't puke into my gas mask. And taking it off was absolutely out of the question. From the moment we first climbed out of the APC to the moment of decontamination — the mask was to be worn non-stop. However, a couple of our guys couldn't take it. Ripped off their masks and emptied their stomachs. They were immediately given medication from the first-aid kit, but what'll happen to them — honestly, I don't know. Maybe they'll be lucky. Maybe not.

Several times we came across absolutely horrific corpses of people who'd survived the bombardment itself.

People with half-charred bodies, with burned-out, seared eyes, people with severed limbs, people with ghastly wounds... All their poses indicated they had been trying to run somewhere after the bombing.

Here I am writing all this — and it gets easier. Thank you, Natalya Ivanovna. I don't know what's happened to you. You were supposed to fly to Spain Saturday morning. Did you make it?

Day 150.

Almost six months have passed. Irka is pregnant. I got a rank. Now I'm a sergeant. Almost everyone has ranks now. Or rather — you can either be a soldier or dead weight. For a woman, that's acceptable. For a man — not an option.

We're still living around Gagarin, but no longer in the field camp — in communal, rapid-assembly barracks. From the homeland's reserves came stocks of tools, food — loads of everything.

The winter was savagely cold. Understandable. The days are grey. Like permanent twilight. Though at least it's not eternal night, as I'd imagined.

Nevertheless — the forecasts are quite encouraging. The government believes that nuclear winter, as such, will not happen. There will be a temporary temperature drop of a few degrees. But not permanent snow. And it does seem that way. At any rate, although it's colder than usual, there have already been thaws. And the haze is gradually clearing.

Radioactive contamination, while persisting in places, has mostly returned to background levels. Perhaps slightly elevated.

As a result of the war, we completely lost Moscow, St. Petersburg, Nizhny Novgorod, Samara, Novosibirsk and a load of other cities. Nevertheless, most relatively small towns survived. Moreover, because the bombs fell on a Saturday afternoon when people were driving to their dachas, far more survived than if the strike had come on a weekday.

What damage we inflicted on the USA — I don't know, but according to rumours, they're even worse off than us. At any rate, the radio guys say that while you can still find our radio hams on the air, the Yanks are hopeless. Though maybe it's just ionospheric disturbance?

Strictly speaking — I only know one thing:

Humanity made an enormous mistake. Nevertheless — this mistake did not prove fatal. And that means — life goes on. The main thing is — we managed to prevent chaos. And as long as people are united and working together — every hardship can be overcome.

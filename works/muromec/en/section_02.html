Chapter 2

Not to lose my mind — I needed every ounce of willpower I had. And even that probably wasn't enough. In a way, I was lucky. Vanya turned out to be a decent and reasonably clever young man. And I could feel that he genuinely liked me, in a human way. Had I been a living person and not a three-kilogram lump of neural node, I'd probably have been happy. Only I was no longer a person. The very thought plunged me into a state of some infinite horror and despair.

I held on and tried not to cry — I could feel I was causing Vanya pain (the lad had a big, kind heart, it seemed), but somewhere deep inside myself, I felt an emptiness into which I screamed with all my might. It was very strange and incredibly hopeless...

I didn't understand how or why I had survived. But since things had turned out this way... I'll be honest: my first thought, once I'd grasped the situation, was suicide. Well, euthanasia more precisely — I figured I could instruct Vanya on how to effectively terminate the neural node. However, I very quickly realised he would never do it. I could sense his shock, discomfort, even fear... But alongside them, I could read determination. A readiness to hold the line and care for me. That also caused me pain... But at the same time, it warmed my soul.

The news that more than 30 years had passed since my death — I took calmly. Apparently, once you've personally delivered your own body to a neighbouring rooftop, you acquire a certain resilience.

The world around me had changed. Half an hour ago, Vanya had been talking to one of his friends on a "mobile." Something like that had been described in our science fiction — the far-future kind. A little slab with a screen, thinner than a bar of chocolate, on which you could talk to anyone who had one too. And watch films on the tiny screen. Or see the person you were speaking to (I hadn't seen that mode yet, but Vanya had told me it existed). I was somewhat troubled that the device's name was written in Latin letters — "Samsung" — but I decided not to ask for more information just yet, lest I go mad from the overload.

While we drove to the shop, which Vanya called a "hypermarket," I also assessed his car. He was probably from a nomenklatura family (the mobile phone, in my view, also pointed directly to that). The shop was likewise astonishing! Enormous, packed to the rafters with every kind of product. Sausage alone — twenty varieties... Maybe more... My eyes kept wandering, and Vanya apparently let me have the controls on purpose. As a result, we rammed into some very rotund comrade who, accompanied by a strange, sort-of-pretty-but-very-doll-like woman, was pushing an enormous trolley full of goods. The comrade, judging by his bearing, was a responsible Party functionary, and he was very indignant at our (well, from his perspective — Vanya's) behaviour. We were told we'd "come crawling in from somewhere" (I don't know why this important comrade addressed Ivan in the plural — he couldn't have known about my presence) and many unprintable words. It ended with Vanya losing his temper and also swearing, which made me blush to the roots of my hair, and the stout comrade huffing off in anger.

Generally, in this situation, what struck me was that Vanya remembered himself and his own problems only AFTER dealing with... my body, and calming me down.

— Listen. This neural shunt thing in my head...

— Neuroport.

— Whatever. Is it going to kill me? It was in you... And then lay there for so many years...

— Honestly, I don't think so. I didn't work on that part of the program, but I heard it's some very clever biological construct that's almost alive in itself and forms an almost symbiotic bond with the brain. And it's very well protected against bacterial and viral contamination. I suspect that when Muromets extracted it from me — he sterilised it inside his medical module. Did I not tell you Muromets has a very advanced medical module? Capable of healing wounds on the move, stopping bleeding, and so on? Well, that's probably where the neuroport was sterilised, and then when you fell in — it was implanted... But I'd probably take antibiotics. Just in case.

— Right. — Vanya got up and headed to the kitchen. There, rummaging through drawers, he unearthed yet another box with English letters, pulled out a foil blister pack, popped it open and swallowed a pill.

— There, I feel better. I do believe you're a clean girl, of course, but given the circumstances... — Vanya smiled warmly and in a friendly way. I couldn't see his face, of course (we'd managed to get the "neurointerface," as Vanya called it, to display only my face in his field of vision — well, presumably my synthesised idea of my own face), but I could feel the movements of his facial muscles as clearly as my own. — Better safe than sorry, as they say!

— Trust in God but don't sit on your hands! What's with this sudden bout of religion?! — I demanded sternly.

— What do you mean? It's just a proverb. But if it matters — I'm a believer. Well, agnostic more like. And you, I take it, are a committed atheist? Damn, I'd bet money you're a Komsomol member too!

— Why bet? What else would I be at twenty-five? Or are you telling me you're non-Party?

— Oh, you're going to be sooo surprised... — said Ivan.

...And I was surprised...

Chapter 1

...Muromets hung from the chains. There was no particular air current in the hall, so he wasn't swinging.

I let my gaze slide over the tubes entering the flesh, over the flasks and retorts... On the indicator screen, the lines ran a steady green. The American equipment beeped, monitoring the vital signs of what was, generally speaking, a non-living object. The neuroport itched horribly — it had been implanted two weeks ago, and although it had taken perfectly and reliably established a link with Muromets' neural systems, the sensation of a foreign body near the cerebellum hadn't yet gone away...

— Vasilisa! Vasya! — Professor Talalantsev had lost me again. Most likely he was about to dump some monkey-work on me. No, I don't mind washing flasks or draining biowaste... But I'm a test pilot, for crying out loud! The first one, if it comes to that!

I stared at Muromets again: the system of artificial flesh — myomers — was enclosed in a frame of titanium structural beams. In the future, it was precisely onto this frame, forming the exoskeleton of Muromets, that... Well, it's obvious what. Unpleasant as it was to acknowledge, but in a world where capitalists still existed, peace was impossible. They were organically incapable of accepting our country's existence. And since that was so, Muromets would have to carry not a plough but a sword. So onto those titanium beams would go titanium armour, being developed by our partners at MISIS. And on top of that, probably, bolt-on weapons from the Urals. Our institute was under the Ministry of Defence for a reason. I wasn't really supposed to think about all this, but I just can't help sticking my nose into everything. Been like that since university.

— VASYA! Why aren't you answering?! — Oh! Granddad found me.

— Sorry, Boris Ivanovich, I was thinking. I wonder — does Muromets feel pain?

— Every time you talk about it I get the feeling you're calling it by its first name. All that's missing is a patronymic. It's BFD-1M "Muromets." A technical device. Pain... Well, its systems register damage to synthetic myofibers and structural frame integrity breaches, with high precision of localisation. But calling that pain... You see, it doesn't actually have brains as such.

— What about the neural node?

— The neural node is a neural node. There's no personality there, and all the neural connections and synapses serve the operating system. The brain for Muromets is the pilot. When you're inside — do you feel pain?

— Well... Not exactly. It's more like an itch.

— There's your answer. Come on, in an hour the "visitors" from the ministry will be here. We need to work through the demonstration programme. You'll be doing the acrobatics...

The demonstration went predictably well. When a petite girl in a skin-tight suit climbs into Muromets' skin, turning into a broad-shouldered microcephalic, and then starts juggling Zhigulis and tying rebar into sailor's knots — the "visitors" traditionally lose their minds with enthusiasm. Just one drawback — for now, the entire synthetic part has to be "grown" from scratch. That is, stamping out Muromets units isn't possible yet. Ours, the first prototype, took three years to make. Since '81. No, there are no cells, at least not in the usual sense, in the myomer tissue. But still...

And you have to feed it, by the way. The cocktail isn't too complicated, made from yeast and alcohol — which is one reason why there are no drinkers on our team.

And you can't do without electricity. That's one of the main problems: our batteries are still too weak. The guys from the physics department say there's some hope for new lithium-ion batteries they're working on... But who knows when there'll be results...

For now... Well, Muromets and I showed off for about an hour and a half — and there you go. 30% charge gone, 20% of the nutrient mix. And that's without the military add-ons. Properly speaking, I should drag myself to the lab and plug into the "feeder." But getting out of Muromets is something I absolutely don't want to do. And it's not even about the pain of connecting and disconnecting from the neuroport. It's just the feeling of this mighty body, as if it were your own... Indescribable. Doubly so considering that in real life I'm quite the shrimp.

Oh well, I have to...

Whoa! Passing the canteen, I couldn't resist popping in for a bite (and showing off a little). And there on the TV — oh my! A tornado! Here! In the Moscow region! People are dying!

I don't know. I've never been able to put the brakes on in time. In school I'd get into bloody fights with the boys — defending those who were weaker. Tiny as I was — but how else? Anyway, running to the "catapult" takes about a minute. We'd already been conducting launch tests — in fact the main cycle was supposed to start Monday. Well, everyone knows Monday begins on Saturday, right?!

Already as the safety cocoon was closing, I heard the professor's shouts. Sorry, Boris Ivanych, but I simply have to...

...In hindsight, I already understood what had happened. But I couldn't just give up: to die like this, in the wreckage of a factory... That's just...

At first I was quite successful: I dragged a bus off the path of the funnel, letting people evacuate, then held a metal structure that nearly rammed the wing of a children's hospital. I did get slammed back-first into a wall — physics is a harsh mistress, and Muromets and I are certainly very strong, but from the tornado's perspective, we're very light. However, we're also sturdy. So we distinguished ourselves.

But then something went wrong: the tornado passed through the town and headed toward the old suburban buildings of a textile factory. I don't know what possessed me to run there — I understood there probably weren't any people. But understanding is one thing, and checking is another...

I mentioned above that we're light... Well, compared to a tank — probably yes. But we still weigh a good 120 kilograms. My 50 and Muromets' 70. Maybe a bit less. In any case, not the best thing for rotted floorboards over a dye vat. The basement, yes. And if I'd just fallen — I'd have climbed out. But the funnel apparently decided to dispose of the overly energetic little human, and on its return pass collapsed the workshop wall onto Muromets and me.

Now I was in darkness, silence, and only the helmet-mounted gas-discharge indicators showed utterly discouraging readings. I had two to three hours of air left. Muromets' food reserves weren't great either. And the battery...

I struggled once more — all I achieved was the pile of bricks (with a Tsarist stamp, no less! Komsomol girl killed by the hands of Tsarism!) packing down even tighter. So it seemed I should start thinking about the eternal. And only one thought wouldn't leave me alone... The neural node...

...It's not that urban exploration was my hobby. I like comfort too much to spend ages on "abandos." But sometimes, for fun, in the company of seasoned explorers — why not take a ride? I've got wheels — Dad and I rebuilt this old Pajero Sport with our own hands. The metal detector I also built myself about five years ago — I love tinkering with a soldering iron. So when they invited me to Ivanovo, to dig through an old factory — I said yes.

Palych explained on site that these workshops had been abandoned even in Soviet times, and in '84 a tornado came through, after which the building was completely forgotten. The locals, in the nineties, had stripped practically everything from above. And we wouldn't have been interested at all, if Palych hadn't found in the archives that the building had a substantial basement. Which, it seemed, nobody had ever entered after the collapse.

The venture was extremely risky but exciting. Especially since, according to those same archives, the basements might hold supplies from Tsarist times.

We worked ourselves half to death, of course. Broken brick (now I understood why the locals hadn't carted it away) was hauled out by wheelbarrow. Progress was painfully slow. By evening we'd reached an old, and apparently copper, dye vat. Enormous tank — sell it for scrap metal and the trip pays for itself.

As exhaustion mounted, the searchers peeled off to a nearby hostel called "Malina," so at some point only Palych and I remained. Knowing about my photography hobby, the senior explorer helped me set up lights, clapped me on the shoulder, and with the words "I'll head to the cars, so as not to get in the way" — left me in splendid isolation.

The first two shots came out... well, ordinary. Not bad, but nothing special. Sharp play of light and shadow, hard edges... Good shots. A solid B+. But then I changed angle and through a gash in the copper side I saw it...

I didn't call Palych. I don't understand why myself, but... The colossal hand — twice the size of my own — turned out to be part of an enormous... Spacesuit? Alien? At first I nearly dislocated my brain. Reality only held together because I found Cyrillic script on the chest plate. Titanium doesn't rust, so I could read the mysterious "BFD."

Obviously, pulling the monster out alone wasn't happening, so I sprinted upstairs for Palych. And couldn't find him. Didn't bother searching further — I had a winch, after all.

I'm probably wrong for this. Probably shouldn't have done it. But I couldn't resist. Somehow I shoved (nearly threw my back out) the thing into the boot (had to fold the rear seats down), then tore off toward Moscow. Sharing a find like THIS — was out of the question.

At home I borrowed a trolley from the Tajik workers, the kind they usually haul rubbish on, then, taking advantage of the early hour when everyone was still asleep, dumped the robot (or suit?) onto the trolley, and cursing, dragged it to the freight lift, then to my flat. Returned the trolley to the Tajiks, and finally got a good look at my catch.

The device (I decided to call it that) was about two-and-a-bit metres tall. The head was dome-shaped, with an opaque visor that stood out against the grey — presumably titanium — armour. The body was heavyset, proportioned like a weightlifter. It looked very strange: like a person with the top layer of skin removed, but without blood and with a bluish-grey colour to the muscles. Over the top, partly penetrating into the body, ran a powerful frame of titanium. "Space frame," I thought, remembering my Pajero's construction.

The arms, hands — were structurally quite human (though enormously large), but the legs were trickier. While anatomically they apparently bent like a human's, the knee was completely out of proportion, and the foot was more birdlike. With four thick support-toes.

On the left side, at the waist, I found a heavily dusty socket for a standard Soviet two-prong extension cord. The kind they used to put on tape recorders and record players. Rummaging around, I found one. Amusingly enough, the power cable from a PlayStation 3 fit.

Honestly, I wasn't fully aware of what I was doing when I plugged the cable into the device. I suppose I was working on the assumption that robots in Soviet science fiction were universally friendly companions of humanity.

Nothing happened, but perhaps it just needed to charge. So I left everything as it was and went to take a shower... The hot jets of water, combined with exhaustion, put me in a half-doze, from which I was woken by a strange squelching sound and a crash.

As I was, stark naked, I leapt from the bathroom and rushed to the living room where I'd left the monster spread out. My wild imagination painted scenes each more terrifying than the last, in the "rise of the machines" category. However, reality exceeded all expectations:

The device... No, apparently a suit after all, was... open. I'd laid it face-down for convenient charging, and now the entire back had opened along its full length, including arms and legs.

And behind the suit, near the wall, lay a dark mass, seemingly ejected from the suit's innards like a catapult.

I cautiously approached it. The room light was off, and I didn't go back to the switch — dawn was already filling the room through the windows.

However, I shouldn't have relied on my eyesight. I'd been told since childhood that I had "night blindness." So I only understood what I was looking at when I practically shoved my nose into IT.

My scream, I'm afraid, woke every neighbour! I recoiled from the mummy — a shapeless heap slumped against the wall — stumbled, tried to keep my balance, and with full force flopped into the belly of the suit. A second later, with a squelch, the suit closed, and I felt a sudden pain near the cerebellum...

...Can you feel pain after death???

...When I came to, the sun was already brightly lighting the whole room. I was lying next to the suit — apparently I'd torn myself free while unconscious. The back of my head stung — I ran my hand over it and wasn't surprised to feel blood. Somehow I got to my feet. Every movement took enormous effort. As if I had to overcome some resistance. Coordination was swimming too. The idea of investigating THIS in solitary glory was looking rather less attractive. Cautiously turning around, I naturally saw the mummy in its former place. My entire body was pierced by some alien spasm, which I nonetheless managed to suppress.

Swaying and barely keeping balance, I stumbled to the bathroom and washed with cold water. Felt a little better. I dabbed my face with a warm towel and stared into the mirror at an unfamiliar, pretty girl of about twenty. We blinked synchronously a couple of times, after which I instinctively covered my chest with my hands. Well, she did it, but for some reason I repeated the movement, feeling enormous relief at NOT feeling lush, firm girl-boobs under my palms.

The situation was getting tense. Just in case, I leaned forward slightly and looked down. Looked back at the mirror with relief and saw the girl blush deeply. As an experiment, I shook my head. The reflection (not mine) repeated my movements. I pulled a face — same result. Then I pulled another — involuntarily this time.

— HOLY SHIT! — I swore aloud.

— AAAAHHH!!!!! WHAT IS THIS?! — I said, also me, but in a slightly different voice. No, it wasn't a squeaky female voice like in personality-split movies, but I definitely don't normally sound like that.

— WHO ARE YOU?! (I'd never have thought I could sound in two registers simultaneously.)

— AND WHO ARE YOU?!

I had to use all my willpower to suppress HER (I was no longer in any doubt) question. At the same time, my vision slightly doubled, and a second later I saw my own reflection, as it should be. Though to my right, a girl was still standing, silently moving her lips.

Intuitively I listened. It took about a second before, at first barely audibly, then louder and louder: "AAAAAAAHHH!"

— Can you hear me?

"Aaahh... What?! Yes!"

— Good. I feel like an idiot talking to a mirror, but... Who are you?!

"Va... Vasilisa" — the girl sniffled, but immediately pulled herself together. — "And, I think, I'm dead."

— Original. And how am I talking to you?

"Neuroport. I think Muromets, at the moment of my death, extracted the neuroport from my skull and implanted it in you on first contact. Didn't know he could do that."

— Neuroport. That certainly explains everything... Do you mind if I have a drink?

"Actually — I do mind." — For a dead woman, the girl was holding up remarkably well — "If my theory is right, alcohol is off limits for the near future. You've got a neuroport integrating with your brain."

— Speaking of which. What even is that?

"A device that lets your brain synchronise with Muromets' neural core. Oh crap!"

The girl's eyes went wide and she clapped both hands over her mouth. Apparently some thought had hit her. Then I felt urges to run to the living room. By this point I was already learning to distinguish my own thoughts and desires from hers, but here I went along with it, slightly "stepping back" from the controls.

Vasilisa, stumbling (judging by the reflection, I was two heads taller and noticeably more massive, broader in the shoulders and narrower in the hips), reached the living room. Somehow she approached the mummy. I think she was scared and in pain — I couldn't see her face without a mirror, but I seemed to dimly sense her emotions.

Vasilisa reached her hand toward her (presumably) remains, but couldn't bring herself to touch them. Instead she turned sharply to the suit and thrust her hand into the back. With a "squelch" the panels parted. We stuck our head into the helmet and stared at the gas-discharge indicators. Apparently the girl didn't like the numbers. She pulled back, grabbed the suit by the arm and with a slight strain — flipped it face-up. Then she stuck her hand behind the right metal plate on the chest and clicked something. The plate snapped open, revealing something like a fuel filler cap.

— Listen, do you have... Kefir?! Vodka, sugar, yeast would also work...

— Got it. — I exerted my will and took back control, sensing a hint of displeasure from the girl, which she clearly suppressed quickly. — I've got vodka, kefir, milk... And we'll find sugar.

"Then let's get to the kitchen. If we don't hurry — I'll die. Again. Literally of starvation." — Vasilisa laughed nervously.

Preparing the nightmarish mixture took ten minutes. I mixed a litre of kefir, all the sugar I could find, warm water to dissolve the sugar, and a bottle of vodka that had been sitting for two years as a disinfectant. Following Vasilisa's instructions, I poured the whole concoction into the opening on the suit's chest and snapped the lid shut, then collapsed onto the sofa in exhaustion.

"Perfect time for a cigarette..." — the girl mused profoundly.

— I don't actually smoke. By the way, I'm Ivan.

"Vasilisa. Christ, this is so awkward... Unusual... I feel myself — as you. Only sort of paralysed. But at the same time, I understand you can hear what I'm trying to say... Probably through the verbalisation mechanisms. Can you hear my thoughts?"

— Nope. If I could hear those too, I'd probably lose my mind. But I do sense emotions. What's actually happening? (I'd relaxed enough to let her speak aloud. I don't know why, but I keenly felt she really needed that right now.)

— Well, it seems that by the time I died, my consciousness had been more or less copied to Muromets' neural node. The node isn't fully capable, so it can't support higher cortical activity. But you can. Or rather, your brain can. Muromets himself, apparently, with my death and on the brink of resource exhaustion, went into hibernation. By bringing him home and plugging him in, you woke the system. It automatically implanted my neuroport in you, linking your brain to "my" neural node...

— Whoa! — I was so surprised I actually interrupted the girl — That's literally a virtual machine with external storage: your data sits on a dumb storage unit, and executes on a server with excess processing power! What kind of bandwidth does your neuroport have?!

— Of what you just said, I understood only "dumb," "processing power" and "external storage." But I think you've grasped the essence. My memory, my personality — is on that "storage," if you mean the neural node. And I come alive using the spare capacity of your brain. I'm sorry, Vanya, but it looks like I'm your parasite...

...Many men handle women's tears poorly. I'm one of them. When I hear a girl crying, I want to break my neck trying to comfort and calm her somehow.

In our case with Vasilisa, the situation was compounded by the fact that the girl was sobbing bitterly inside my head.

Vasya was, first and foremost, a scientist. A junior researcher at a defence institute. And even a test pilot of complex and dangerous equipment. So while she hadn't figured out the situation — she was maximally focused and collected. But once she had...

To be fair, she desperately tried to hold it together and pull herself together. But in her situation, that was extremely hard: her body was dead. Her consciousness existed as a sort of brain parasite on my reckless head. In a way, her body was the suit itself — Muromets.

Essentially, all that remained of her was a bodiless spirit, chained forever to me, forced to see and feel everything I saw and felt. This, incidentally, could lead to a heap of rather unpleasant problems for us both going forward.

At the present moment, to be fair, her life belonged to me. I could go to doctors and have the device removed from my brain. I could "starve" Muromets, though the "cocktail" we'd fed it had, admittedly, done a decent job replenishing nutrients.

At the same time, I didn't want to cause Vasilisa any harm. I simultaneously respected her for her courage, for the way she'd died (recklessly rushing to save people), and I felt terribly sorry for her. Vasya was three years younger than me — when she was buried under the bricks, she'd only just turned 25. She had a whole future ahead of her. A whole life. Damn, if you thought about it, had she lived to my time, she wouldn't even be old yet. Instead — she'd died and been resurrected by me. Under those circumstances — I bore responsibility for her. Damn, and that bloody Don Quixote complex of mine.

So — as far as it depended on me — I had to protect her.

The second task — and in complexity and priority, perhaps the first — was disposing of Vasilisa's remains: how to discreetly commit a mummified young woman's corpse to the earth, especially when the mere thought of touching it made me violently nauseous (not certain those were my feelings, but I hadn't yet learned to filter). The mummy didn't particularly smell for now (as we understood, after death Muromets had absorbed all moisture from her), but rot was now only a matter of time. And generally, having the corpse of a girl who currently lives in your head lying in your flat — was deeply unsettling.

We thought for a long time. The idea, in the end, came from Vasya herself...

Preparation took the rest of the day. During that time I managed to run for alcohol (well, vodka) to the nearest hypermarket (and in the process we discovered that the neuroport maintained a perfectly stable connection with the neural core at least within a five-kilometre range), thoroughly rinse the suit's interior with the alcohol, let it dry (otherwise I'd be getting high on fumes), after which I donned Muromets, took a large bin bag containing Vasya's remains, and via the fire escape made it to the roof. For Muromets' strength, the lock posed no problem. Another twenty minutes or so I spent, under Vasilisa's instruction, learning to run and jump, heavily thumping the chicken-like feet across the roofing felt. I should note that when Vasya first proposed her plan, I was surprised — the suit was designed for a petite, slight girl. However, said girl, laughing at my ignorance (as soon as goals appeared, the tears vanished and determination returned), pressed something inside the suit with my hands, then suggested I lie in the device and rest a bit. To my astonishment, in about three minutes the suit completely adapted to my dimensions, so now it was hard to understand how such a shrimp had ever operated in it.

About twenty minutes later, my "coach" concluded that I was probably ready for the mission. Furrowing her brows (we'd managed to get the neuroport to display her "ghost" in the corner of my field of vision — impractical, but it greatly eased communication), she measured the distance one more time through my eyes, and nodded with concentration.

I took a running start, and... Basically, I cleared the 50 metres separating my building's roof from the next one. On landing I automatically (probably from a mental nudge by Vasya) rolled, killing the speed, then stood to full height. I wanted to scream with exhilaration and my head was spinning, but the task came first.

I carefully placed the girl's remains near the roof access door. People — janitors, electricians — regularly came up to the roof; there was a chance she'd be found and properly laid to rest.

One more run-up — and I was back on my own roof. Not waiting to be spotted by any neighbours, I ran down and returned to my flat...

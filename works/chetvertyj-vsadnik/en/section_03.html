Chapter 3

They say love is the greatest driving force in the universe. Sometimes it truly is. They say movement is life... Perhaps. But sometimes, it carries death...

...Basil looked at himself in the mirror. The mirror returned the image of a young, slightly stooped man. No heartthrob, but quite presentable.

Vasily pulled himself up — there was nothing remotely presentable about the colourless eyes and receding lower jaw. What was attractive was his — or rather, his father's — wallet.

Usually was.

What had happened last night somewhat broke the usual framework.

Basil looked at the rumpled bed, still holding Kate's scent. What irritated him was that because of a moment's weakness, he now didn't know how to look his friend in the eye. One of the few real friends in his life.

Unlike most students, Basil lived not on the campus itself but in a neighbouring cottage, the rent for which cost a tidy sum. His father's stereotypes were at work — the elder Koshelev had spent his student youth in the old dormitory of RUDN University. Willy-nilly, the cockroach-template was projected onto Yale, after which his father, without further discussion, instructed his personal assistant not only to arrange the heir's education at the university but also to rent the best private cottage. The rent included daily servicing — the homeowner, a charming old lady bearing an uncanny resemblance to Mrs Hudson from the Soviet Doyle adaptation, came at midday, cooked food for the day, tidied up, collected laundry and ironing and departed before Basil returned from classes. Today, however, the lad had no desire to attend lectures. The more he replayed the situation in his head, the stupider it looked. So he'd been upset about his grandfather dying. So he'd let his guard down, burst into tears. Kate had tried to comfort her boyfriend's best friend... And then something not entirely comprehensible happened. At some point, Basil lost all sense of what was happening around him. After that, memory survived only in extremely fragmentary form. Here they are walking to his place, stopping at every tree to caress each other in bursts of some animal passion. Then another dash through the park... Here they are at his place, here in bed...

"Chris is going to smash my face in," Basil realised, "Definitely going to smash it. And he'll be right, in fairness... Well, I need to pack for the funeral. If I don't run into him in the next twenty-four hours — maybe he'll have cooled down by our next meeting..."

...Kate stood under the shower jets in her cubicle. The girl's thoughts were likewise in some disarray. Of course, she understood that something in her had changed, something had become different, but up to a certain point these changes hadn't gone beyond the bounds of common sense. The trip to Japan had changed her profoundly — she'd returned with a reconsidered view of her life, having grasped, through the death of a great scientist, that all people are mortal. Regardless of age or social standing. And that meant not a minute of life could be wasted. Being a bookworm meant simply burying yourself — your youth, your feelings and emotions. After Japan, that seemed an unaffordable luxury. Especially since, on trial, life turned out to be remarkably colourful, cheerful and pleasant. It was wonderfully pleasant to socialise with people she'd previously ignored. The world blazed with new colours. And even sex, which previously, because of cousin Tom's stunt five years ago, had seemed something vile, shameful, painful and terrible — turned out to be an incredible pleasure.

It was incredibly wonderful — being alive. But what happened yesterday... She sincerely loved Chris — this slightly clueless but strong, brave and noble country boy, a country girl herself. She never wanted to cause him pain. On the contrary — she would have given her life just to protect him from extra pain...

But when at the party she saw Basil sitting in a corner with his phone clutched in both hands, sobbing his heart out, completely like a child — something stirred in her soul. One word led to another — and she felt herself drawn to this strange Russian guy. And an hour later, they were loving each other like madmen, not even remembering the rest of the world.

It was like a spell.

Kate remembered the touch of Basil's hands. Shuddered as if a charge had run through her body. The charge was followed by sweet languor. The answer was somewhere close. The key, perhaps, was that Kate felt no guilt whatsoever. Kate pressed the shower to her chest and focused on the strange, wonderfully pleasant sensation deep in her chest. It was akin to the meditation that Mr Sayn, the psychology professor, talked so much about. And the answer came.

She truly loved Chris. But she loved Basil too. Her heart turned out to be completely boundless and limitless. And its warmth should be shared with everyone who needed it. This was above any possessiveness or selfishness. It was the highest form of altruism.

Kate understood she had finally found herself. And quietly, melodiously, happily — she laughed.

By the time the holidays came and all exams were passed, Basil had been in Novosibirsk for nearly a month already. After the funeral, he hadn't returned straight away, having arranged to sit all his exams in the autumn session. The university accommodated the oligarch's son. Kate and Chris had broken up. Though it wasn't really a final breakup. Rather, it should be called a transition to "open relations." Before leaving for the holidays, Kate managed to sleep with a couple of dozen guys. The girl had lost all inhibition. Watching this, Chris likewise decided to follow her example. Naturally, the best football player didn't get turned down by a single girl in the year. And yet, at least once a week, Chris and Kate still found time for each other.

There were 223 days left until the generalised manifestation of the pandemic.

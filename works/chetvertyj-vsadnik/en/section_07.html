Chapter 7

Instinct comes in many forms. Some people play the stock market flawlessly; some manage to anticipate the development of events in poker. But certain people are capable of sensing trouble and its location — and placing themselves as far from it as possible.

Sergei left Moscow at the very end of November, partly helped by a friend at the Ministry of Health who leaked him a small insider tip. Nothing especially secret or alarming (deaths in India and China from an unknown virus, mass die-off of Yale students), but Sergei's danger sense began to tingle. So he grabbed his mother, Vika — his faithful better half — packed them into the car, commanded Tuman to take his usual spot in the bed (the Weimaraner loved road trips; no persuasion needed), and set course for the village of Kuvyrdaikovo on the shore of the Rybinsk Reservoir.

That's where his wife's roots were; there, too, her grandmother had left the Sobolevs a small but cosy house. For the past three years, they'd used it exclusively in summer as an extremely remote dacha, but formally it was quite habitable year-round.

En route, having already put a good hundred kilometres between himself and Moscow, Sergei pulled into a roadside supermarket and bought out their entire stock of coal, firewood, buckwheat, tinned meat, tea, sugar and other necessities — substantially depleting his bank account and loading his L-200 until the suspension sagged.

The shop owner watched the proceedings with surprise. The surprise gradually grew into concern as the purchase total went from five digits to six.

— Mate, what, they've announced nuclear war?

— Nah, just sick of the city. Want to live in the country with the family for a bit.

— One of those "downshitters," are you?

— Downshifters. No, I've just gone mental. Don't sweat it, uncle. How much do I owe?

...The Sobolevs celebrated New Year in a practically empty village. Thank God there was at least electricity. Though in the barn, a brand-new diesel generator and several barrels of fuel already stood at the ready.

It couldn't be said that Vika was delighted by this emergency evacuation, but having lived with Sergei for nearly ten years, she had learned to trust her husband's instinct unconditionally. And if her husband said "we're leaving now" — it meant no arguing, just pack. He'd turn out to be right again, as always.

Fortunately, Sergei himself was no great lover of "extreme recreation" and possessed hands that grew from the right place. So he'd begun improving the grandmother's cottage while she was still alive. The current assets included a not-half-bad renovation (including plastic windows, camouflaged from outside with decrepit wooden overlays against those who liked to rummage through houses abandoned for winter), decent appliances — as the household tech was upgraded in the city, Sergei shipped everything that was freed up to the village, so the little cottage could boast a Samsung fridge, an old but perfectly working Sony television, and even a washing machine. Cold water was piped in from a well; a water-heating boiler hung on the wall. All this high-tech coexisted with a fully functional Russian stove. For dessert, literally that summer, Sergei had finally installed a proper bathroom. Now, to answer nature's call, there was no need to hop like a bunny through the snowdrifts.

In the cellar lay an inexhaustible supply of pickles and preserves, made first by the grandmother and then by Vika together with Sergei's mother.

By February, Vika had even grown accustomed to their new life: there was no livestock, apart from the dog spoiled by the entire family (which was, of course, a right beast, but in an entirely different sense), so there was no need to rise with the roosters. The fridge and cellar were stuffed to the brim — the husband had made several more runs to surrounding shops, gathering every product within reach. On her husband's insistence, Vika had quit her job (and now, listening to the horrifying news reports on TV, marvelled yet again at her man's instinct). The entirety of the chores amounted to feeding the family and keeping the house tidy. All in four female hands. Both males (Sergei and Tuman) spent their days in the forest, apparently having set themselves the goal of exterminating the entire rabbit population of the region. At any rate, there had not yet been a day when, having gone hunting, they failed to replenish the family's meat reserves. In the evenings, Vika and Sergei battled on the games console or surfed the web (Sergei had set up Wi-Fi, providing connectivity through three operators, loading a round sum onto each account. Money, for now, was holding — back in January, seeing the trends, Sergei had dumped his one-bedroom flat near Petrovsko-Razumovskaya at a fire-sale price, delegating all paperwork to his lawyer).

Among their acquaintances, the Sobolevs had a reputation as slight hermits — the family didn't much care for socialising, rarely had guests and rarely visited others.

Now this was rather an advantage. Being self-sufficient, the couple didn't experience particular discomfort from self-isolation.

In the world, meanwhile, a kind of hell was unfolding. From late February, Sergei stopped chasing rabbits and set about training his wife in the use of all the weapons the family possessed.

As a gesture of gratitude for handling the flat sale, along with a round sum, he had back in January advised his lawyer and old mate Pavel to get the hell out of the city. Pavel had heeded the advice and, moreover, bought a house in the same village. So now he lived with his family next door. Though for the first month, the neighbours didn't even interact, and Sergei had honestly warned Pavel that for the immediate future he would, without discussion, shoot him if he came within a hundred metres of Sergei's house. Pavel took no offence. He'd already done his research and understood the situation. He'd also managed, literally days before the tipping point while the world still operated by its old rules, to obtain through old channels a dozen worn but still perfectly serviceable bacteriological hazmats, which he shared with his friend's family.

By the end of February, three more families had relocated here from Moscow. After deliberation, Pavel and Sergei singled out a family both knew slightly but who came with the very highest recommendations — the Kharitonovs, who had all dedicated their lives to medicine: the father was a brilliant surgeon, the mother a therapist. The daughter was studying dentistry, and the son was training as a virologist. The latter, admittedly, was only in his second year. The Kharitonovs were not very wealthy, so the two founding fathers of the colony bought them a house at their own expense. The doctors didn't take much persuading — being well-informed people who perfectly understood how bad the prospects for treating amplectavirus syndrome were, they preferred not to play at nobility but to save their lives and their children's.

The medics also had to pass a month-long quarantine. Fortunately, none of them turned out to be infected.

But the Stern family, invited to join the commune, had to be turned away at the entrance to the village. By this point, the locals, having grasped the situation and assessed who was the main driving force, had voluntarily "sworn allegiance" to Sergei, so with the efforts of two young lads and a dozen local grandfathers, the village was fairly promptly enclosed with a makeshift fence and a checkpoint was set up.

The Sterns stopped before the closed barrier and stared in bewilderment at Sergei, Pavel and Valentin, who had their weapons trained on the new arrivals.

— Guys, what kind of joke is this? You invited us yourselves!

— This isn't a joke. Boris, Olesya — you're infected.

— How?! Have you seen our tests? — Boris flared up, clenched his fists. Sergei raised his Saiga and flicked the safety off with his index finger.

— Darling, don't be angry... There's been some mistake... Calm down, love, please... — Olesya literally hung on her husband, diverting his attention to herself. Kissed his lips, his cheeks... Boris's shoulders dropped, he exhaled. Sergei lowered the shotgun again.

— Boris, you and Olesya have always, for as long as I've known you, been at each other's throats. None of your friends even understood how you hadn't divorced yet. Forgive the bluntness. Today, before you even got out of the car, you couldn't peel yourselves apart for a full minute. And just now... I'm sorry, mate, but you're infected.

— You think? — Boris was taken aback. Listening to himself and his feelings, he reluctantly, but recognisably acknowledged Sergei was right.

— I'm sure... Sorry, mate, but it's best if you leave.

— And... And what happens now? — Olesya, it seemed, had also understood it was serious.

— I don't know — Valentin Kharitonov answered for Sergei. The question was medical, so it fell under his jurisdiction — judging by everything, you're in the early stage of the illness. That means you've got three to six months in reserve. Maybe by then they'll invent a vaccine. In any case, you'll live those months with pleasure — that much I can guarantee. It's a feature of the disease.

— We're going to die, aren't we? — Olesya pressed against her husband's shoulder — Strange, but I'm not even afraid of it. Let it be six... Even three — but I've never been as happy as I am right now! And you, Boris?

Boris listened to his feelings and, with surprise, recognised that his wife was right.

— Me too. Thank you for everything, guys. Thank you for trying to save us. No hard feelings, honestly. All fair. Only, you know — I've just now realised that I became happy... Well, probably this past week... All these years together, we genuinely hated each other. We probably only stayed together because splitting up would have pleased the other one. And now — we're happy. You know what, guys — we may only have six months left, but those six months we'll be far happier than you.

— Happy for you — Sergei tried to smile. The smile came out crooked — We're staying in touch, in any case. While the networks hold, we'll keep in contact.

The Sterns' jeep turned around and drove away down the snowy road. Sergei, without speaking to anyone, went home. Only there, in the warmth, was he able to shed the frozen half-smile.

— Sweetheart, what's wrong? You've gone white as a sheet! — Vika was alarmed at the sight of her husband's hollowed-out gaze.

Sergei didn't answer immediately. He thought for a while, replaying the entire exchange in his mind...

— You know, sunshine... I think I just saw the living dead...

Already finishing the sentence, he realised he couldn't answer himself one single question: saying this, had he meant the Sterns... Or his own comrades in the commune... And then he understood he didn't want to know.

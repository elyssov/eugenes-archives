Chapter 1

...For an avalanche to begin, most often all it takes is a small push. For an army to flee and a city to fall — a single nail, forgotten in a horseshoe.

To destroy humanity — all it takes is the desire to make it happy.

Cities emptied. Fear drove some away. New desires — others. The latter outnumbered the former.

Emotions were placed under ban. A mother who kissed her child once too often could be killed on the spot by that child's father. Lovers who held hands risked dying just like that — holding each other's hands.

"...And Hell followed with him" — John saw to the root of things. It was Hell. And this Hell was inside everyone. Life itself demanded you become a monster — simply in order to survive. And yet it all began with a great discovery...

317 days before the fall of civilisation.

...Hibiki Masashika gazed with anguish at the door leading to the basement of his and his son's house. From below came again the laughter of animated girls. Before the door stood an empty food tray. At least Kato had eaten. There were days when he wouldn't even open the perpetually locked door for food.

Hibiki clenched his fists. The illness was taking his child from him. The only memory of Sanako. And he, a professor, a physician — could do nothing. Mental illnesses were not his field.

The old man didn't bother to knock. He knew the door wouldn't open. He also knew his son was fading, and there was nothing he could do about it. An attempt to force entry would simply kill the boy. He'd already tried. Sanako's death had taken its toll on the whole family...

The father gazed with anguish at the clay sake bottle standing on the table. Strictly speaking, it wasn't in Japanese tradition to get drunk at home, in splendid isolation. But nothing better came to mind, and Hibiki couldn't bring himself to take antidepressants.

Somewhere around the fourth cup, when thought had nearly abandoned the tormented consciousness of the old professor — She appeared. Later, Hibiki would recall that it was the incarnation of Amaterasu herself, descending to a father on the verge of surrender. She ran her delicate hand through his hair, leaned to his ear... He caught the subtle scent of spiced herbs from her hair as a strand of it slid like silk across his face.

And then she whispered something in his ear. The old scientist tried to concentrate, but the meaning kept slipping away... Dissolving... And then came merciful oblivion...

...It took only ten days to work a miracle. The following morning, Hibiki awoke without the hangover that had become habitual, and with a complete understanding of what he had to do. He would save his son. He would save all the children of Yamato.

That morning, he arrived at the institute before everyone else. He wrote to the director that he was exercising his right to an independent project and asked to be assigned a competent assistant and no questions until the research was complete. The old man knew he wouldn't be refused. The authority of the planet's greatest genetic engineer was too great.

The general outline of the project was ready by lunchtime. All that remained was to select the right codons to describe the changes needed in the organism. As a vector, Hibiki chose the AAV virus, owing to its high contagiousness and low pathogenicity. Moreover, the AAV2 strain was specific to neurons, and the core of Hibiki's planned intervention was aimed precisely at brain cells.

The virologist's idea was fairly simple: increase dopamine secretion to reduce stress and boost social adaptability. To the same end, anandamide secretion was boosted, which was also meant to counterbalance the increased testosterone secretion. The latter was necessary to rouse the patient, increase his activity — including sexual. Aggression was offset by the high levels of dopamine and anandamide. The crucial factor was a substantially increased synthesis of oxytocin. Overall, the brilliant virologist used every last bit of the modest AAV payload capacity. With fewer than 5,000 nucleotide base pairs available for encoding genetic information, he used them all — at times quite ingeniously, encoding protein production in stages and making certain operons perform multiple intended functions at once. The resulting genetic material was a masterpiece.

By the end of the ninth day, the vector was ready. It was time to replicate a sufficient quantity of viral units and begin testing. The preparation was to infect Kato via airborne transmission. To this end, Hibiki planned to inject the viral material through the ventilation directly into his son's "kingdom," which the boy had set up in the basement.

The old man entrusted the replication to his assistant, Kate Greyhawk — an exchange student from Yale. The girl, gaijin though she was, upon learning who she'd be working with, fell into euphoria and delighted the old man with her incredible enthusiasm. She spent hours fussing with preparations, searching for information the professor requested, processing data. In short, she proved herself admirably. Her single deficiency — not knowing the language — was compensated by the fact that the professor himself spoke fluent English.

Hibiki went home. Even Japanese people, especially old ones, sometimes need sleep. Besides, Kato needed feeding, and food left by a stranger the hikikomori rejected on principle. Sometimes Hibiki wondered how his son managed to discern this nuance. He concluded that Kato's heightened sense of smell allowed him to detect the olfactory signature of the cook. The theory was so-so, but nothing better came to mind.

Kate gladly stayed in the laboratory as long as possible. The old genius had not revealed the ultimate goal of their work — he said it would be a surprise and a gift to all of humanity. In principle, through participating in the work, Kate had already understood it was something related to the correction of social behaviour. The smart girl put two and two together and concluded it concerned correcting the famous Japanese shyness and inability to engage in sexual behaviour. Based on this, during development she slipped the genetic engineer a couple of her own ideas and was very pleased when he accepted them. In particular, when he asked her to find a way to force the secretion of neurotransmitters enhancing sexual desire, Kate, after several hours of struggle, produced an elegant method that also tied into a substantial increase in pheromone secretion. Understanding that in the course of evolution, the human sensory organs had virtually ceased to perceive pheromonal effects, Kate achieved a pheromone synthesis increase of several orders of magnitude. As already noted, the professor accepted the idea — though, to the girl's vexation, never praised her brilliant nested synthesis solution. But then, who was he and who was she. Kate understood the difference in levels perfectly well. And so she didn't grumble, but continued working honestly.

She went home so late that a few days earlier she'd been chilled through by the night winds on the outskirts of Sapporo, which in late April could still cut right to the bone. As a result, Kate was sniffling and sneezing, but refused to give up and stubbornly continued working.

This time, having completed all procedures and put away the biomaterial, she headed to the vivarium — to the rhesus macaques serving as live in vivo replicators for the modified AAV. It was time to feed Caesar (someone in the vivarium really loved the film "Planet of the Apes") and take blood samples. The virus had been introduced the day before and could not yet have replicated in the necessary quantities. Nevertheless, monitoring was essential. It was also important to control the number of adenoviral units in the monkey's blood.

Usually calm, Caesar was behaving strangely this time. He was nervous, wouldn't let himself be handled, and during the blood draw even managed to bite the girl. Kate didn't pull her hand back in time, receiving a deep scratch on the back of her hand. Caesar, being a good-natured and tame monkey, seeing what he'd done, calmed down and even rushed to the girl to nuzzle her, apologising for what he'd done. Cursing, Kate put the sample in the centrifuge, then treated her scratch and, switching off the light, left the vivarium.

Caesar watched her go with a wistful gaze. He didn't like it when people left. However, the monkey was distracted from his sad thoughts by an irritating itch under his knee. A pimple had been growing there for the second day, itching horribly. Once again, the macaque contorted himself like a professional gymnast and sank his teeth into the irritating spot with relish. The patch of skin, already scratched raw from the previous time, hadn't yet healed, but at least Caesar felt considerably better.

Chapter 2

...A journey of a thousand li begins, as is well known, with a single step. An avalanche — with a single pebble. A system is always more complex than the sum of its parts' complexities.

This is synergy in action.

When a genetic vector, complicated by an adenovirus no less, enters an organism already infected with the influenza virus — much can happen. Moreover, the vector had been deliberately modified to be zooanthroponotic. And Kate had her own adenovirus — her throat had been scratchy for two days already. The mechanisms of viral hybridisation remain, to this day, a process not fully understood by humans. Generally speaking, hybridisation comes down to the exchange of one or two operons, possibly even with fatal consequences for the virus itself (though here, natural selection intervenes, destroying such a strain). By and large, nothing terrible should have happened. Except — synergy...

It is difficult to describe everything that happened that night. What matters is that a new virus was born. Something that bizarrely combined the properties of both the adeno-associated virus and a seasonal flu strain of Eastern origin. This became an additional negative factor: the Western girl's organism was simply not adapted to this kind of surprise. Her immune system had not yet cracked the new infection. And after the genetic material integrated — it was simply too late. Whether this was an absurd quirk of nature or a joke by the goddess Amaterasu, offended by mortals, the new virus turned out to be, among other things, simply invulnerable to the immune system.

However, the newcomer itself was also out of sorts, primarily due to the resulting record-long DNA chain for a virus. The hybrid replicated in tissues extremely slowly.

But persistently.

...Hibiki stood frozen before his son's door. The food tray remained untouched. More than a day had passed since his son had touched food. The scientist didn't deliberate long — he shouldered open the flimsy door... And found Kato hanged. A short suicide note, written in a jagged, nervous hand, stated that he could not live in a world where he would never be with Hatsune Miku. Hibiki finished reading the note more by inertia — by this point, the great scientist was already, effectively, dead. The old man's heart couldn't take the stress.

Falling, he caught with his arm the Sony console standing before his son's crumpled lair. The console was followed by the television, on which a virtual hologram girl was singing something silently and endlessly.

Japanese electronics is usually renowned for its reliability, but synergy intervened again. Here, however, it might be called a "deus ex machina" from a fate that had evidently decided to finish off humanity. The television toppled a jug of water. The short circuit lasted fractions of a second — then the fuses tripped... But those fractions of a second were enough for a spark to fall on the thinnest sheet of rice paper, on which the face of the virtual girl had been rendered in skilful brushstrokes. The sheet caught fire, from it the notebook, the carpet, the bedding...

When the firefighters arrived, they could only confirm the death of the Masashika family... Where once stood a beautiful and ancient house, the wind stirred the fragile ash. The smell of burnt meat and melted plastic was unbearable. The brand-new ultrabook containing all the old scientist's work had burned to cinders, leaving behind only an unpleasant odour.

The University of Sapporo took the great scientist's death hard. Kate cried for two more days, understanding what a great mind had been lost and what an opportunity she had missed. Unable to remain within the university's walls after this tragedy, the girl prepared to go home. The professor's invaluable notes she handed over to Professor Mamoru. He, however, showed little enthusiasm (which, in fact, became the final straw in her decision to return to the USA), coolly thanking the student and ordering the destruction of the monkeys. It was hard to believe that the good-natured Hibiki could have had enemies, yet the closest to that dubious honour was Mamoru himself.

Kate's mild indisposition cleared up a couple of days before her flight, so at John F. Kennedy International Airport she disembarked in excellent spirits and full of energy. The native air seemed to blow all the dark thoughts and memories clean out of her head. The world was beautiful and wondrous — and it awaited her and only her.

There were still almost six weeks until the start of summer break, so the girl threw herself headlong into her studies.

...Christopher Parker caught the ball, feinted past Jim, shouldered aside Kerry West and slammed the ball into the ground.

— Touchdown! Game! — yelled the referee.

Chris threw his arms up in victory... And nearly fell when Kate leapt onto his neck with an ecstatic squeal, passionately fastening her lips to the winner's. Lately, Kate, returned from her trip to Japan, had changed a great deal. Over the past month, from the biology department's premier "blue stocking" and "bookworm" at Yale, she had been gradually transforming into an ever more remarkable girl. She'd started attending parties, made loads of friends, finally found a boyfriend — Chris himself, in fact. It should be said that Kate had changed physically too. Or rather, she had radically reconsidered her image, whereupon it suddenly turned out that beneath the prim blouses and pencil skirts lurked a rare beauty, absolutely irresistible in jeans, cowboy boots and a checked shirt knotted at the waist. As Kate herself joked — "I just went back to my roots — jeans can't not suit a girl from Kentucky."

All in all, Chris was extremely pleased with his acquisition. The only thing — lately he'd been somewhat bothered by Kate's slightly excessive familiarity with other guys. Chris, like Kate, came from the heartland, and in his understanding of the world, a girl was supposed to maintain a certain distance with boys who weren't her boyfriend. Kate, however, cheerfully draped herself around the necks of all her friends, kissed everyone she deemed deserving (which wasn't hard to be), and generally didn't overthink it. On the other hand, Chris recognised that despite her origins, Kate was smarter and, essentially, more modern than he was. He, a simple country lad who'd gotten into Yale on his athletic merits and physique, didn't understand all this political correctness and tolerance stuff, but trusted that Kate knew what she was doing. Besides, not long ago they'd had a conversation about it, during which Kate tenderly called him "her silly," explained that it was all just friendliness, after which they had their first time together — and it turned out that "still waters run deep," as Basil later commented — Chris's best friend, who'd come to Yale from distant and snowy (though Basil, whenever its "snowiness" was mentioned, would for some reason lose his temper, spit, hiss and threaten to summon his pet bear with a vodka-powered nuclear reactor — who would show everyone "Kuzma's mother") Russia. Basil's father, one of Russia's oil kings, had paid an indecent sum for his offspring's education, which didn't much affect his grades — but Basil had become the university's chief ringleader and acquired extremely useful connections. Chris valued the friendship with this slightly affected, snobbish but fun guy all the more — unlike Basil's other friends, he had nothing to offer in return, which meant the friendship was definitely genuine.

In short, in bed, "bookworm Kate" turned out to be quite something, wearing poor Chris out to a pleasant exhaustion. Today the couple planned to continue their experiments in this area, so the plan was to dispense with victory celebrations as quickly as possible.

While Chris was working his brain — Kate had already slid off her boyfriend and was now generously bestowing kisses on the entire university football team. The last game of the season had kept the Ivy League cup at Yale, so the reward was more than deserved. The lads happily offered their necks and cheeks to the nimble, petite and merry beauty.

Basil, meanwhile, was quietly distributing cash to the winners of the totalisator the shrewd Russian had organised. Chris recalled with a satisfied grin that he'd also bet on his own team — Basil hadn't objected, and had in fact given friendly advice, explaining that the odds weren't actually in their favour, but he had a wonderful insider tip about the health of the Columbia squad's irreplaceable striker, "Unstoppable" Frank Stefman. It later emerged that Frank had indeed had a run-in with some odd types near his home. The result was bruises (not too serious in itself) and a nasty leg sprain (which was extremely unpleasant for Columbia). Rumour had it that the men who'd beaten Frank swore in some guttural, harsh, possibly Middle Eastern language.

Kate met him by the campus — at THEIR spot. The girl already had a picnic basket and a pot-bellied, dusty bottle of some spirits. As Kate explained, it was a gift to them from Basil. The label read: Clos Du Mesnil 1995.

A quiet, warm May evening was descending on the campus.

There were still 275 days until the catastrophe.

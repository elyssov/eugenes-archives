Chapter 5

It is commonly believed that the development of transport is a boon for humanity. The Earth has shrunk to the size of, if not a large city, then a not-very-big country. Just a couple of hundred years ago, crossing France from end to end took several days. Now the entire world could be circumnavigated in less than twenty-four hours. If we trust Jules Verne's estimates, the world has shrunk by no less than eighty times. Those who achieved this were thanked by merchants and entrepreneurs, tourists and explorers... But most of all — by those tiny ticketless passengers we carry within us. Everything has its price. And if the world has shrunk to tiny dimensions, then its vulnerability to pandemics has grown by exactly the same factor.

It is impossible to check everyone. Impossible to filter out a carrier during the incubation period. Impossible to seal every crack. As long as international exchange exists — so does plague. It cannot not exist.

Such is the price of our mobility.

The hybrid virus had mastered the most effective methods of transmission: airborne, sexual and contact. Effectively, being near a carrier in the prodromal or later period made it virtually impossible not to be infected.

During the incubation period, the virus manifested no symptoms whatsoever. This period lasted from three weeks to six weeks, depending on individual characteristics. Then came the prodromal period: the secretion of some neurotransmitters was enhanced, that of others suppressed. At this stage, the pathogenic component of the new strain was inactive. The carrier felt more than wonderful — mood improved dramatically, sociability soared. The world shone with new colours, all problems retreated, life acquired volume and flavour. People began to be perceived not as walking irritants but as brothers and sisters, as friends. With each passing day, the soul was seized by ever greater rapture. One wanted to socialise, to shake up friends, to let them see and feel what you yourself felt. The sad you wanted to console, the gloomy to cheer. With each day, these sensations intensified. The carrier was already a source of infection. Only, for the time being, the infection was not pathogenic. The new perception of the world drove the infected to travel. It was one thing to simply sit in one place when the world around you was ordinary. You expected nothing new from it. And quite another when the perception of the surrounding world through a child's eyes returned to you. When every new impression brought incomparable pleasure, when consciousness began to distinguish millions of shades of a single taste, millions of tones of a single note, millions of nuances.

Gradually, amid the general good-natured euphoria, a note of sexual activity began to emerge. Earlier in some, later in others, but alongside the desire to embrace everyone came the desire to love everyone. Around the same time, in men, irritability often increased — testosterone playing its role. Though quick tempers were equally matched by quick forgiveness, thanks to the rest of the hormonal profile.

After some time, from a month to roughly half a year, the pathogenic component of the disease generalised. It appeared to be ordinary flu. Simply one invulnerable to the immune system. And it killed the carrier within a week at most. However, the carrier died happy and feeling neither pain nor fear. In this sense, death was maximally merciful.

However, not everyone died...

How many people die on the planet per day? How many of them are young people, not yet even thirty? In both cases, the number is fairly large. Sometimes we simply lack the scale and clarity of vision to see the real connections. Remove all the information noise, all the rubbish — and we immediately see the interconnectedness of events. Alas, more often than not, the leaves are hidden in the forest. Frequently — an autumn forest.

A report of a student's death abroad is, of course, an emergency. But primarily an emergency for the relatives. Flu, complications. Our condolences.

Someone simply vanished, not making contact — also an emergency. But for a different department.

Neither FEMA nor the CDC paid these reports particular attention. The channels were clogged with the latest epidemics in Africa, an outbreak of some nasty thing in Afghanistan. Besides, the seasonal flu was beginning in the Northern Hemisphere.

Naturally, nobody connected any of this with the remarkable friendliness that had swept the entire city of Somerset, Kentucky. After all, they hadn't started an armed uprising, had they? And the fact that the town had seen a sharp drop in criminal incidents — well, good for them!

In Novosibirsk, they were seeing off a new youth idol. Vasily Koshelev, son of Mikhail Koshelev the oil magnate, once a gloomy and not particularly sociable young man, had won the hearts of the entire city's young people. Today, Vasily was heading back to the States.

Over the time that had passed, the lad had changed considerably. Straightened his shoulders, stopped slouching, lost the habit of speaking under his breath. His character had changed too — he'd become much kinder, lost the desire for cruel jokes. America had really changed the sole heir of the Koshelev family. His father was now fully convinced the money spent on his offspring's education had not been wasted.

Vasily was flying to the USA via Moscow, where he planned to spend another week with friends at his father's villa in Podushkino — to show the American guys the ancient capital, the nightclubs and museums.

Overall, the Koshelev patriarch rather liked his son's whole group of friends. Good-natured, sweet, sociable kids, lacking the snobbery perfectly expected from people of such exalted origins. Over the two months spent at the Koshelevs', the group managed to befriend loads of Russian peers, picked up Russian remarkably well for beginners, and even found themselves girlfriends. However, here, according to Koshneftegaz's security service, a certain mess prevailed: positive in all other respects, in matters of love the Americans and Vasily himself displayed excessive indiscriminateness. This upset Mikhail, though on the other hand, it was gratifying that his frankly not-particularly-handsome-from-birth son had managed to change so much that girls were throwing themselves at him. Given that these relationships were mostly one-or-two-time affairs with no intended consequences — meaning money clearly wasn't the dominant factor — there was nothing to do but be happy about the son's success in socialisation.

All the father's worry ultimately boiled down to one serious talk about the benefits of condoms, which ended with Koshelev Jr producing, from the pocket of his jeans, an entire garland of the relevant products.

...Professor Mamoru read through the document handed to him by Professor Hibiki's assistant for the umpteenth time. He couldn't stand this fragmentary style of notes, going back to their joint project. Hibiki kept too much in his head and too little on paper. No, the old slug wasn't afraid of having his ideas stolen — he was far too naive and good-natured for that. He simply thought that way — solving the most complex genetic puzzles in his head, unspooling polypeptide chains, calculating codons... And writing down only intermediate results, without mentioning what for or why.

Nevertheless, the direction was clearly promising. Something to do with gene therapy for psychological disorders. Mamoru gleefully recalled that Hibiki's son had been one of those touched-in-the-head hikki types.

In any case, there was no choice. If you want dividends from someone else's discovery, you have to swallow your pride. Besides, the girl had left the university and gone home. Which meant nobody would know about the call. Mamoru resolutely launched Skype and entered the number found in the documents. A dozen rings later, the other end picked up.

Mamoru asked his question, listened to the answer, and silently disconnected. He drummed his fingers briefly on the desk, then with a swift motion selected the files, hit shift-delete and confirmed with Enter.

The trail was cold, which meant the files had no value.

There were 150 days left.

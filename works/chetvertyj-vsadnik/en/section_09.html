Chapter 9

...When trouble comes, people tend to retreat into their own little world. Not to see others' problems. To believe that what is happening concerns only them. It's curious, but this is clearly an acquisition of modern times — for pack animals, the individualisation of stress is not characteristic.

Believing all the world's woes are concentrated upon himself, a person often fails to notice how much luckier he is than others. The folk wisdom long ago captured this phenomenon in a short proverb: "The grass is always greener on the other side!" Though, perhaps, this proverb has another interpretation.

...While the bloody events described above were taking place in Russia, the rest of the world was faring no better. In China, the destruction of slums along with their inhabitants had become systematic. Nobody counted victims. Residents, despite the information blockade, somehow learned of what awaited them and by every means dispersed throughout the quarantine zone. Unlike the guest workers in Russia, the Chinese took into account that epidemics spread better in crowds. Therefore, the doomed did not clump into large packs, preferring instead to scatter individually.

Due to the geographical peculiarities of major Chinese cities, a proper blockade was often extremely difficult. An additional problem was logistics. Nearly two billion people had to be fed somehow.

Fairly soon, famine began in several regions of the country. In the most remote villages in the south, reports of cannibalism appeared.

In the USA, the situation was even more cheerful. Since the virus had begun its triumphal march from there, and the nearest major city to Yale was New York — the consequences didn't take long to arrive. As a result, the Big Apple became, probably, one of the first major quarantine cities. Deaths began there earlier too, than everywhere else. By March, it was clear the situation had spiralled out of control. By this point, the city had been centrally supplied with food by sea for two months. Food was delivered on barges.

One fine day, a number of city residents received text messages inviting them to proceed to the cargo section of the port. The messages were sent by FEMA. They were to bring only essential belongings, documents, and closest family. The recipients were people of no ordinary stature: scientists, senior officials, representatives of big capital. Barges were already waiting for them at the port. This time, however, without food. The cargo vessels had been converted into quarantine ships. With minimal amenities, but — with medical laboratories and isolation wards. Into these, all who arrived at the appointed time were packed.

From that moment, no food barges sailed to the city. The next day, the power was cut. Mass looting began. The National Guard holding the cordon did not intervene. It was conditionally assumed that everyone in the city was infected. To be fair, the assessment was close to reality. Effectively, the city became a nest of anarchy. Within the city limits, everything was permitted. Problems began when you tried to breach the cordon: the military opened fire without warning and without much deliberation.

However, even here there was no absolute barrier. People still managed to break through the cordon. And they dissolved into the still-uninfected cities. As in Russia, local law enforcement tried to round up the refugees. Efficiency, however, was low.

To put it simply — the whole world was slowly but surely rolling into chaos. The only thing standing in the way of this chaos was the military. A structure united by discipline, purpose and unified command — it held out longer than all the rest...

Alas, armies always consist of people. Those very people who were such a delectable morsel for the infection.

Yes, strictness, discipline, regular medical checks — allowed the world's militarised structures to hold for some noticeable time after the fall of civilian civilisation. But the virus's structure was such that, living in a collective and having any contact with carriers whatsoever, not getting infected sooner or later was simply impossible.

The expected happened.

The soldiers of the African republics were the first to start dying. The South African army held out longest, but even it could not escape the cup.

Then Asia collapsed.

Europe descended into chaos by mid-spring — weakened by the population's complacency and an excess of migrants whose culture differed sharply from that of law-abiding Europeans.

The forces of Russia and the USA held out longest of all. Both fought the epidemic heroically. Both lifted the city blockades only when it became clear there was no longer any point.

At first, infected soldiers, together with their immediate circle (company and commanders), were transferred to quarantine. The patient separately, comrades separately. Then it became clear that, with high probability, everyone got infected.

In early May, Russia's Vector institute presented rapid tests for the virus's presence. The test was a simple glass capsule with a needle on the end. To test, it was enough to prick the subject with the needle. If the patient's blood contained a sufficient quantity of viral particles, the moist silvery powder in the capsule turned sharply yellow. The geneticist-virologists had cooked up something based on qPCR, in a solution binding a fluorescent, whose activation was tied to the reaction. Unfortunately, establishing mass production of the test ran into entirely predictable difficulties.

In any case, the formula was shared with the entire global scientific community. A non-trivial solution was found for production too: in India, where mass production was to be established, the assembly lines were manned by the avowedly sick. The finished product was simply sterilised with radiation.

It should be said that India was perhaps the calmest country amid this chaos. Life and death had always been treated philosophically there. So the fact that a lethal disease was sweeping the country was simply ignored by most of the population. Indeed — why stress when the illness was asymptomatic (surely behavioural changes didn't count?), nothing hurt anywhere, and your limbs weren't falling off. So Indians lived as they'd always lived. And the fact that after a while they began dying didn't provoke much emotion in the nation. The next victim's corpse might lie in the street for a couple of days; if the clothes were decent, someone might even strip and steal them (with the predictable consequences). After that, kind people would come along to drag the body to the nearest ravine or to the monks (depending on the volunteer's degree of kindness and the body's physical condition). In principle, by the still-living WHO experts' assessment, India was doomed to total extinction. India itself, however, wasn't bothered by this melancholy fact.

Infected servicemen in both Russia and the USA formed volunteer groups. It was they who continued to patrol cities and help the infected in the late stages. They had no chance of survival in any case, and this was infinitely better than sitting and waiting for death. By May, the migrant problem had resolved itself. Even the most enraged among them had become completely tame. The off-the-chart doses of neurotransmitters did their work. In the major cities, fraternisation reigned — military volunteers, civilians, migrants... All feuds were forgotten. It looked like an idyll, were it not for the awareness that most of those fraternising wouldn't live to see autumn.

In principle, by May there were virtually no uninfected people left in the major cities.

Far more interesting was the situation in the smaller cities. Where the population and municipal authorities proved strong and decisive enough, measures were taken to identify and purge carriers. From banishment to physical destruction. As a consequence, there arose city-states with entirely healthy, if considerably thinned (thanks to the "Witch Hunts"), populations — extremely hostile toward any outsiders.

In some cases, such structures arose not spontaneously but quite deliberately and artificially. For example, Zheleznogorsk; Akademgorodok in Novosibirsk (where surviving and healthy army units joined forces with healthy scientists, established a perimeter by blocking Berdskoye Highway and the bridge over the Ob, then imposed strict discipline and military order. The locals, half-jokingly, half-seriously, dubbed the new entity "The Brotherhood of Steel, Russian Branch"). A similar situation arose in Kronstadt. The military had gathered doctors and scientists from St Petersburg there as early as February, strictly restricting entry to the city. On the basis of local hospitals, a unified research centre was deployed. The floating nuclear power station, launched the previous summer, was also towed there. It came in handy.

Analogous processes were underway in the USA. In China, owing to the large population and, crucially, its density, things were more complicated. Nevertheless, similar centres of civilisation formed there too.

By midsummer, approximately two billion people remained alive. Of these, roughly a billion were "the recovered." In common parlance, they were called "carriers" — "hosts." The short, punchy English word stuck.

Another approximately billion (for obvious reasons, a census was extremely difficult) consisted of survivors. People who had managed to avoid infection during the worst phase. Predominantly these were either inhabitants of the city-states, or escapists who had gone out into the wilderness early on. Some enclaves maintained contact with each other using satellites or amateur radio. Former military among the survivors tried to maintain communication technologies.

Fortunately for humanity, the infected didn't die instantly, and the behavioural changes didn't entail complete irresponsibility and stupefaction (quite the contrary — in the first stage, intellectual abilities only grew). So the last surviving personnel at nuclear plants and complex chemical facilities "turned off the lights when they left." They shut down reactors, ensured proper cooling of the active zones, saw to the completion of chemical reactions at factories.

In short, they did everything possible so that humanity, having barely survived the epidemic, would not die of nuclear contamination and not repeat the fate of the population of Bhopal.

On the whole, one might have been glad that it all ended not so badly and begun gradually to rebuild civilisation... Were it not for several factors:

First, the "hosts" were contagious. These were not simply drastically dulled, love-besotted hippies, barely able to meet their basic needs (the average host IQ was 75–80. There were geniuses who reached 95. Though there were also proud possessors of a decent chimpanzee's intellect: 60 appeared in the reports rather often). These were walking reservoirs of infection. Yes, it had somehow stabilised in their bodies without killing them, without causing visible symptoms — and even, rather, protecting them from other diseases (research showed that hosts didn't get sick with anything other than the base virus. Moreover, not only didn't they get sick but catastrophically refused to become infected with anything else). At the same time, unlike the survivors, the hosts gladly clumped into large packs, in which they roamed the empty expanses of depopulated continents. Aggression was not characteristic of them; however, the mere fact of their existence sufficed.

The second factor was that the virus was steadily mutating. No, it was still detected by the rapid test (millions of units of which by the end of summer had been delivered to the largest survivor settlements, from where they were distributed as far as possible to smaller communities), but it was manifesting new properties. The virus had acquired resistance to external factors, so that now, a host's spit could infect a survivor a week after contact with the dried material. The incubation period of some strains had also changed. Now there was a strain that could develop for half a year. Fortunately, this variant, during incubation, was not contagious. But the reports that emerged in late August about the discovery of zoonotic strains of the virus were genuinely alarming.

Third was the need to provide food: like it or not, the survivors needed feeding. This meant working the land — often at considerable distance from the fortress. Where the probability of a chance encounter with a group of hosts rose sharply. No, the hosts certainly wouldn't attack. But friendly group hugs with guaranteed infection were not in the new-minted farmers' plans either.

There was also a fourth reason. Into the survivor community, apparently from the hermits, came the legend of Host Masters. It was said that these people had not only survived the infection with their selves intact but had also grown considerably smarter. It was said that the hosts obeyed them unconditionally. It was also said that the Masters had not been subject to the chemical neurocorrection that all the infected had undergone. The legends held that the Masters were, on the contrary, filled with hatred for those who had abandoned them to their fate.

It was also said that the dozen settlements that had recently gone silent on the radio were on the mysterious Masters' conscience.

How many they were, who they were, how you became one and why — nobody knew. Military intelligence from the survivors on all continents had not yet confirmed these rumours. But neither had they refuted them. And the legends continued to pass between people, growing new details.

A new autumn was approaching. The first autumn of the surviving world.

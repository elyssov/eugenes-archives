Chapter 6

The Russians have a good proverb about a roasted rooster. Of course, it's really about something else... But you'll agree, it often describes the WHO rather well.

Bureaucratic machines aren't known for speed of reaction.

The CDC began sounding the alarm after the death toll from the new virus exceeded two dozen. By this point, the students had long since returned to their studies (though, owing to certain behavioural changes, studying wasn't going so well. They scraped through on dramatically enhanced intellectual abilities — some consolation for the emerging lack of focus). Most students were strong, young, healthy lads... And therefore held out the maximum. Six months.

By that point, for entirely understandable reasons, Yale more closely resembled not a prestigious university but a lupanar-cum-hippie-commune. Of all those infected in the first wave, only two survived by January: Basil and Max. That said, it hadn't come cheap...

Mass student deaths began in late November. After the tenth death with a diagnosis of "influenza," the CDC established quarantine and began hunting those students who had decided to flee the epicentre before the perimeter was set.

Naturally, they caught far from everyone. Moreover, in mid-January, mass mortality began in Somerset. Only after that did the WHO connect these events with the increasing deaths in India and China.

The picture emerging was extremely ugly.

By late January, the virus was finally isolated. Based on the aggregate of symptoms, it was named "amplectavirus." Its genetic code was, of course, sequenced. In the remnants of the original AAV structure, they even found the University of Sapporo's signature. The problem was that this information didn't help. Especially since Hibiki Masashika's deputy — Mamoru Ibu, as it turned out — had received no data about the old geneticist's project and was not himself conducting any work with AAV.

By February, two-thirds of Yale University's students and faculty were dead. The town of Somerset, Kentucky was three-quarters gone.

Characteristic behavioural changes were being demonstrated by people in Russia, in Chile, across the entire European Union.

The EU finally made an announcement.

Yes, a new virus. Yes, it causes behavioural changes. Yes, currently incurable.

Panic gripped the world.

The end of civilisation came on February 27, when Chinese troops began liquidating the population of the Qinhuangdao urban district. On that very same day, the WHO published its mortality data on the amplectavirus.

Worldwide, by that point, more than 50 million people were dead. Of those, approximately 27 million in India, 18 million in China. The remainder was evenly distributed across the globe, with a peak of 2 million in the USA. In Africa, not a single death had yet been linked to the amplectavirus, although millions of Africans were already demonstrating behavioural deviations.

The Chinese authorities' decision and the data on the real state of affairs breached the thin barrier still holding human civilised restraint.

By the end of the day, most countries had not only completely sealed their national borders (without regard for possible economic consequences) but had also imposed quarantine on most cities and territorial entities. Nobody had yet gone as far as the Chinese, but civilians were ordered to stay home, not leaving their premises if possible. If going outside was necessary, all exposed skin was to be covered, breathing strictly through a gauze mask, and no bare-skin contact with anyone.

Major cities were divided into quarantine zones.

None of this helped much.

Civilian paranoia set in.

**

Jake had joined the Marine Corps to protect his homeland from terrorism, and because in the Smith family there wasn't a single man who hadn't served. Want to be a Smith — serve.

Today, he was fighting a far more terrifying enemy. Jake stood in the cordon around Lakewood, California. According to unconfirmed rumours, the entire Presidential family was already ill, but that wasn't so important. Far more important was not to let the infection leave the epicentre. To give the sick the chance to die in peace, in order to save the few survivors.

Jake recalled the training film they'd been shown at the National Guard base, temporarily used by the USMC as a forward operating base. It had shown those who'd recovered, and the sight had lodged deep in the Marine's consciousness:

Approximately 10% of the infected made it through the terminal stage of the illness, through the crisis, and then recovered. If you could call it that. Certainly, their lives were no longer in danger — but they remained contagious, and could this even be called life?

The recovered became incredibly dull. Personality was maximally simplified, flattened. Motivation was reduced to the most basic needs. Communication — to the most primitive level. Though the desire to socialise only intensified. Under "natural" conditions, the recovered clumped together in packs. In the training film, there was a segment showing the recovered communicating, filmed in a CDC isolation ward. Essentially, for several hours three people (two young men and a girl), squatting in a warm corner and cracking sunflower seeds, exchanged brief interjections and prepositions carrying no information, only occasionally breaking off to relieve themselves and copulate.

Jake had already decided that if he got infected, he'd ask — while he could still think — to be put down. Because death was better than that kind of existence. He'd already discussed this with his husband. Hussein agreed completely.

They'd served in the same unit for six months, since LGBTQ-community pressure had legalised open homosexual relationships and marriages in the American military.

On the road leading out of the city, a battered old van appeared. Before reaching the checkpoint, it nosed into the barriers. Sniper Sam raised his M-21 and pressed his eye to the scope. Jake headed for the barriers.

Meanwhile, a woman of about thirty-five climbed out of the vehicle. Fussing, she extracted a child's car seat with an infant from the back. A girl of about seven slipped out after her. From the boot, the woman retrieved a small backpack, popped the wheels on the car seat, and headed toward Jake.

— Halt! — The Marine raised his rifle. He tried not to look at the children.

— Sir, please. We're definitely healthy — no temperature, and I'm not going to pounce on you to rape you. Those are the main symptoms, right? We haven't had contact with anyone.

— Ma'am, I'm sorry, but you'll have to return to the city. Federal government orders.

— Sir, have pity at least on the children... They haven't done anything wrong! — At the mention of children, the girl pressed close to her mother, trustingly offering her head under the free hand. The mother pressed the child to herself, absently running her fingers through the fringe.

— Ma'am, I'm sorry. The most I can do is call the medics. Perhaps they can do something. For now, please move back from the barrier and drive your car to the parking bay three hundred yards from here.

— Thank you, sir! Thank you! Even that much! You're so kind!

Jake lowered his rifle and took off the damned helmet. The sun was merciless. And this was supposed to be mid-March. Besides — the nerves... The nerves were shot to hell.

The woman, noticing the soldier's glistening forehead, produced a bottle of water from her backpack:

— Poor boy! Here, take this — you'll feel a little better — she held out the bottle and smiled so warmly that it pierced Jake to the core. He already understood everything.

— Thank you, no need. Everything's fine, ma'am.

— But it's so hard for you standing here, protecting us from this epidemic — the woman's eyes were full of warmth and sympathy — at least let me wipe your forehead.

Sam's shot cracked across the ears. The infected woman slowly sank to her knees. Her child didn't have time to understand anything — Sam fired again.

Jake jammed the helmet back on his head with shaking hands. The horror was that the infected weren't zombies. Quite the opposite — they were far more human than those who hadn't yet fallen ill.

Toward the soldier, a hazmat-suited disposal team was already hurrying... The following morning, Jake submitted his resignation from the Marine Corps. This was denied. Hussein beat him savagely for his cowardice.

That night, getting up for water, he found Jake hanged.

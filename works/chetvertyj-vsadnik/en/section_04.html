Chapter 4

People are mortal. We try to forget this frightening fact, but unfortunately it is in our nature. Death is the eternal equaliser, coming for the rich and the poor alike. For the weak and the mighty of this world. Old people die, but so do the young.

Kate was buried in the small cemetery of her hometown of Somerset, beneath a spreading tree, not far from West Columbia Street.

The girl had died suddenly. Just four days prior, she'd been tearing around her cousin Pete's ranch on her beloved gelding Red, then spent the whole night at a club with friends... And the next morning, she woke with a temperature of 104°F and crushing weakness throughout her body.

Doc Smitters, the family physician who had rushed to the Greyhawks and had known Kate since she was an infant, gave an unequivocal diagnosis: influenza with complicated course. For three days, the girl's young body fought. At one point, it seemed the illness was retreating.

And then, after a brief remission, the agony began. Temperature rose to 106°F, the girl was shaking non-stop... But amazingly, Kate herself didn't seem particularly bothered. The whole time, she encouraged her family, never lost her composure, never complained...

The whole time, Doc stayed by her bedside, fighting for her life.

And when, before death, another very brief moment of clarity came, she raised herself from the bed with her last strength and hugged the elderly doctor, whispering in his ear — "Don't be upset, Uncle Ben. I know you did everything you could. I just... it's time..." Kate kissed the doctor on the cheek and sank back onto the pillows, never to rise again...

Chris couldn't make it to the funeral. The news of Kate's death reached him in Hawaii — Basil, wracked by guilt, had sent his less-than-wealthy friend a ticket and a voucher to one of Honolulu's most expensive hotels. The Russian remembered well how once, during a drinking session, Chris had voiced his long-standing dream of visiting the memorial of the battleship Arizona at Pearl Harbor, where his great-grandfather had perished on December 7, 1941.

The guy took the mobile, listened to Kate's brother, and fell face-down onto the sand, sobbing like a child. He had sincerely loved this girl, and the loss burned his soul like napalm.

Perhaps it was precisely this sincerity that drew the attention of the acknowledged queen of the beach, Angelica, daughter of a wealthy Chilean cattle baron, who had been vacationing in Hawaii for a second month already. It was with her that Chris found solace for his grief...

Summer was coming, the season of holidays and breaks. Yale students, being fairly well-off, were rather less constrained in their choice of holiday destination than the average American. Some students went travelling through Europe. Mostly France, Switzerland. A small but close-knit company of drinking buddies headed to Basil's in Russia — the Russian had long been inviting everyone over.

However, some were drawn to far more exotic places: two people went to India. One, together with his girlfriend and her sister, to China. By an irony of fate, all of these students were Kate's last lovers.

Yale students, Kate's friends and lovers, were scattering across the planet.

There were about 200 days left until the catastrophe.

Chapter 8

Human civilisation of the late 20th and early 21st century is fragile.

No, if you transported a benighted peasant to our time... Or, for that matter, the King of England from, say, the tenth century, and walked him through the streets of London, Paris or Moscow — he would be shocked. A modern megacity, with a population many times exceeding all his subjects — would leave him dumbfounded and make him believe ours is a civilisation of titans.

Only this civilisation stands not on legs of clay, but of glass. Cities are clinically unsuited to isolation. The inhabitants of these cities have long forgotten how to do anything for themselves. London and Paris were once ravaged by epidemics. But the survivors managed to revive life in what seemed dead centres of civilisation.

Modern cities do not possess such resilience...

...The first to vanish from Moscow were the guest workers. Initially, when rumours of the epidemic were only beginning to creep but the government wasn't making any sudden moves — they fled of their own accord, with an animal instinct sensing the beginning of the end... and carrying the epidemic to their home republics.

After the February events, after martial law was declared — they began shooting them. Under the circumstances, when the very survival of the nation was at stake, it was decided that persons not subject to registration and control were simply infected "by default." And, in fact, so they were.

This secret has long been known to physicians — the sharp worsening of flu epidemics in Moscow and other large Russian cities, compared to the 1990s, was owed not to changes in the flu strain (whose pathogenicity had remained comparatively stable for decades) but to the presence in cities of natural, uncontrolled reservoirs of infection. As the joke goes — "who counts them?" Tajiks, Uzbeks — living in the hellish overcrowding of illegal dormitories, crammed by the dozens into small rooms — instantly caught every new strain. Having no access to modern and expensive therapies, they were ill "to the bitter end," continuing to go to work, ride public transport, hand you hamburgers at McDonald's... Until they recovered or died from complications. And all this time, they spread the virus to every corner of the city, from the slums of Kuzminki, Aviamotornaya or Prospekt to the elite gated communities on Rublyovka and Krestovsky Island. Like cockroaches before them, migrants were present everywhere — lived in the khrushchyovkas, trimmed gardens at villas in Zhukovka, rode the same carriages as native city-dwellers, sold them food in eateries. Delivering the virus to every home, every family, everyone who came into contact with them.

Physicians had known this secret for a long time. The country's leadership knew it too — at various times, various doctors had written about it to every level of authority, after which the reports invariably ended up in the drawer, until it was too late.

When the matter was no longer a common flu but a deadly infection, the decision was taken instantly. Deportation no longer made sense — resources were needed to fight the epidemic, not to play at humanitarianism that was meaningless in the conditions of a new plague. Collecting them into some kind of concentration camps was equally pointless, for exactly the same reason. Treatment — even more so, especially considering that all treatment boiled down to palliative care.

The troops were given their orders. Soldiers whipped up by propaganda didn't hesitate much. Martial law was declared in the city.

At first, migrants were rounded up on the streets, driven into corpse-wagons and brought to the sand quarries at Podgoryevo and Dushonovo (Kolotushi near St Petersburg). There, people were shot at the edge of an already-prepared pit, then covered with quicklime, and the next batch was lined up. Those who tried to break free and run could be thrown into the pit alive. Given that the pits were dug quite deep, the chances of climbing out were negligible.

They grabbed people "not by passport but by face," so a considerable number of perfectly respectable citizens with Eastern or Caucasian roots also fell under the sweep. Some managed, having already arrived at the quarry, to reach the commanders of the Einsatzgruppen, show their passport, convince them they clearly didn't fall under the "public health protection act." For the first week, this worked. Many were released. Eventually, information about what was happening leaked to the diasporas. Troops began meeting resistance. Here, the authorities' thoughtless policy of mass importation of foreign labour came back to haunt them — particularly epic were the events of March 15, when a crowd of eight hundred thousand stormed the Kremlin.

Although the government and, above all, the president had already been evacuated to Yamantau by this point, the Kremlin garrison gave battle. The migrants, prior to the Kremlin assault, had managed to arm themselves by ransacking several police stations, and had also acquired heavy equipment with which they easily demolished the Spassky Gates and breached the fortress wall in places. Garrison resistance was crushed by "mass," after which the mob poured into the Diamond Fund, the Faceted Chamber, the Armoury. The Kremlin regiment cadets were torn apart alive. The enraged mob, spurred on by testosterone-fuelled secretion, seized the garrison's heavy weapons.

Nevertheless, the Kremlin forces bought time. The army managed to regroup. The air force struck first, drenching the crowd gathered on Red Square with napalm. GUM caught fire, the Cathedral of St Basil the Blessed — but under the circumstances, none of this mattered anymore. The blazing, pain-maddened crowd rushed down the Vasilyevsky Descent toward the river but was ground under the treads of tanks that had just emerged onto the embankment. Those who tried to flee through the Historical Museum were shot at point-blank range with heavy machine guns and automatic cannons by motorised infantry in APCs.

The bulk of the rebels was annihilated without even entering close-range engagement. Those who tried to run were finished off by Mi-28N gunships with thermal imagers, scrambled from Kubinka.

The hardcore of the most desperate and armed guest workers barricaded themselves in the ancient fortress, taking up all-round defence. They already understood their fate but had no intention of surrendering without a fight.

It took two days to clear the Kremlin, with serious losses in the special forces units assigned the operation. During the clearing, the walls and towers of the fortress suffered considerable damage; the Grand Kremlin Palace was almost completely destroyed. The dying migrants, out of sheer spite, blew up the Archangel Cathedral using construction explosives, of which, as it turned out, they'd acquired a generous supply before the assault.

Some of the damage to the Kremlin complex was inflicted by government forces — the attack helicopters in particular.

From March 16 onward, migrant workers were declared persons outside the law. Reinforced military patrols fanned out across Russian cities, shooting everyone who even slightly resembled a migrant. Registration and accents were no longer checked — mass paranoia had set in.

In the city at that time — in Moscow alone — there remained at least five million illegal migrants. If you counted people who were in the city perfectly legally but had Middle Eastern or Caucasian roots, the total number of persons who had simultaneously found themselves outside the law exceeded ten million. Across the entire country, the figure was probably around twenty million.

Some of these people decided to try breaking through the cordon and fleeing the city. Such attempts were pursued and eliminated by aviation or purpose-built "jäger" teams in Tigr vehicles with thermal imagers and machine guns.

Some went to ground, ceasing to leave their flats. Many were saved by their Russian neighbours — bringing food, warding off patrols with the claim that "the Ivanovs live here, they're just under the weather." Many, on the contrary, were turned in by their neighbours — and then death came in the form of soldiers in chem-suits with automatic rifles. It all depended on pre-crisis relations with the neighbours, and many "guests of the capital" had time, already standing at the edge of the quarry, to bitterly regret having been rude and aggressive toward them.

Many, deciding "you only live once," threw themselves into abandon. Crimes against persons surged, especially rapes and robberies. Outright murder gangs appeared, hunting specifically Russians — out of revenge.

The country plunged into bloody chaos.

Meanwhile, famine began in the cities: suppliers were afraid to drive food into cities gripped by the underground fire of civil war and riddled with infection. Moreover, many shops — above all the mega-malls — had closed. Some due to the absence of staff afraid of catching the disease, some due to the fears of the owners themselves. Large shopping centres were shut by government order, as potential breeding grounds for infection.

Medical services also deteriorated: clinics were closed for exactly the same reason as the mega-malls. Medical aid was provided by ambulances, but after several incidents where the address turned out to harbour a gang of war-path migrants who killed the "smurfs" on the spot — doctors became afraid to go out without a military escort. The doctors themselves traded their blue scrubs (which had, in fact, given them their nickname) for plague-protection suits. This, too, did not add to the population's calm.

Beyond the infection, which was somewhere out there, people now faced a threat from their own society. No wonder that flight from the cities began. Now it wasn't just migrants doomed to extermination who were running, but ordinary peaceful residents. The helicopters dutifully engaged thermal-contrast targets. On "animal trails" through the forests, entire graveyards formed of those who'd tried to break through. The corpses simply rotted, as the smart animals had long migrated from the combat zone, frightened by the commotion, and the dumb ones had long since been shot on thermal signatures.

And yet — many managed to break through. Unexpected solutions were deployed: thermal blankets cooled with CO₂ extinguishers, tinfoil suits. In the end, it was the height of spring, and there weren't enough helicopters to control every hundred metres.

Strangely enough, urban infrastructure, more or less, in most places still worked. Water flowed from taps, sewerage functioned, there was light and even the Internet worked. The secret was simple — the utility workers were infected almost to a man, but out of human decency had resolved to "stay at their posts" for as long as was remotely possible. A call went out to citizens, urging those who had already realised they were infected to join the effort of keeping the city running. There was no shortage of volunteers. The utility workers gladly taught them how to keep the city afloat for a while longer.

The doomed teaching others, to prolong life for the living...

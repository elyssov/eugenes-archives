THE PRICE OF DISHONOUR

When all this commotion began, I was ten.

I clearly remember when the Moon exploded. At that moment it was right above our hemisphere, and I could see the process in the smallest detail. With my own eyes, not in broadcast or recording.

I wasn't scared then. Didn't know how yet. I expected an admirable fireworks display, and was rather disappointed when I saw only several dim flashes near the Ocean of Storms, where the crust turned out to be thinner and plasma was the first to break to the surface. And that's all. It didn't even strongly change shape at first — only small jagged edges emerged at the disc's rim. A normal ring of debris formed only several years later.

My classmates and I even wanted to write a protest letter to the cykts — why are you slacking, you bastards? We have only one Moon, if you decided to break it, then do it beautifully, so there's something to remember!

When I grew up and began to understand celestial mechanics better, I understood that we'd been spared once again. Of course, the captains of their fleet could have arranged a real show for us. The power of their planetary booster was enough that the planet would have instantly scattered into pieces, like in the old film... what's it called... "Star Wars," yes. Only this would have been the last thing we saw. The impact of Moon fragments at hyperbolic speeds our planet definitely would not have survived. Therefore they very meticulously calculated the demolition procedure. It was an almost surgically precise operation — to conduct an intimidation action without significant human losses. The trajectory of every fragment had to be accounted for.

It wasn't possible to account for ALL. But it's not the cykts' fault here — just chaos theory. Their mathematicians count no worse than our computers. They ensured the maximum theoretically permissible accuracy. And all sufficiently large fragments which entered intersection courses were then shot down by our PKO. Then it still existed. Possibly they deliberately allowed such "inaccuracy" — while we were defending against asteroids, the cykt landing ships without particular difficulty passed the last echelon of defence and began landing invasion forces.

Three million infantry and a hundred thousand units of armoured vehicles. And that's only the first wave. In space we could fight at least somewhat on equal terms. But on the surface... Those who managed — put their paws up. The surrendered weren't shot. Those who didn't manage... well, may their dust rest.

The most insulting thing is that it wasn't even an army. The cykts in general don't have what in the past was called a "regular army." Only people's militia. Militia. Volunteers. After the Butcher's reckless actions, many volunteers were found. And considering that a modest worker cykt was equal to our paratrooper in hand-to-hand... you can imagine what they turn into with normal equipment and a month's training.

"We are not at war with anyone. We are kind plush bear cubs. It just happened by chance that we are by evolution itself better adapted to violence than you. However, unlike you, we did not erect it into a cult. Only sometimes the need arises to take a big club out of the basement. This does not bring us joy. You have a good proverb... God did not give horns to the goring cow. This is a general law of evolution in many galaxies — beings without natural weapons have no natural brakes against using them."

At times I admire them. If anyone is worthy of being called a superior race, it's the cykts. Not us. They are stronger, smarter, more fertile, more industrious — and at that more pleasant and kinder than us. It's quite possible that the human race is simply a mistake of nature.

But while we exist — we'll do everything to prove the opposite.

I grew up — and with me changed the planet, as if it too had entered puberty. Demilitarisation, cultural revolution, agricultural revolution... With each year customs changed. Children born after the invasion already sincerely didn't understand how their ancestors could live without the help of the ubiquitous kind, generous and wise plush fluffies.

It's not even that the economy changed and now every person was provided from birth with an allowance one could very well live on. After all, before the Calypsian Slaughter we also weren't in want. After all, we were a space civilisation. And exit into Deep Space automatically means basic socio-economic problems on the planet's surface are more-or-less successfully solved.

But they gave us something far more important. Demand.

Every hack now has hundreds of thousands of readers throughout the Galaxy. Attentive, sensitive, enthusiastic readers. Every artist and director — hundreds of thousands of viewers. Every composer — hundreds of thousands of listeners.

By some strange evolutionary kink, the cykts turned out to be incapable of artistic creativity themselves — but at the same time joyfully and willingly consume it. Their trouble is that they turned out to be... too efficient. They simply don't know how to be idle and kill time, as we willingly do. In their days are twenty-seven hours. Nine of them a normal cykt sleeps. Nine — works, and in three hours fully provides all his own needs, in an hour — the needs of old, children and disabled, in the remaining five — all the needs of the state. One hour he mates. And another eight — suffers from terrible, unquenchable sensory hunger! And is ready to pay with anything, even his life, just to somehow fill this void.

If not for the idiot Butcher, we could have swallowed them whole without a single shot. Simply culturally assimilate. Buy them with our classics and modernism, our cartoons and legends, our fanfics and originals, our games and symphonies. Thousands of fans would themselves bring beloved authors the heads of their queens. On a platter.

Possibly this is the real reason that at the first meeting the Community of Cykts declared an information blockade on us. They felt a threat that came not from our combat starships but from our habit of making up all sorts of nonsense. The official version about "evil" they allegedly sensed in our ancestors doesn't hold water at all. Yes, cykts are indeed empaths and can sense evil intent against themselves — but not on a neighbouring continent! And much less at space distances!

There's a hypothesis that in large collectives their empathic field intensifies. But still. Think for yourselves — you know there are evil thugs living in the neighbouring house. So evil that even talking to them is dangerous, suddenly they'll attack. Will you move into this house with your wife and children? It turns out the cykts simultaneously considered us dangerous enough to avoid contact — and simultaneously harmless enough to settle nearby fearing nothing. Well that's nonsense!

But if you suppose they didn't fear our soldiers but feared our writers, then information blockade becomes quite a logical precautionary measure. Necessary and sufficient. Until things came to planetary boosters, of course. But if they did — even better. Then dangerous independent creators can be neatly pinned down and turned into a milk cow. Into a guarantee of the Community's further prosperity.

With such stakes, eighteen billion become an insignificant trifle. The Community replenished these losses in several months. It's not excluded they even threw us the booster schematics — to neatly nudge us toward the necessary decision. However, no need to fall into conspiracy theories. We didn't need to be nudged. We ourselves perfectly played the role assigned to us. The Butcher's idiocy was enough for everyone.

Alas, this is also only a hypothesis. No worse and no better than others. There are versions that paint cykts as demons of hell, there are such that make them quite angels. Use in both cases — zero. No more than from our fanfics. We have abundance of speculations but insufficient facts. We already know well enough what an ordinary cykt breathes — a worker, peasant or militiaman. But have no idea what their authorities are guided by. Even the most devoted fans of our creativity can't say this — they themselves don't know. A normal cykt simply doesn't think to ponder the motives of superiors. He's told — he does. Possibly this is the key to the stability and invincibility of their civilisation.

I'm sprawled at the feet of a tourist family. Practically licking the pads of their hind paws. Hysterically begging them to forgive us. Forgive for everything. For burned planets, for downed ships, for our stupidity and aggressiveness. I ask not to transfer fathers' sins to children, ask to understand that we can still become better and purer...

I'm absolutely sincere. I really, really regret what happened then. If I had the opportunity, I'd strangle the Butcher with my own hands. That's why I was chosen for this mission. With cykts you can't do otherwise. They're empaths — they'll sense lies and pretence.

And the fact that at the second level of perception I attentively analyse their reactions in the smallest details — they don't need to know yet. Even if they sense some curiosity from my side (carefully hidden behind a powerful hysterical emo-background), unlikely they'll suspect anything seditious.

Cykts are empaths. But not telepaths. For twenty years we've very well learned to understand and use the difference between these two concepts. Ancestors would even find it hard to imagine how easily we — the new generation — can evoke in ourselves any desired mood, as long as it doesn't contradict our beliefs. Maybe that's why there are so many good actors among us. Or vice versa.

They understandingly meow and hug me with warm paws. The youngest rubs his fluffy little forehead against me. I scratch him behind the ear. The hysteria gradually passes, and I become convinced that life is not so bad.

The pseudo-Father of the family at his own expense orders riips pastries for all. With sincere love and gratitude I smile at him. The pseudo-Mother of the family feels me between the legs and invites me to a small evening orgy. With joy I agree.

And tiny drones hovering at three hundred metres' altitude carefully record every detail of their reactions. For our xenopsychologists' notice.

Yes, we can no longer produce military electronics. Or even dual-use. But the workers at the factory on Vega are big fans of my younger sister's creativity. No, they won't sell me a planetary booster — even for the most brilliant story in the world. Not yet. Although I have an acquaintance, captain of an attack cruiser, who very much likes riips from father's farm. But I never ask her for any services.

But a couple of trucks with cheap junk for pollinating fields — they can always supply. Lovely lads. I love them.

Also because not a single cykt will ever rat on me to authorities. No, not because they fear being left without new works of a beloved author. Just such a concept as denunciation is absent in their mentality. In principle.

The most formidable and powerful weapon is information. Stronger than attack cruisers and planetary boosters. If we can understand how cykts will react to this or that of our actions, we can negotiate. Not as house pets. As partners.

At 17:52 planetary time a call wakes me. I carefully push off the soft fluffy body, under which is felt impenetrable armour. Reach for the comm, activate...

— Chief, everything's lost! Everything's lost, Chief! We're done!

— Calm down, Alice. I'm still breathing, and you too, it seems. Everything else can be fixed. Pull yourself together, soldier, and report by form. What happened?

— The Butcher... I mean Admiral Perrin... got somewhere twenty jump-ships with planetary boosters... and aimed them at the capital and at key colonies of the Community!

I sit down where I stood. Consciousness habitually divides experiences into three layers, although there's now no point in this.

Outer layer, strongest and most painful — compassion. Capital worlds... The Pearl Necklace of the Empire... That's no less than a trillion lives total! More than ever lived on Earth!

Second layer — horror. I don't even know, really don't know, what's worse — if we're ground to dust in retaliation, or if we're allowed to live even after this.

And third — vexation. Everything we achieved with backbreaking labour — all gone because of a single idiot whom they didn't let us kill in his time. The fact that the cykts are essentially themselves at fault, with their own hands carefully preserving their own destruction — changes little. Now.

— How many... Dead? — I squeeze out in a sunken voice.

— None.

It doesn't immediately reach me that this wasn't said by Alice. The father of the family returned to the room and with great interest listened to our dialogue.

— Nevertheless you, Earthlings, are amazing beings. Even the least xenophobic of you preserve thought artifacts characteristic of ancestors. On a subconscious level you preserve the conviction that you are the only race in the universe capable of long-term planning and technical development.

— You mean to say that...

— These bomb-ships are obsolete by two decades. You seriously think they're still capable of representing a threat to anyone? Or that you are the first race we've encountered using planetary boosters as a decisive argument?

Moreover your scientists copied them from our own technologies. Perhaps, then, immediately after Moon's destruction, they could have reached peripheral colonies — but not Capital worlds. Although they've long had purely symbolic significance for the Community's prosperity — they're dear to us as memory.

— You... you mean to say you knew about them?

— Specifically about these ships — no, human. But no one doubted that sooner or later your extremists would throw something similar. Although, honestly, we expected... something more impressive. Well, at least capable of bypassing our observer-satellites in the Lunar Ring and atmospheric drones. But personally I'm fully satisfied. This spontaneity of yours is so delightful... I've already ordered one of these rarities for my clan's home museum. There'll be a fight between museums for the rest. Most likely they'll go to the authorities of those Capital worlds at which they were aimed. In the whole Community you won't find such antiquity in fully functional condition.

It starts to reach me.

— So you... you left the Butcher alive to mock him? You were absolutely sure he couldn't cause you real harm?

— Not entirely. Our analysts allowed a probability of seven percent that he'd find a real way to cause damage equal to those eighteen billion, or exceeding them. And risk of forty-two percent that he'd inflict significant damage on your own planet. Which, in principle, did happen — jumps at the atmosphere boundary will provide considerable trouble for weather control services. But the risk was deemed worth it.

— Why?! For what?!

— Sensory hunger, human. The same sensory hunger. Consider this another work of art — and your last contribution for destroyed Alpha and Secunda. All these twenty years of the admiral's gradually growing despair were a magnificent empathic symphony that ended with the brilliant crescendo of missile launch. Your panic and despair became a wonderful counterpoint to it, shading Perrin's vengeful gloating. And how he played that piano in Vienna while bomb-ships were taking off... ah, how wonderfully he played... a great pianist died in him!

One of the youngest Quasi-Daughters rubs her head against my hip.

— Time to the shelter. A hurricane is approaching.

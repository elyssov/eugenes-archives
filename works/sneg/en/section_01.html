Chapter 1

Hans slipped and, cursing, tumbled into the darkness of a deep, snow-covered ravine — more of a washout, really, formed over years by a forest stream. At the last moment, Feldwebel Thomas grabbed him by the scruff and held on, noting, incidentally, that even while falling Hans hadn't let go of his MG. Nevertheless, the screaming had to be stopped, so Thomas yanked the Gefreiter closer and hissed in his ear:

— Quiet! They'll hear!

The soldier shut up in terror. It was shameful to admit, but Thomas well understood the cause of that terror. They were the only survivors from a full-strength regiment. And just yesterday, what had happened seemed impossible. Today, he understood that the very fact that he and Hans had survived was already a miracle. ...

...After the rout at Kiev, the Russians had rolled back, no longer resisting as ferociously as in the first phase of the war. The enormous losses in manpower and materiel were taking their toll. The RKKA's resources and resilience had been exhausted if not completely, then to a significant degree.

Thomas's regiment was advancing in the second echelon when the Wehrmacht, exploiting its strategic advantage, managed to conduct a partial rotation of forces — pushing fresh, full-strength units forward and pulling battered ones back for refitting.

The 75th Infantry Division, to which their regiment belonged, had taken up defensive positions after the capture of Kharkov, happily avoiding the meat grinder of the Moscow operation. However, the Soviets were not content with merely defending the ancient capital — they launched a counter-offensive right after New Year. So they had to abandon their cosy positions (and Thomas, incidentally, had managed to establish relations with a charming Kharkov Frau who knew German rather well and had gladly provided a room in her flat to the dashing soldier of the Reich who had brought her liberation from Soviet tyranny. Well, at least that's what she said. As Thomas himself reckoned, at thirty-five having learned a thing or two about life, the primary motive was the enhanced rations — the city had developed quite predictable food supply problems by winter) and repel the suicidal Soviet attacks, now under friendly fire from General Frost.

After the main attacks on their sector were beaten back, command had the unhealthy idea of conducting a reconnaissance in force. For which their regiment was chosen. In principle, after the thrashing the Soviets had received, serious resistance was hardly expected — the only question was what was the point of chasing retreating, shattered Russians?

Nevertheless, orders were orders. And so they caught up with the Russians. Or, more accurately, the Russians chose to give battle. Not many of them remained — two or three hundred men against more than two thousand.

Yes, they had dug in. Yes, they met the attackers with dense (as dense as possible given the ammunition shortage) fire. Yes, the regiment took casualties. But one man in the field is no warrior.

The firefight lasted no more than an hour. Then the Russians began running out of ammunition. Normal people, after that, usually surrender. These ones — went on the offensive. Hand to hand.

Horror, however, was not what this evoked. Surprise — yes. Bewilderment. Someone even broke down, watching the Red Army soldiers run at full height in a bayonet charge across a fire-swept snowy field. It goes without saying that not one of them made it.

The horror came later.

The horror had the appearance of a man in an undershirt and trousers, walking out from the Russian side in the bitter frost. The horror was pale, and the cause of that pallor was visible to all — his arms, spread as if crucified, were scored with the deep furrows of opened veins, from which hung short icicles of frozen blood.

The horror walked with an escort of howling blizzard, crawling at his heels and covering everything behind him in the white darkness of snowfall.

But even that howling could not drown out his voice. Indifferent and dead. Echoing in the temples with its measured cadence. Dead words, in a dead tongue, whose very sound caused an ache in the bones and hellish pain in the teeth.

He was, of course, not reciting this for the living. And those for whom he was reciting — heard.

Yes, of course, when the first stupor passed — many began to shoot at him. But the horror seemed not to notice the hits.

And then — the already fallen began to stir.

Terrible, mutilated by machine gun and rifle fire, but still warm bodies — began to rise. They had no ammunition for their weapons, but they did not need any.

The dead Russians rose to defend their land when the living proved unable.

The regiment wavered. Some ran. Someone began desperately, hysterically raking the advancing corpses with machine gun fire — a waste of ammunition, as it turned out.

In any case, the horror had no intention of letting anyone escape. The blizzard riding on his shoulders — surged forward, covering the still-living with a snowy shroud, blinding them with razor-sharp snowflakes and killing with a soul-draining frost.

And then, from that blizzard, at point-blank range, they emerged — leaving no chance of survival to anyone. The icy hands of the risen dead seized the living soldiers' throats. The resurrected moved no slower than the living — but unlike the living, they couldn't care less about wounds.

Hans knocked the dead man gripping Thomas off with a butt-stroke of his MG-34 to the head. The heavy machine gun worked like a mace, shattering the skull bones and caving in half the Red Army soldier's head — but that which was already dead was not laid to rest by this injury, though it did buy them time. Thomas ran, dragging Hans with him. The dead man, his head caved in, chose a new target and did not pursue them.

The first thought was to try breaking through to the vehicles, but in the frozen murk of the snowstorm — orientation was impossible. In this icy hell of people thrashing about in terror, one could rely only on instinct, and instinct guided Thomas and Hans, who struggled not to lose him. In the chaos raging around them — a sudden lull descended.

And then they saw the one with whom the nightmare had begun. The man in the undershirt with slit veins pierced Thomas with a gaze that in an instant seemed to turn the blood in the Feldwebel's veins into streams of liquid ice. No one moved for a couple of seconds, and then Hans, standing behind, broke the silence.

— Lord God, our Saviour!

The undead creature silently extended a bloodied hand to Thomas's chest and drew from his breast pocket a golden cigarette case. Hans remembered that Thomas had taken it from some Russian woman. Apparently it had belonged to her husband, going back almost to the first world war. In any case, it was reasonable — she had no further use for it. They had burned that village.

The dead man clenched the cigarette case in his hand, closed his icy eyes for a moment, and then looked at the soldiers again. He did not need to speak — they read the sentence in his gaze.

But nothing happened. The dead man merely walked past them and dissolved into the renewed storm. Yet in that last instant of clearing, Thomas caught sight of the tree line. The dash to the forest was like a dash toward life itself. Fortunately, not one of the risen dead pursued them — apparently they were occupied with the extermination of the soldiers still remaining alive. Waiting to be noticed was not in Thomas and Hans's plans...

...Hans saw the lights first. The blizzard ended suddenly, as if someone had flipped a switch. One moment — raging snow, cosmic frost draining the last of their strength. And then it was all over. As soon as the snow settled — before them appeared a little village, as if stepped out of a Christmas card. Thomas inopportunely remembered his little daughters and broke down crying. He had no longer expected to return home, to see them.

The village blazed with lights, as if there were no war at all. The gingerbread Ukrainian huts beckoned with the warmth of smoking chimney stacks, while the icy Russian winter chilled them to the bone, draining the last of their life, strength, ability to move and think...

With difficulty pulling himself together, Thomas got to his feet and walked after Hans, who was running slightly ahead. There was only a little way to go to reach the houses.

Only a little. But the field would not end...

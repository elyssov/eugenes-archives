The Choice

When fate throws you into the arse-end of the inhabited universe — you take it with a certain resentment. Yes, in the twentieth century there was a strange and pathetic saying about "where no human foot has trodden," but how should one feel when your assigned destination is precisely a place where the human foot did tread, fastidiously dusted itself off, and walked away as far as possible?

In any case, the positive moment for Captain First Rank Max von Ritter was that to the "front-line" Sigma-10 station he had been appointed as nothing less than the commander. A native Russian speaker (as Max indeed was) could find plenty of humour in the English designation traditional for Earth's Aerospace Forces combined with the assignment. But with the high post and the pompous (and in Russian also somewhat tautological) title of it — the bonuses, perhaps, ended there.

Humanity had gone into space more than six centuries ago. The creation in the early 21st century of efficient gravitational engines allowed the entire solar system to be mastered very quickly, and the discovery in the middle of the century of the hyperjump principle (for which one had to stumble upon druses of trillium crystals on Europa, on Titan, and then to discover that Phobos and Deimos consist of them almost entirely, which in its turn once again gave rise to many conversations on the theme of Phaethon's fate... But let us not dwell on the sad...) — turned, if not the whole Galaxy, then a fair part of it into a thoroughfare. In the time that had passed, star empires were built and crumbled, thousands of planets were explored and hundreds settled, old colonies began to compete in development with the Metropolis and even outpace it in some respects... And people, strangely enough, had hardly changed. Perhaps because of the sharp expansion of living space all need for inner growth had vanished? Who knows.

Be that as it may, Max's predecessor had shot himself out of boredom. Max himself had been interrogated for a long time before being sent here by a herd of psychiatrists from the New Earth Republic's spacefleet. As a matter of fact, Ritter would have preferred to remain on his battleship "Harrington," and the rank of first officer seemed more than worthy to him. But alas, the incident on Hilary, the knee pierced by an arrow of the aborigines gone wild over 400 years without contact with the metropolis... And how they had managed to make the arrowhead from ship metal, and to sharpen it so that it managed to punch through the protection of a landing suit? In short, the wounding and the aboriginal Max had shot down in response (the latter rather more so — given the absence of serious conflicts in the last 50 years, the fleet had grown fat and infected with excessive liberalism, so such self-defence actions, though deemed in keeping with the letter of the charter, were judged excessively aggressive, for which it was decided to reward the excessively initiative-taking captain with a promotion for saving the rest of the landing group, but to punish him with an honourable exile to the edge of the universe) — had brought him here. To Sigma-10.

Max poured himself a two-finger measure of whisky. The whisky was excellent, well aged, honestly privatised from the galley of the Empress Mariam. This cruise behemoth had been hanging off the zero (designated, strictly speaking, for planetary-class dreadnoughts) docking node now for the third week. The vessel was very venerable in age — it had been built back in the days of the Last Empire of the Paralings. From there the ship had inherited its incredible luxury and imperial baroque style. From the inside it resembled a kind of inside-out wedding cake. To visit Max it had been brought by chance and certain peculiarities of cosmology.

As already mentioned, the Sigma-10 station was one of the most remote from Earth of all space stations. Some three hundred years ago, service on the frontier would have been considered particularly honourable. People at the time had not yet lost faith in little green men, and with each newly discovered system, relying baldly on the law of large numbers, they expected an encounter with them with ever-growing hope. However, one cannot fail to note that this hope was expressed in festooning frontier stations with an insane quantity of emitters, shields from system fortresses, and missile armament sufficient to destroy a small fleet.

In the three hundred years that had passed, people had become definitively convinced of their loneliness in the universe. No, some optimists insisted that perhaps somewhere further out there was something, and the galaxy was so vast that we simply had not yet met our brothers in reason. The usual reasoned response to this was the mention of the fact that our hyperdrives allowed for a monstrous speed of two dozen light years every two hours (strictly speaking, the jump itself took an insignificant time; one simply then had to wait two hours for the crystalline jump assembly to recharge, and this time was constant regardless of the size of the druse), so in six hundred years of expansion — we should at any rate have met someone.

As a result, distant stations were used rather as emergency aid posts. As a matter of fact, it was for this that to the old orbital fortress "Tverda-99," dragged from the orbit of New Warsaw and named "Sigma-10," there had been added a whole heavy frigate "Swift," refitted into a cross between an air ambulance, a light cabotage vessel and a flying first-aid station. And the Sigma would never have seen here even a tolerably heavy "freighter" (for there was nothing for one to do in such a hole), let alone the Mariam, had not, some twenty years ago, a small yellow star floating almost 13 parsecs from the station decided to collapse into a singularity. At first interest in this was confined to scientists. As a matter of fact, this natural phenomenon carried no risk whatsoever. Given powerful gravitational engines, force fields and humanity's deep knowledge of space, it was entirely safe to study a collapsar. The main thing was not to poke too deep into the accretion disc and certainly not to approach even close to the event horizon.

However, after a while it was discovered that the composition of the plasma cloud around the star, in combination with gravitational distortions and other physics that perhaps a million and a half people out of humanity's countless billions understood — had given rise to a phenomenon of unusual beauty. The observable could only be compared to an aurora aureolis, having, however, cosmic scale. As a result, tourism companies specialising in tours of the universe's wonders ("the flying pyramids of Algol," "the Star serpents of Trialon" and similar products of chance and nature) began to include "The Death Glow of Olifer" in their tours. Sooner or later, this had to end in some unpleasantness. Which it did — after exiting from a jump, the onboard diagnostic of the Empress Mariam issued a high probability of failure of the hyperfield initiator. Strictly speaking, a ship of that class could, without fussing, remain in orbit around the accretion disc and wait for help. The gravitational engines and the powerful antimatter reactor allowed it. Nevertheless, the regulations required arrival as quickly as possible at any stationary base point of the civilian fleet or the Aerospace Forces. The nearest turned out to be Sigma-10. Having made two jumps, the Empress docked at the station and began to await the arrival of an evacuation vessel. She could no longer leave under her own steam. The diagnostic gave a 50% probability of initiator failure after the very next jump. That wasn't a problem; initiator failure couldn't damage the ship. The ship simply lost its hyperdrive.

A couple of days later, another cruise liner arrived. It took on all the passengers, the entire crystal stock from the Empress (even now the price of refined trillium did not allow leaving it just so on a stranded ship; particularly since a behemoth of such size, by regulations, carried a druse of a good hundred standard crystals) and flew off to continue the excursion. The ship itself remained to await the arrival of hyper-tugs. Since only the Aerospace Forces had such equipment, the shipowner started long bureaucratic faffing about with provision of aid. And Max — tried not to miss the positive moments of his post, which, for example, included such a piquant detail as the fact that any ship moored to the fortress, pardon, to the station, automatically came under the jurisdiction and at the full disposal of the station's commander.

As a result, Max dined and supped every day in the ship's mess (the entire crew having remained on the liner to await the tug, and the cook in mastery surpassing by a head most of the chefs in most colonial restaurants... and even in the Metropolis, perhaps), inflicted damage upon the ship's bottomless wine cellars, and soaked in turn in all ten pools scattered across various decks and having different conditions (there were here sulphurous pools resembling the natural hot baths of Japan, and "rainbow lakes" mimicking those on the planet Eden, including "micro-piranhas" that replaced the traditional scrub). The ship's captain looked upon this sybaritism of the station's commander through fingers — he understood perfectly what service on the frontier was.

Just as Max sipped at the exorbitantly priced "moonshine," his secretary appeared in the doorway. James politely waited until the boss had swallowed his firewater, then with a laconic gesture presented his tablet. On the tablet sat yet another request for replenishment of fuel storage. Max applied a finger without looking. No, the antimatter reserve on the station was sufficient, more than sufficient. But there was exactly one crystal in the storeroom. The one which, properly speaking, should have been in the Swift. Removing it and placing it in the store had been the order of von Ritter's predecessor — precisely just before he evaporated half his stupid head with a shot from an army "Smirnov."

James silently nodded and evaporated like a space ghost. As a matter of fact, as had long seemed to Max, it was on him that order and discipline on the station rested.

In sybaritism, drinking and watching interactive psi-pictures of piquant content in the V-hall of the Empress, another week passed. The tug was not coming and, by all appearances, was not planning to come. Max was already seriously thinking of taking the imperial suite as his temporary residence. This thought seemed particularly pleasant when he was yanked from sleep by the buzzer of a combat alarm.

As a matter of fact, that the alarm was not exactly a combat one, Max realised already fully dressed, with his tunic buttoned to the very chin and his fingers lying on the buttons of the combat console. How he had managed within a few minutes to dress and rush to the CP remained a separate mystery. As a matter of fact, only here did his brain switch on at full capacity, jerking his subconscious back and forcing it to catch in the rhythm of the buzzer the desperately familiar sequence: three short roars, three long, three short. Breaking the habitual combination of control touches that opened gun ports and brought missile cellars to full combat readiness, Max jammed down the intercom button and rasped, "Cancel the alarm, commander on the bridge. Senior duty officer — report situation. Information operator — summary to the main screen!"

— SOS signal, Commander! Coming from sector Sigma-10-2-37. Vector 10.

— Singularity? Second time this month? Is there comms?

— Comms established. Ready to put on screen.

— Do it.

The screen blinked, then displayed a bridge resembling the combat citadel of an old "C"-class cruiser. The bridge, however, was a background for a face grey with the emotions being experienced.

— Commander of outpost Sigma-10? Captain Leonov on the line.

— Captain-1R Ritter. Listening.

— Captain, we have very big problems. I have a cruise ship here. The reactor is running away. On board — 5000 children. Girls. From a parochial school. The reactor field auto-stabilisation system has gone. In effect it has already exploded. The chief energy officer has been holding it manually, from the inside, for twenty minutes already. He is in a heavy suit, but I'm afraid even to imagine the dose he has already caught...

The Star of Altair was, for the colony Volna, the lead ship. No, it was clear that on any old colony, and especially in the Metropolis, plenty would have been found who could have identified in the brand-new cruise liner (only thirty years on the lines) the hull of a "C"-class cruiser of the Old Zindan Empire Dynasty. On the other hand — the military base implied always high reliability and repairability. Well, and the hull and everything else had been refitted for the needs of the owner tourism company.

In effect, the Star was the pride of the agrarian colony. A kind of sign of her admission to the ranks of the large and grown-up colonies possessing their own civilian fleet. The inhabitants were not even particularly troubled by the fact that half of the company's shares belonged to the wife of the perpetual and hereditary President of the colony, Anastasis Arnov. Be that as it may, the company "Harvest" and her Star were the pride of the whole planet.

Therefore, when the main prize at the contest of the best religious staging for the Great Orthodox Ecumenical Church was named as a cruise through the star wonders aboard the Star of Altair, the struggle among the planet's Sunday schools developed in earnest. It ended with neighbouring schools beginning to cooperate, in hopes of crushing their competitors by the size and epic scale of the staging. So it came to pass that the residents of the district of Zlatopolye fielded a team of as many as 5000 girls. Their performance, "The Lord Lights the Stars," moved the President himself.

To see the winners off into flight, a whole crowd gathered. Parents wept for joy. Girls squealed. On the field — the girls who had taken other places sobbed aloud. Certificates, education grants — all of this faded before the prospect of touching the breath of the Creator personally. And aboard the Star, which seemed the summit of civilisation and luxury. Volna was far from the wealthiest of worlds.

The flight went as everyone had expected. It was the most unforgettable month in the history of those girls. They saw myriads of wonders. They spent all their time in front of the observation windows, soaking in every wonder, from the enormous stone monoliths-pyramids eternally running in figure-eights around the binary stars of Algol to the very velvety blackness of infinite interstellar space. And what hymns they sang to the Creator every day! Perhaps in a month they were to return forever to their land and never again see the stellar magnificence. Most of them awaited an ordinary life married to a farmer or agricultural-machinery engineer — a modest life of a housewife, flowing in caring for the home, the children, the husband... But the stars had forever scorched their shining trail on the soul of each of them... Those who awaited them on Volna would, I think, be very surprised at the changes that had befallen these girls — now stars burned in their own eyes.

Just before the very departure homeward, the road to which was to take a couple of days — the girls learned of the last surprise. "Harvest" had saved the highlight of the programme. The Star, on the way home, was making a small detour: They were being given a singularity. A black hole. A most beautiful and most unusual phenomenon. A place where time and matter themselves ceased to exist. No, let us be honest — the gathered flock was unlikely to grasp well what was being talked about. The girls were assembled from ages roughly 10 to 16, and by the particularities of education on Volna they perceived the Galaxy more with the heart than with the mind, but...

Already in orbit, slightly above the plane of the accretion disc, the on-board engineer, Simon Crez, decided to prophylactically dump the charge accumulated on the kinetic emitters of the force field. As he commented on it to the captain, who was indignant at the initiative: "And what? The singularity pulls all the rocks to itself, it's clean and nice here, and I only need a couple of minutes for the reboot!" Alas, fate is very fond of vanishingly small chances, as is rightly noted in "Pratchett's Law."

A cruise liner does not have the armour plates of a cruiser. A cruise liner is built as is convenient, not as is efficient. That is precisely why a cruise liner with its protective field's kinetic component removed — is enough done in by even a small meteorite. Especially if it, by fate's whim, lands in the control block of the antimatter reactor.

On the other hand — the peculiarity of military vessels is that their reactors are made calculated for the ship being subjected to nuclear torpedoes and lasers. And also — for the fact that in war, heroism is always possible.

As practice showed, the combination of a core from a military ship and a wrapping from a civilian one — gives rise to heroism even in civilian service.

Unlike the civilian, the military reactor, deprived of its containment-field control loop, did not blow at once. At first it honestly attempted to dampen the reaction. Alas — that did not succeed. But the senior energy officer, Semyon Grigoryev, had enough time to leap into the heavy protective suit and break through behind the biological shielding, to the reserve reactor console. The old man locked the door behind him. In any case — he was the only one who knew this system well enough.

As a result, for twenty minutes now he had been holding, in effect, an already exploded reactor with barely perceptible, micron movements of his fingers across the control panel. What dose he had already caught and how much more he would catch — he tried not to think about. The irony was that his own granddaughter, Iroska, had not made it onto the ship, having gone down with a cold the day before departure. But on the ship there were another five thousand such "Iroskas." Which meant his own life now meant nothing. And the senior energy officer, with some tenth sense, with his very skin scorched by radiation, sensed every fluctuation of the field and parried it. The reactor lived, taking life from the man. There remained only one question — how many hours of life he had left. In effect, exactly as many remained for all the passengers of the unfortunate Star.

...Max slashed the air sharply with his palm.

— They're idiots! How could they send a ship with children to a singularity without even thinking about safety?

— Fools, sir. But in general, they did think. Who knew that the field would be temporarily removed and that in that moment they would catch a meteorite?

— Damn, and using the lifeboats won't work either...

— Affirmative, sir. The lifeboats will be pulled into the accretion disc, where they will be destroyed before reaching the boundary of the horizon. Chemical engines won't be sufficient to hold against the surface of the gravity well. Besides, the lifeboats have no force fields — the passengers will be killed by the hard X-rays from the disc...

— Time-time-time!!!! Damn! What a choice. Is the crew already refitting all the spaces on the Swift?

— Affirmative, sir. The on-board mechanic is sure we can take aboard about 2000 people, sir.

— Why so few... Damn... And the Empress... No, one crystal will certainly be enough to take that behemoth on a couple of jumps...

— Affirmative, sir, even more — if the drive is in tune, then even five. Only there's a risk that after the very first jump her initiator will burn out. And she will be left drifting halfway to the children.

Max bit his index finger of his clenched fist until it hurt. The choice was diabolically simple and diabolically complex: To save 2000 with a guarantee... Or to try to save 5000. To save 5000... Or to save none... And the odds were too great for "Pratchett's Law" to trigger...

Something salty trickled down his lips. Max realised with surprise that he was weeping soundlessly. He wanted to shoot himself, just so as not to make such a decision. On the other hand — there was a sharp understanding that this would only make the situation worse. No, if his death would save those children — they'd be carting him to the morgue already. But... but here something else was required... Yes, of course, to the SOS signal patrol cruisers of Earth's Aerospace Forces were now racing from all corners. Moreover, learning that it was about children — ships of the Caliphate of Altmuru had hurried, and even the wondrous, art-piece-like ships of the Betans, with all their transcendent speed...

But the closest of all was him. And the chance to arrive before the old energy officer died — was only his. That is not to mention that one had to manage to carry out the evacuation as well.

And every minute pressed in...

Twenty minutes later, the Empress detached from the station's side. The crew hurriedly prepared the spaces to receive the children.

Dozens of people tensely awaited the jump. Now or never.

In the depths of the hyper-core control post, the chief engineer, praying to all the gods he knew, smoothly depressed the initiator launch key. From the moment the situation became known he and his team had been doing everything possible to make the initiator survive two jumps.

Just two jumps, that would be enough.

The Empress was one of the largest and most luxurious ships of the Metropolis. A symbol of the Empire's flowering. Her engines would, without problem, allow her to drive a respectable-sized asteroid around the entire solar system. Her force fields, worthy of a dreadnought, would have withstood passage through the prominence of a solar-class star and easily protected her from the highest intensity of gamma radiation.

She didn't care about conditions in a singularity system.

She simply had to make the jump. The theory of probability had to fail just one time. The coin had to fall on the correct side.

Yes, there the ship would find itself without a hyperdrive. But that was no longer scary. On board — a place would be found for every child. For every crew member. And then they could drift even for years, though in fact — help would arrive within hours.

Max put his hand on the holster of his blaster and with his thumb flipped the cover loop off the stud. The floor beneath his feet vibrated barely perceptibly. The surrounding world — began slightly to double. The jump field — enveloped the enormous ship...

And the next instant — on the observation screen, in place of the star Olifer, swirled an enormous nebula and burned a bright, bright star.

Max felt a strange chill run down his spine. It was the face of a dead body. The star had long since exploded and become a black hole, which turned out to be a trap for tourists, and from here one could still see the last days of its life...

It was precisely there that the Empress would jump in two hours, after the accumulators recharged.

However — now the gazes of all present on the bridge were riveted to the comm-link.

Several seconds passed, which people perceived as full-blown hours. But then the comm-link coughed with the voice of the chief engineer.

The graveyard words filled the air of the bridge:

"the initiator... has burned out"...

...— Looks like we have no chances left — the captain smiled joylessly when the comm screen went dark. — The Empress reported that their hyperdrive has failed.

The first officer drew a shuddering breath. The astrogator sobbed and lowered her face into her hands. The condemned ship slowly drifted on the "outskirts" of the plasma generated by the rotation around the black hole accretion disc. People felt sorry for their lives, sorry for the ship... But most painful of all was for the children. Whom they had been entrusted with. And whom they had not protected. Help clearly was not in time. By the most optimistic calculations, Grigoryev could hold the field for another five hours at most. The nearest rescue ship — was due in eight. The lifeboats by that moment would be in the raging plasma element, and that under the condition that they had not burned earlier in the flames of the annihilation of the out-of-control reactor packed with tens of kilograms of antimatter. However, the hard gamma radiation too — did not leave a chance for those saved on the lifeboats...

The senior teacher of the school, who as the one responsible for the children was also present on the bridge — rose.

— I shall go to the girls, gentlemen. The children are not yet aware of the situation. And I do not think it will be better for anyone if they realise they are doomed. Nevertheless — one must prepare their souls. Tell me, is death in the flame of annihilation painless?

— You will have no time to feel anything. — the senior security officer seemed absent, his thoughts somewhere else. He had given this answer on full "auto-pilot." The captain inwardly snorted. Just before this trip — the officer had been wed. Now, evidently, he was rerunning scenes from his and his wife's honeymoon in his head. One supposes — not the worst way out when there is very little time left until death.

— That's... good. The Almighty, evidently, wished to receive our children into His Kingdom. And took care that it should be as easy as possible. Well then. Then we shall meet death singing hymns to Him. — Sister Gretel turned on the spot and walked out, the hem of her long skirt softly rustling along the floor.

The captain followed the small woman with the gaze of admiration for what had turned out to be enormous fortitude and courage. Being an atheist, he had always slightly envied people endowed with faith. Foolish or not, but they always had a prop for the spirit. They simply — were never left in complete solitude before the Universe.

— Gentlemen, I want to remind you that on our ship there is a not-bad bar. Strictly speaking, it is intended for tourists, but in connection with the extraordinary situation... In short — first officer, give the order. The only condition — drink, but don't get plastered. Drunk astronauts in a critical situation — that is disgusting.

— One moment... — the security officer emerged from his meditation and, raising a finger upward as in a classroom, with a hoarse and barely audible voice tried to attract attention. In another situation, he would probably not even have been noticed, but now the nerves of all were so sharpened that the heads of all present turned to him.

— Yes, Alexei? — the captain raised an eyebrow in surprise.

— Sir. Our energy reactor is unstable, correct?

— Obviously.

— The automatic reactor field control block is damaged. This does not affect any other components. Meanwhile, thanks to the feat of Semyon Pavlovich — we haven't even been left without energy. So we can still actively work the engines, we have light, gravity... As a matter of fact — the ship is alive.

— And?

— One moment, sir, if I may. I'm trying to formulate my idea clearly. You see, in training school I studied, naturally, not the design of modern ships. But I learned some details. The hyperdrive core — is a completely separate core from the energy one, correct? Well, I mean, this block of crystals — is an independent construction. Right after exit from hyper — it automatically begins to charge. As soon as the crystals accumulate critical charge, which takes two hours, the device enters standby mode and idles, until the captain orders a jump...

— And we'd been in the system for almost eight hours, until we caught the meteorite! — the captain caught on to the safety officer's thought. — Why, this might work! Only... at the moment of the jump — most likely Semyon will lose control of the reactor... So right after the jump we could explode... Force compartment, give me a line!

On the holographic display appeared the impenetrable visor of a heavy suit. The image was being transmitted from the camera of the reserve console, the voice — from the internal microphone.

— I'm listening... captain... — Semyon's voice was muffled and somewhat detached... The energy officer was in such a meditative state that he seemed to be personally seeing the fields and every passing erg of energy through them.

— Semyon, tell me, how long can you hold the field if we now perform a jump?

— A jump.... But the rescuers are on the way to us?!

— They were. Their drive cut out one jump from us.

— So that's how it is.... — the old man sank into deep contemplation. By the movements of the powerful shoulder-pieces — all this time he hadn't ceased to conjure with the fields. — Well then. After the jump, most likely, I will completely lose stability. But not at once. You'll have about an hour. I'd even orient by half an hour. More I'm not able to give....

....Max with difficulty tore his head from the ozone-scented pillow. His body was chained to a hospital cot by soft but sturdy restraints. Out of the corner of his eye, the commander noticed a hand with a syringe being withdrawn from his neck.

— I'm glad you're awake, Herr Ritter. — the Empress's chief doctor appeared in his field of vision. — I barely managed to knock you out with a neuroparalyser when you nearly shot yourself.

— I shall shoot myself anyway, as soon as you release me. — Max's tone was filled with lifelessness like an ancient and forgotten crypt....

— Well, commander, there's a chance that won't be necessary — the doctor smiled at him. — Just now the Star contacted us. They want to try to jump to us...

Sellers of Love

...They first appeared on TV screens, eliciting no small amount of laughter and jokes on the Net. It was January 2020 outside, the Second Great Crisis had just ended, so reviving entrepreneurs had flooded everything with advertising.

All the fresher and more amusing looked, during prime time, a twenty-second white screen with black writing in large font: "We sell love. Telephone 8-800-985-008-75-75."

Understandably, this did not go unnoticed. The Net at first burst out in jokes on the theme of legalising prostitution. The impulse from the Net put, as usual, the apparatus of government into motion, which resulted in an FAS inspection of the firm. The inspection ended in nothing — that is, the FAS found no violations, though they did demand the aggressiveness of the advertising be reduced. In its "weakened" form, the advert quickly dissolved into a stream of "Auntie Asyas" and "Hochland" cheeses, so the blogosphere serenely forgot about this amusing phenomenon. The "Big Nine" conference had just begun in Moscow, so all the media switched to skirmishes with anti-globalists, terror attacks prevented by the "civilian police," and much else that accompanies every city upon which falls so honorary and burdensome a duty as hosting an international forum.

All the greater was my surprise when, on one warm summer morning, my brother who had dropped in for breakfast, inquired:

— Eug, you know, I've decided to go to the "sellers."

— Sellers of what, Nick? Drugs? Organs?

— "Love" — my brother was extremely laconic, while I had to spend a couple of extra seconds remembering those clowns.

— Er-er-er... "Sellers of Love"?! You're off to a brothel? Little brother, I can easily help you find a far cheaper and certainly no less quality "escort"! Just don't tell Olya I tipped you off.

— What have whores got to do with it! I've already found several hundred mentions in journals. They actually find your other half! I've been thinking about them for some time. As a matter of fact, remember I suddenly turned down buying a new "rig"?

— Yeah, deserves a medal!

— Not funny. It's just that their services cost two grand, and they don't even consider credit. Had to save up a bit.

It seemed Nick had taken the matter seriously. I thought briefly. In principle, my little brother, being a brilliant computer specialist and professional gamer, who had represented Russia at numerous international competitions and even been invited to the new Olympic reserve, suffered nonetheless from a total lull on the personal front. The word "lull," however, doesn't even quite reflect the vacuum-purity that obtained in his affairs of the heart. In my memory, the first and last girl with whom he had dated for as long as half a year was during his third year at MIREA.

At the same time, purely for the historical record, I must note that with these computer types, the females are of a fairly specific phenotype, so that even me, a great fan of the fairer sex, would be brought to a state of extreme concentration by her presence, with the effectiveness of an ice-cold shower.

All this was the more odd because my little brother differed from me in appearance not too greatly, while on my own front there were traditionally perennial battles being fought on every available direction and in every existing medium.

Incidentally, the female representatives of my journalism faculty at Moscow State University also compared favourably to the MIREA programmer-sysadmin types.

Be that as it may, it seemed loneliness and rare bought sex had bored Nick to death. Bored him so much that he had decided to invest a chunk of money in some incomprehensible adventurers. No, of course, Nick earned decently and could afford it (he could have earned more if he hadn't been changing gadgets every week), but in this case he had even decided to put off buying a new, ultra-powerful virtual-reality rig from one of the leading American companies. His affair, of course, but as the elder brother, I simply could not allow him to pour money into obvious "horns and hooves" without a fight.

— Nick, why do you even need this? Let's get ourselves together now, drive to the centre for some clothes, and from there crash the clubs? Or, alternatively, sign you up right now for some dating site?

— Eug, I've tried. None of it works. More precisely, none of it is what I need. Empty-heads from a club, or teenagers from the latest "love-cosmos"? Why would I want them?

— And you think the "sellers" will be better?

— THEY WILL! — I knew that fanatic gleam in his eyes and conviction in his tone. It meant that Nick had taken the bit between his teeth and would now go to any lengths, even if he had to sell a kidney or walk around the whole earth on foot. — Eug, this works! I've read loads of reviews! You can't imagine — people find what they've been looking for their entire lives! Not some random person they'll then spend their lives suffering with and trying to find common ground. They actually find a person's other half.

Understand, I don't need just any girl. I want exactly her, mine. As beautiful as I want, fond of computers and comics. One for whom "TF3" or "Mortal Arena" wouldn't be an empty sound. I want to watch "Babylon" and "Mass Effect" with her, not "Pretty Woman"! The "sellers" guarantee the result.

— Well then, your money. So what do you want from me?

— Me? Well, you see... I'm a bit nervous. No, not quite that... Just somehow... In short — will you keep me company?

— In the sense of finding myself a half too?

— In the sense of simply going along with me to the office when I go to sign up.

The office was located in "Kutuzov-Plaza" on the eponymous avenue. It occupied as much as three floors in a nine-storey business centre. I noted the scale. We went up to the sixth floor and found ourselves in a hall with slightly subdued lighting, panelled in ash covered with dark varnish. Overall, the atmosphere was rather... intimate, perhaps.

Along the walls stood cosy capsule-armchairs, as if specially enclosing the seated person within themselves. A wonderful opportunity to be alone with oneself even when the waiting hall is full of people.

Nevertheless, at the moment there weren't all that many people. Nick and I, a tall and skinny nerdoid with a pimply face, a plump shaggy red-haired girl in an off-puttingly pink dress, a woman of around forty in a business suit, and a contemporary of mine — an emo girl.

Behind the counter sat a pretty, or rather, frankly even beautiful receptionist. She greeted us politely and warmly, took down Nick's initials, and then suggested we take seats, listen to music and relax.

Naturally I would have been the last of idiots if I had let such a chance pass. So my brother was sent to languish in an armchair, while I cautiously but insistently moved on the not-too-busy beauty at reception.

To my enormous surprise, I was simply ignored. More precisely, very politely and cordially invited to take a free chair and wait. Naturally, the leading journalist of "Health of Man," Eugene Steshevsky, simply had no right to retreat!

Thus, I ruffled my plumage, hoisted my tail, puffed out my crop... In short, set for victory, I went in for a fresh assault... And ran into the receptionist's sorrowful gaze.

— Mister Steshevsky, I have a loved one. Please, don't trouble yourself. I'm not interested in any side communication. Believe me. I think your brother will soon understand me. And now, please, take your chair. Your brother will be called, but I'm afraid you'll have to wait for him here.

The queue gradually moved, people were summoned one by one. What was interesting, I noticed a pattern. To all appearances, each arriving person was kept in the hall for exactly 30 minutes. There were several meeting rooms, but they invited people in strict order of arrival. The turn finally came to my no-good little brother...

— 31 days?! Have they lost their minds?! In our age of universal Net and universal databases? Nightmare!

— Eug, calm down! That's time for reflection. And then, do you really think an ideal is so easily found? — after visiting the meeting room my brother was surprisingly calm and at peace. I don't know what they talked about in there, but Nick looked like a Dalai Lama who had gorged on rice.

I cast another glance at my brother's tranquil expression, after which I mentally cursed and stamped the accelerator. My new-generation "A-mobile" gave a soft meow and leapt into a window that had opened in the traffic flow.

On August 8th we again stood in the reception. The receptionist, the same young woman, warmly greeted my brother and gave me a friendly nod. This time we were let past reception. Behind the receptionist's back a double-leaved door opened, and we walked into a small cosy office. At the desk sat a young man in a business suit. We exchanged the customary greetings, and then the young man, who turned out to be Igor Nikolayevich, turned to Nick:

— Nick, we are ready to fulfil your request. Say "yes," and behind this door — Igor waved his hand behind himself — your destiny awaits. Nevertheless, we perfectly understand how frightening it is to decide to hand over your fate to others' hands. You have every right to refuse. You will be refunded your money, less a quarter of the sum as payment for our costs.

— Tell me, Igor, do people often refuse? — I inquired.

— I'm glad you asked. No. Practically no one refuses... Nevertheless, we are simply obliged to warn that such an option exists. So, your decision, Nick?

My brother sighed heavily, helplessly glanced at me, as it seemed to me, then raised his head and silently set off toward the far door. Igor nodded understandingly and pressed a button on the desk. The doors parted, revealing behind them a room flooded with even white light, and closed behind my no-good kin.

— And how long will this be? — I inquired.

— This? Hm, for life, I should think — Igor replied. — And if you mean the question of how long you should wait for your brother, the answer is simple: don't wait. He'll be with us for about a day. Loads of our internal procedures, acquaintance. We'll deliver him home ourselves, don't worry.

I next crossed paths with Nick only a week later. No, obviously, I called him the following day. Nick was joyful and excited, but tried to wrap up the conversation as quickly as possible. Apparently he was very busy with something. I too had a deadline approaching, so I had to urgently submit material, and I hadn't even started. Plus my discord with Olga began. She had cottoned on to a couple of my dalliances, and although I had never left direct evidence, she was packing her things and getting ready to ship out. Sadly, even an exorbitantly priced diamond necklace did not radically change the situation. It was announced to me that she needed to spend some time alone and think about whether she needed a relationship with a "glamour scumbag." For the scumbag I wasn't particularly offended, since I was guilty, but experience whispered to me that this was the end of our relationship.

So, I broke loose to my brother only the following weekend.

At first, no one answered the bell for a long time. Then the door was opened by some strange lad. To be honest, I didn't recognise my brother at once. Nick had lost quite a lot of weight, taken on a more athletic build. Even his face had changed somewhat, become more finished, more masculine perhaps.

Even his voice had acquired greater depth.

My brother greeted me in a robe and with a cigarette between his teeth. This particularly surprised me — Nick had never smoked and could not understand what people saw in it. Obviously I had distracted him from something, but nonetheless he had politeness enough to invite me in.

In the old flat everything was new. Generally, Nick had usually not been particularly fussy. Functional furniture from IKEA, hardware strewn here and there and half-assembled PCs, a virtual reality interface with suspension rig sticking up in the middle of the room. He cleaned the flat too only occasionally.

Today I was met by an immaculately spotless flat. The furniture had been completely replaced. In the hall, scrubbed to a shine and repainted, hung decorative pots with flowers. In short, one felt comfort and... a WOMAN'S HAND?!

— Nicky, darling, who's there? — sounded from the bedroom.

— It's my brother, baby, come out, get acquainted!

In response to Nick's invitation, from the bedroom emerged a red-haired plump beauty, wrapped in a single sheet. A lush mane of radically red hair flowed down her back to her firm bottom, clearly discernible even through the sheet. Damn, even I didn't often manage to hook a beauty like this!

My brother and his girl, evidently, had decided to fully shatter my templates: finding themselves beside each other, they merged in a long and passionate kiss. While they snogged, I had time to grow thoroughly bored and decided to look around a bit. To my enormous surprise, in the study I found not only no freely-lying hardware, but no virtual terminal at all. Now there stood, just as in mine, an expensive and good all-in-one, rather than a "handmade" supercomputer. This change especially astonished me.

— Hey, little bro, what happened to your gaming station???

— What? Ah, I hauled it off to work. At home, the computer's only needed to go online, check news, answer a couple of emails, watch a film. And I can practise at work — I tidied the system up so well that in any case there's a ton of free time.

— Er-er-er... Nick, but you used to play days on end?!

— Why would I need that now? I have my Katyusha! No time for games, and frankly no desire either, brother!

I was floored. The man standing before me was my brother... And was not him!

He had matured considerably, grown more serious, more collected. And at the same time he remained the same Nick. At least, on the bedroom TV screen I had managed to catch the closing frames of the first "Mass Effect." But why on earth he had started smoking remained a mystery to me. To my direct question Nick himself explained that he had simply wanted to keep company with his beloved.

Katya turned out to be a rare merry-laughing thing, though indeed with the bad habit of smoking. And in general, over the couple of hours I spent with the lads, I was touched. They understood each other from half a word, half a glance. Often they even talked half-and-half. One says the first part of a sentence, the other finishes it. This was frightening and at the same time alluring.

Be that as it may, the kids were happy, and I for the first time thought about the fact that I'd turned thirty this year...

— Good day, Mister Steshevsky. Glad to see you again. Would you like to buy love?

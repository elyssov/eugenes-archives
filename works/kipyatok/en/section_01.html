Chapter 1

After ten minutes and five scorched fingers — I got bored of boiling water in an eggshell. I tossed the shells, went to the kitchen and grabbed a dried vobla. Brought it back and gave it a good crunch. Then I took the lid off the aquarium and lowered the fish in. The vobla bobbed gently "on the waves."

The infant watched my manipulations with decidedly un-childlike curiosity.

Paying no attention to the observer — I sprinkled dried planaria into the aquarium. The planaria slowly spiralled, drifting to the bottom. I dragged a fishing rod I'd spotted earlier from the balcony and a lace from a ski boot. Tied the lace to the rod and cast it into the aquarium.

This, the infant could no longer endure. He sat up sharply and proclaimed in a deep bass:

— Tá mé níos sine ná na crainn a fhásann i gclós mo thí, ach den chéad uair feicim fear ag breith ar iasc marbh — le dul i ngleic le bréagán*

— Gotcha, fairy?! — I shoved my pistol under the infant's nose. The infant fell onto its back and let out a revolting squeal, kicking its legs.

— Nice try. You broke character. Normal babies scream two octaves higher.

The infant fell silent. Then raised its head and looked at me with adult, angry eyes.

— Cut the comedy, fairy. Otherwise I'll have to kill you.

— I am immortal, fomor. And if you attempt to harm me — I shall never return the child! — the infant sat up and crossed its little arms over its chest.

— Are you really so immortal, fairy? Will you really survive even if I wound you with iarann fuar?

The fairy winced as if from a toothache. Either my accent bothered him (well, my Irish is terrible!) or the mention of cold iron.

— Do not speak our language, fomor! You are not given to grasp its melody. Where would you even get iarann fuar**?

— Fairy, you astonish me. Do you really think we're too stupid to replicate what our ancestors did? In here — I waggled the GSh-18 without taking it off the "infant" — are eighteen bullets of cold iron. And I shall put them into you one by one, until the glamour dissipates and I see your true form. And then I'll apply whatever is worst for your true shape. Here, have a look. — I pulled a cartridge from my pocket and tossed it to the fairy.

The infant reflexively caught it. In that same instant, it howled with an inhuman voice. Its hand immediately grew heavy and elongated, and then the rest of it changed too. Instead of an "infant," in the cradle sat a wizened little old man with an unpleasant face and yellow eyes with vertical pupils. The old man wore an emerald doublet and striped stockings with black buckled shoes.

— A leipreachán! Mother of — ! Why on my shift???!!! — I'll admit, I was ready to howl myself. Getting rid of these little pests came down, essentially, to fast-talking them. And shooting was basically useless. Killing one (and I could do it easily) solved nothing — the baby replaced by the changeling would never be returned. The problem was that the entity you were meant to fast-talk was, by its very nature, one of the greatest hagglers in existence!

— Why the sour face, fomor? — the creature inquired snidely — bet if a young alv were in my place, you'd react differently?

— Make a bet with a leiprea — to hell with you and your language — with a leprechaun? You really think I'm that stupid? — I holstered the pistol in my shoulder rig.

In truth, from the moment the disguise dropped, this particular fairy posed no threat to me personally. Had a "young alv" been in his place — I'd likely be facing evisceration by "sun blades" or getting fried by one of the Tuatha Dé Danann's spells. Alvs are like that. Completely unhinged.

Leprechauns didn't care for tangling with big folk — or fomors, as they called us — in open combat. And it would have been pointless anyway. In principle, in a fair, open fight, a leprechaun was no stronger than a five-year-old child.

But God forbid you enter into any kind of deal with one... That's when, according to the convoluted fairy laws, a leprechaun gained complete power over you. And within the framework of the deal, his magic was stronger than any alv's... To avoid painting the world only black, I should note that some people actually managed to make quite a decent profit this way. Where do you think John D. Rockefeller's instinct and financial luck came from?

The fairy folk, in and of themselves, are not villains. The bastards simply amuse themselves. And if helping you doesn't fall outside the concept of entertainment, why not play the good "fairy godmother"? The thing is — fairy folk mostly enjoy WICKED pranks. When one of the fomors suffers. And so, even if they're helping you, it most likely means they're counting on royally screwing someone else in the process.

I put on my HUD visor and called Central with an eye movement.

— Guys, I've got a situation with a leprechaun. The parents are still unconscious, so... But I'm not planning to sit with this scumbag all day. I need a negotiator.

— Copy, Mark. But afraid we can't help right now. No free negotiators available. Before this Beltane of theirs, the fairy folk have gone absolutely mental...

Mark — that's me. Zarnitsky, Mark Aurelius Alexandrovich. And you still ask why I hate my own father???? This ancient-Rome-obsessed bookworm couldn't think of anything better than to name me — like that!

— So what are we going to do, leprechaun? — I looked at the little wretch. The leprechaun visibly perked up.

— I think, fomor, we shall seek common ground and negotiate! Don't you agree?

— Well, look — you could voluntarily return the baby. That would count as cooperation, and we wouldn't have to hunt you. How's that sound?

— Foolish fomor — you should understand yourself that even if I very much wanted to return this child to you, I could not go against fairy law! No, we must strike a deal. On my side — the child. And what's on yours?

— Oh, not this... — I grabbed my head. — God save me from making deals with fairy folk.

— Well, no deal — no baby — the leprechaun pouted. — And anyway, I should be going. You've exposed me, so this guise is no longer available to me. Nothing to catch here — nobody's going to give a mother's milk breast to a plain leprechaun who doesn't look like a baby ('Bloody pervert,' I thought), so, I'll be off. And how you explain to the parents where the baby is — that's not my headache.

— Sit! — I barked. — How about we try guessing your name.

— Oh, please! I refuse to believe there's anyone on Earth who doesn't know all leprechauns are called Patrick — the dwarf grinned.

— No, well, I understand it won't be easy... But I had to try. Maybe you'd swap for some trinket?

— Did you hear what you just said? — the leprechaun inquired.

— Yes, I realise, not the best offer.

— Or perhaps you could give me power over yourself for a single day? And the child will be right here! — the wretch inquired in his sweetest voice.

— Not a chance. AT BEST I'd find myself in a sleazy brothel, pumped full of every substance known to man! ('No chemicals! Only natural ingredients!' the leprechaun cried in an offended tone.)

— Well, I don't know what to suggest. Maybe you could dance the 'Blackbird' for a whole day? Or manage to surprise me even more than your fishing?

— Maybe... — I murmured, absently threading between my fingers a narrow, satin ribbon of a delicate blue colour, as if glowing from within. The leprechaun's eyes locked onto my amusement.

— And what sort of string is that you have? — the fairy inquired, trying not to show particular interest.

— Oh, nothing much. Found it once in an old house. We were looking for a ghost there.

— Could you let me have a look, fomor?

— I could even give it to you. Or rather, trade for the baby.

— Oh, no. But do let me look — the dwarf greedily thrust out his hand.

— By all means — I held out to the greedy gnome a length of Gleipnir***.

— Ha! What a beautiful ribbon! — the leprechaun enthused — And so soft, so tender... I shan't be returning it. You didn't stipulate a return when you handed it to me! — the dwarf cackled with satisfaction.

— Well, I expected nothing less. Sjakler! Fanget!****

In a split second, the hitherto harmless ribbon, like a snake, bound the entire leprechaun like a premium sausage — ready for the smokehouse. The dwarves, in their day, had truly done fine work.

— Hey! What's this about! Stop it! Take this off me immediately! I'll simply vanish and you'll never find the child, fomor! (Here the dwarf closed his eyes and strained so hard I thought he'd burst. However, vanishing didn't work. Understandable. Originally, the chain Gleipnir had been forged for Fenrir. Our leprechaun rather paled in comparison.)

— Greed! That's what destroys! Not drink, not women. Greed! — Now tell me, why on earth did you need this ribbon? And now — you're entirely in my power. By your own laws — I am the aggrieved party, since you tried to take my magical artefact. And here's the result. So — you're my prisoner. Whatever shall I do with you?

— Let me go? — the dwarf smiled as charmingly as he could manage.

— I'd be glad to, perhaps, but I can't... It's the job...

— How about a pot of gold? — the leprechaun asked with interest.

— Also not interesting. I know your prank. Afterwards, at best, I'd be scrubbing myself clean for weeks.

— Fine, fine, fomor! I'll return your baby! Happy?

— Not just return it. You'll give me your promise that you will never again appear within the borders of my country.

— Fomor!

— Don't like it? — I yawned and stretched — the ribbon can hold you in exactly one position indefinitely. And imagine — your nose starts to itch. Speaking of which. — I briskly scanned the room, then resolutely walked to the parents' bed and rummaging in the nearest pillow, fished out an enormously long rooster feather, a wonderful shade of brown.

— FOMOR!!!!!! DON'T YOU DARE!!!!

— Don't dare what? — I inquired, seating myself opposite the cradle and gazing affectionately at the fairy. — Don't dare this? — I ran the feather under MY OWN nose. Knowing the suggestibility of leprechauns — the result was merely a matter of time.

— Ah... Ah... Ah-ah-ah-ahh... My nose... It itches... Fomor — scratch my nose... Fomor, you fiend! You torturer! Release me! Release me and I'll grant any wish! Untie my hands... Or at least my head, let me rub it on something... I can't... I CAN'T!!!!

The leprechaun was tossing, bulging, squishing and sausaging — all at once and a bit of each separately. Meanwhile, the magic ribbon provided complete immobilisation of the victim, so the poor fellow truly couldn't even rub his nose on anything.

I went to the kitchen and made myself tea. The screams from the room already resembled death rattles. By the time the tea was finished — the screams had progressed to hysterical howling.

— So then, do you promise that when I release you, you will immediately return the child, and after that promptly and without delay leave the territory of our country?

— Yes! Anything! Yes!

— Say the word "I swear"!

— Mionnaím!!!*****

I reached out and touched the ribbon. The ribbon slithered up my sleeve and wove itself into its usual beautiful pattern on my forearm.

The gasping leprechaun, clawing at his entire face with his nails, rose to his feet. Having caught his breath, he glared at me ferociously and vanished with a pop...

— Thank you! Thank you, Doctor! — The happy parents were cradling in their arms the contentedly snuffling and smacking infant, who bore absolutely no resemblance to the creature I had found when I first arrived on the call. The pistol, along with most of the "harness," lay in my "doctor's bag," and the standard-issue "armoured three-piece" was invisible beneath the white coat.

Cases of changelings don't occur too often, at least in our latitudes. Nevertheless — we carefully monitor doctor visits regarding changes in infant behaviour. And naturally — we try to respond promptly, dispatching professional negotiators to the scene. Sometimes, of course, it pays to let the enemy's guard down by pretending there are no negotiators and he'll be dealing with an ordinary field operative.

And just now — another such case was behind us. The parents had been given to understand that I, as a doctor, had administered the right injections and cured the infection that had turned their baby into a little monster. The baby itself — returned. The leprechaun-changeling, who had wanted to secure himself a "milk supply" — banished to the Beyond, whence he had crawled...

And only on the approach to the entrance of the underground base that served as home to our outfit did I remember that I'd never fished the vobla out of the aquarium.

* I am older than the trees that grow in my courtyard, yet for the first time I see a man catching a dead fish — with a toy tackle (Old Irish)

** Cold iron — an item made of iron that was never touched by fire during manufacture. Typically achieved through repeated cold forging of the original ingot, without heating in a furnace. Considered the most lethal metal for any supernatural or undead creature.

*** Gleipnir — the chain of the gods, forged by the dwarves from the footfall of cats, the voice of fish, the beards of women and other such impossible ingredients. Created and enchanted to bind one of Loki's chthonic offspring.

**** Chain! Bind! (Norwegian)

***** I swear! (Old Irish)

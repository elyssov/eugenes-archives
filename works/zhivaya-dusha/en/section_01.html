Chapter 1

The corpse of the famous artist, Ivan Sokolov, was tied to the back of a monumental chair. The victim's body was so drenched in blood that the colour of the flannel robe in which the servant of the muses was clad was completely unreadable. However — the blood bore only an indirect relation to the master's death, as it did not belong to Ivan. The not-entirely-voluntary "donors" were located in the same room, evenly and portionally distributed around its perimeter. By the stumps, I could roughly count six distinct bodies. Blood from the massacre had splattered the paintings hanging on the walls, a couple of unfinished statues. It had even reached the master's new work — an enormous canvas, placed on an easel in the far corner of the hall. Fortunately — the painting was covered with a heavy cloth, so there was hope that the blood stains would not soil Sokolov's final work.

Beside the easel stood a small marble pedestal, evidently for a model, and a heap of clothes also smeared with blood. Behind a partition of shelving units, on which gathered dust various tools useful in art — lay directly on the floor an enormous "four-person" mattress, made up with rumpled bedding. Strictly speaking, Sokolov, at thirty-four, was a famous and highly sought-after artist, enjoying great popularity among the country's political and financial elite, so he had both a country house and a not-insignificant city flat... But, according to all his friends, when the mood "struck" him — he would lock himself in his studio and create, managing to live there for weeks. According to the dossier — his obsession had cost him his wife, Maria Ivanova, née Krammer.

Not far from the improvised sleeping area, currently piled with a small mountain of literature, from the Zohar on the Tanakh and a battered Torah to a collection of "Legends and Myths of Ancient Greece and Ancient Rome" lying beneath an enormous anatomical atlas for artists and a good dozen assorted issues of Playboy and Men's Health, stood a black Samsung "smart" refrigerator with the door torn clean off.

Behind me, the retching sounds finally stopped. I turned my head slightly and glanced at the police officer, pale as a sheet. District officer Shaikhudinov fastidiously wiped the back of his hand across his mouth and straightened up.

Alexandra finished fussing with the sensors and turned her gaze to me:

— Stas, I'm done. In summary, preliminary assessment says this was done bare-handed. From what I can see, these six took the artist when he wasn't prepared for it. At least, I see no signs of resistance on his part. Bruising, skin under nails — absent. Curious thing is there are no signs of physical violence either. The man was simply taken, sat in the chair and tied up...

— Remarkable. And what did he actually die of?

— Well... Call it old age. There simply wasn't any energy left in his body. Biopotential. He was drained so thoroughly that the corpse is currently, in fact, even slightly siphoning in reverse, so immediately upon completion of the forensic procedures — use your authority to order full disposal, otherwise he'll be dangerous even in a cemetery. And generally, a corpse like this is a find for the Crossed Over.

— Ptuh, Sashka. — I shuddered. "Not my first rodeo," but I just can't get used to those creatures — Fine. Central — I called up the link icon with the centre in my field of view. A window with Artyom's face, today's curator of our investigative team, opened on the virtual display.

— On the line, Comrade Captain.

— File a request, form 2-13. Disposal of hazardous remains. Immediately upon completion of forensic procedures, the victim's body is to be seized and destroyed. One moment. Sasha — what about the fragmented bodies?

— Those are fine, boss. Ordinary stiffs. Just killed in an extremely unusual manner.

— Yes, confirming disposal under 2-13 for one body only.

— Copy. Stas, is it true the victim's last work was commissioned by the Kremlin itself?

— First I've heard of it, honestly. It's you lot at the console who get to watch telly. We're out in the field mostly...

— Well, I'm entitled, I'll have you know! — Artyom remarked, not in the least offended — by the way, I've tapped into your camera — the object in the corner of the room, is that it?

I looked again at the easel. The dimensions of the future painting were indeed imposing. Without thinking twice, I looked at the district officer and silently nodded toward the covering. Rustem understood me correctly, approached the easel, stepping carefully to avoid the pools of blood, and yanked off the cloth. Before my eyes appeared His Imperial Majesty Yaroslav the First, in full dress uniform with medals. I involuntarily stood straighter, clicked my heels, then realised the Emperor was, after all, only a painting, and let out a low whistle. The portrait was magnificent...

Having gone downstairs to the mobile operations centre and taken a mug of coffee from the automatic machine, I stretched out my legs, leaned back in the ergonomic chair set to shiatsu massage mode, and fell into thought.

So — what did we have:

The artist — had once again been working in a "binge." For six months running, since January, Ivan had almost stopped appearing in public, focusing on some grand new project in his studio. Outsiders, during his creative paroxysms, he generally didn't admit, so what exactly he'd been doing in there — God only knew. About a year before these events — he'd separated from his wife. They'd split over attitudes toward art. The wife had grown tired of sharing him with his muse. However, there had been no official divorce yet. They'd decided to give each other some time apart.

Evidently, the absence of a woman's touch proved less bearable than the artist had thought: in his refrigerator, the search turned up distinctly

"feminine" products. Several kinds of yoghurt, strawberries, champagne, chocolate. Next to a freezer stuffed with pelmeni (expensive ones, mind you, handmade, from a restaurant), the aforementioned items looked odd. And in the bed, under a huge gift-edition incunabulum of Mary Shelley, with engraved illustrations — they found a crumpled silk negligée of a rather revealing nature.

Be that as it may — for six months he had been working on some project that, in the end, was nowhere to be found in the studio. His Imperial Majesty was certainly fine work, but let's be honest — it didn't quite qualify as a masterpiece requiring six months of most intense labour. One might suppose the masterpiece had been taken by the attackers who had seized the artist — except that all of them were presently lying in the room.

However — there was a lead. The security camera above the entrance had caught a long-legged girl with flame-red hair, wrapping herself in a long, evidently men's overcoat, running from the building. It should be noted that the girl moved so fast she was captured on camera for literally a single second. Beyond that, unfortunately, her trail went cold. Our colleagues at the Fourth Directorate of SIB were, at our request, now analysing surveillance data from the cameras in the block around the studio. It was clear the girl had the most direct connection to what had happened. Possibly she was even the one who had carried out the slaughter. But in any case, the answer to "what happened here" was something I wanted to find before we managed to catch her.

Moreover — it had to be understood that, although the murder by "draining" the artist's bioenergetic resources, and the mincing of the other victims, did fall squarely under our jurisdiction as a paranormal incident, there nonetheless remained the perfectly ordinary attack by perfectly ordinary hired thugs. No, after Decree 203 all the old gangster crop from the early nineties had been put to the knife. But more than twenty years had passed since then, and new idiots had managed to be born, convinced that money could be obtained by violence without consequence. Be that as it may — this particular attack broke the logic of what had happened. What could you demand from an artist? Especially a famous, popular one? This wasn't some businessman in the back of beyond you could squeeze. This was a public figure. We'd hunt even for a businessman across the whole country. And for a crime this high-profile...

Then again — the artist's body bore no signs of overt violence. Bruises on the arms from fingers — yes. Understandable — seating, binding. Face — untouched. Head — untouched.

I felt the thought hopping somewhere nearby.

— Sash, tell me, my joy, did you find on the body any...

— No, no vampire bite marks. And yes, I've already run the blood sample for specific antibodies. Nothing. It's not a vampire and not a rougarou.

— Right... — my permanent partner, forensic expert of the DPI, Alexandra Novikova, flopped into the neighbouring chair and dropped her feet on a locker labelled "Do not open, dangerous." Judging by the whirring of servomotors — she'd set the chair to aggressive deep massage. Four years ago, when we'd first started working together — that noise drove me crazy. Now — I was used to it.

— So, what have we got from forensics?

— On the artist — I've already told you. Their preliminary findings don't differ one bit from mine. On the others — ripped apart alive. Ripped by some strange weapon. In the forensic team's opinion — it looks like they were... ripped with stakes. Or thick electrodes, for instance, since no wood splinters were found in the wound channels. But the wounds were inflicted by cylindrical, pointed objects, at least ten centimetres long and about a centimetre in diameter.

— Claws.

— Huh?

— They were ripped by claws. And fingers. At least — something similar.

— Well, I don't know. Claws I'd have identified immediately. But here you get the sense that these cylinders were driven in and then spread apart. Very powerfully, very widely and very quickly. No sliced wound edges like from a demon's or rougarou's claws.

— The girl hasn't been found yet, I take it. Otherwise they'd have contacted us by now. Did your people lift prints from the scene?

— Yes. Nothing. I mean, on the thugs there's some stuff, the police are running it now. On the artist — obviously. No other prints.

— And that's interesting. He wasn't drinking champagne and nibbling strawberries with the thugs, I presume. And the negligée, I think, would be a bit small for him...

We both fell into thought. I absently sipped my coffee. Sashka buried her nose in a mug of herbal tea. Something wasn't adding up. Although...

— Central, where does the victim's wife currently reside?

— In Podushkino, on the eponymous highway — Minsk direction, Comrade Captain.

— Excellent. Then call the cleaners to the studio, hand over security to the police, and we're heading there.

The mobile HQ growled its engine and screwed itself into the traffic flow. We had a fairly long drive ahead. Yan was an excellent driver, but midday rush...

— Right, let's break the problem into smaller questions: First — what could the artist have been working on.

Second — who could he have been spending time with, if there were no traces. Third — what interest could hired thugs possibly have in him. Fourth — where did his life energy go.

Fifth — who killed the thugs. Sixth — how.

Seventh — who is the girl on the video...

— Okay. Didn't the choice of books strike you, Stas? Never mind the magazines, but the Book of Splendour? For an ordinary artist?

— A golem? Fine, but where is it now? The girl couldn't have been one — the hair, and generally... Have you seen many elegant golems?

— True, that's odd. Maybe an Avatar of Lilith? Incidentally — the hair colour, the sensuality — all "fits."

— Ha! Now that's interesting! Let's take it as a working hypothesis!

— Thank you, messire, you are so kind! A succubus of that power could well have torn the attackers apart, and then, realising she'd spent a lot of energy and left no one alive — consumed the nearest life: her lover.

— Except that doesn't close two problems: first, what the thugs were doing at a simple artist's place, and second — what inflicted the fatal wounds.

— Maybe some weapon? She's a succubus, after all! A hellish dildo?

— Brr... What an imagination you have... Hey, Yan! Who taught you to drive like that?!

The vehicle stopped so sharply we both nearly flew from our seats. Knowing Yan — I immediately concluded the situation was dire. It took less than a second to yank the Vikhr from its door mount. Then I rolled out the side door and froze in a kneeling position, levelling the barrel along our direction of travel. On top of the vehicle, from the hump of the fairing, the twin Pecheneg mount deployed, remotely operated by Sashka.

Evidently, since I was the first one on the road — it was at me, not at Yan who had jumped out after me, that the woman of about thirty threw herself. The comm confidently identified her as Maria Sokolova.

The beautiful features of the woman's face were contorted with terror.

— It's her!!! Her! She's come for me!!!! Save me! Her!!!!

— Calm down. Who is "her"? Yan — hold the perimeter. Sasha — instrumental sweep of the surroundings.

— She... The doll. His last obsession... — the woman went limp and sinking heavily to the ground, broke into sobs. Without releasing the Vikhr from my right hand, with my left I reached into the first-aid pouch on my side and finding a syrette of tranquilliser — injected it into the woman's neck.

At that moment, a target indicator appeared in my augmented reality field of view. I brought the assault rifle up and dashed toward the vehicle.

Along the road leading to the artist's villa walked a girl of unearthly beauty. Long-legged, with a lush mane of flame-red hair with a noble tint of mahogany or cherry, with a sculpted figure in no way concealed by the unbuttoned men's autumn coat.

However — against it, one could distinctly see that the skin beneath was practically pure white, with marble veining. And the movement of her limbs left no creases on it. The material simply flowed in time with the monotonous motion of impossibly beautiful legs.

— Halt! — I barked, gripping the rifle handle tighter and flicking the selector to "burst" with my index finger.

The girl stopped and looked straight at me. Nothing could be read in the marble eyes, and yet I sensed a distinct bewilderment emanating from them.

— She is a murderer. She sent people to kill me. Because of her, my Creator perished. She must die. A life for a life — isn't that how it's done among you? — The girl's voice turned out to be remarkably melodious and sensual. She waited for my answer, while the wind tugged at the folds of her coat, displaying in full glory her perfect marble body. On the ideally polished, living marble — brown stains of the thugs' clotted blood were still visible.

— That's not true! — the voice came from behind me. The medicine had apparently worked, and three operatives armed to the teeth do wonders for confidence. — I only wanted to save him from you! From this ruinous passion! You should have been destroyed so he would return to me, to his work, to his life! You are a chunk of rock! You are nobody! You don't even exist! I don't know how you can be standing here! These must be my hallucinations! I'll wake up any moment and you won't be here, and he'll be beside me!

The flash of rage sapped Maria's strength; she fell to her knees and hid her face in her hands. The marble girl unhurriedly moved forward, with a smooth, gliding and incredibly alluring step.

I fired a burst over her head. The girl, whom I had already mentally christened "Galatea," froze. Then stared at me again.

— Why are you doing this? She just confessed. I must take her life for the life of my... beloved? (The voice sounded questioning-yet-assertive.) Don't stand in my way. I have no business with you. However — if I must, I will walk through your life to reach hers.

— She will be tried. You must surrender to us. That will be better for everyone.

— It would have been better had I never appeared at all — either I imagined it, or that came with a bitter smirk! Extraordinary! — The choice is made. Galatea's silhouette blurred from the speed with which the stone girl, unexpectedly for all of us, proved capable of moving.

Fortunately — we are trained to react with reflexes, not the brain. The burst cut through the spot where she should have been... And it turned out I had calculated correctly. She dropped out of her acceleration instantly. Stared with surprise at the ugly chip on her perfectly polished stomach — where the heavy nine-millimetre armour-piercing bullet had struck.

— That... hurts... — amazingly, tears stood in her marble eyes. — Why are you doing this to me? I only want justice. My beloved (Oh! Now spoken with certainty!) is dead. Because of her! Why do you hurt me, and not her?!

For a second the girl stood looking straight into my eyes, into my heart. And then she launched herself and with some incredible leap, covering the distance to the nearest three-metre fence, cleared it in one motion and vanished somewhere in the labyrinth of private townhouses. Sasha tried to bring her down with a burst from the machine guns, but only shredded the coat — already crusted with blood — which Galatea had shed mid-leap, most likely precisely to distract us.

...— It looks like she entered the house without even thinking about the door being locked.

— I'd say so — I replied pensively, examining the remains of the doorframe, in which the lock mortise was barely recognisable.

In the hallway of the Rublyovka villa sat a girl trembling from her ordeal. Our psychologist was working with her — calming her, and simultaneously extracting details of her encounter with Galatea.

In broad strokes — the situation was shaping up roughly as follows:

Having fled from us, the golem (though — what kind of golem was this...) managed to shake pursuit by leaping over fences. While I was calling in the nearest patrol drones and setting them on her trail, our fugitive managed to spot through a window Margarita Agramyan, daughter of Akop Agramyan... But that's hardly the point. The point is that the two were of rather similar build (of course, Rita was a far cry from Galatea's perfection, but for clothing purposes, the difference was not so critical). So the stone girl entered Margo's house and immediately asked for help. The most astonishing thing was that for the first few minutes, Margo actually rushed to help her, attributing the deathly pallor of the girl's "skin" to the weather (it had already grown quite chilly), and supposing that the strange behaviour and appearance were the result of a sexual assault (which was well enough explained by the guest's nudity and the blood smears covering her body). The result: Galatea was instantly handed some clothes and firmly escorted to the bathroom.

Things got rather more interesting when the girl, now dressed, emerged from the bathroom. That was when our Masha — sorry, Margo — got a proper look and saw that her new friend had a marble face, marble eyes, synthetic hair and skin that was icy even after a shower. That was when such shrieking commenced that within a couple of minutes, armed neighbours burst in from every nearby property. Naturally, the fugitive was already gone. The moment Margo began to scream, Galatea had slipped away at her fantastic speed. The police unit, called while she was still in the bath, could only document the damage to the door and, having listened to Margo's breathless account, call us.

Where the uncontrolled animated statue had fled to — now remained anyone's guess. And — to wait for alarm calls.

She sat on a girder of the bridge support. A pathetic, hunched little figure, like a bedraggled sparrow. Margarita's designer jeans and jumper were torn and heavily soiled. Through the rips, fresh chips and cracks in her marble body were visible.

I made one more effort and finally reached the neighbouring girder. In my ear, Colonel Tikhomirov was quietly howling, promising to tear off everything that protruded the moment I returned. I silently turned and pulled the connector from the communication unit. The video recorder was independent, but shouting in my ears was the last thing I needed right now.

Below us flowed the Moscow River. Though if you fell, you'd land only on the embankment. From nearly fifty metres up. We sat in silence for about ten minutes. Then I turned to her and asked:

— Why did you save her?

— She is human. She should live. You are given... So much... — did I imagine it, or did Galatea sniffle — she had everything ahead of her.

— But you could have been destroyed yourself. Why would you care about a human child?

— I... don't know... My life... The only thing still driving me was revenge. That, you took from me, and later I understood that for a human life — humans themselves must exact the price... I walked through the city... Watched your lives, learned from you... You... You are perfect. I wanted to become... To become human. Someday. But it's impossible. I am merely a living statue.

— Well, many would consider you perfection. And you are alive — you walk, you speak. Come with me. Let's see what our organisation can do for you.

— This isn't life, you understand that... don't you? Do you know what it is — when your heart doesn't beat, when your body is cold as stone... I don't know... For me there was never anything different... But I know this is not life. I know you are different. I know you are fragile and soft... I know that the...

— Lorry...

— The lorry, yes. It would have left none of you a chance... But... What is my durability worth — compared to your soul. You see, I feel infinite emptiness inside myself... I don't want this kind of "life," simply because it is un-life. I am not human. I am — undead...

Galatea rose to her feet. The wind up here blew mercilessly, whipping the scraps of the girl's clothes. Her body, damaged by the impact of the Kamaz's bumper but having saved a first-grader who'd been daydreaming at a crossing — was covered in cracks and had lost its mesmerising fluidity.

— Father created me in vain... He loved me even when I was lifeless stone. And lifeless stone, in truth, is what I remained... But he did everything to give me life. It's not his fault that he never could...

I move, I speak. You're right. But — I am not human. I am a monster. A freak. From the very moment of my emergence from a block of marble — I have brought nothing but grief and suffering.

My Creator gave up his wife for me. He managed to awaken me... At first, only briefly. Then I stayed awake longer and longer. He taught me to be human... For his pleasure, I could even pretend to eat and drink... But it was only an imitation of life. Of course I could not eat. I could not kiss my Creator. I could not love him the way... The way his wife loved him. He withdrew into himself more and more, reading terrible books...

And then I awoke, feeling the touch of his soul upon me. That was probably the only time I understood what it means to truly live.

It was a very brief moment. And in the next second I had to defend myself... Only after killing the last enemy did I understand that I shouldn't have fought. That the one I loved, as much as a statue can love — was dead. He had given his last breath — so that I might live.

And I also understood that after what I had felt, receiving all of his life — I could never again exist in my un-life.

Galatea, holding on to the bridge structure with one hand, leaned over the abyss.

— How strange... It seems to me that right now, of the two of us — I understand better than you: what it means to be human.

She turned her face to me and looked into my eyes. Looked with living eyes, human ones. I lunged forward, already knowing I wouldn't make it in time... And yet — for a split second, our hands touched.

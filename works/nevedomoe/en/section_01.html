The Unknown: A View from Within

paranormal memoirs

I've always said that modern trends in the approach to studying the supernatural frankly drive me up the wall. When I hear the word "aura," my cheek starts twitching. When I hear "astral," I get an acute desire to kill the speaker.

And it's not because of some atheism, scepticism or other ways of denying the obvious. The thing is, I've encountered the unknown too often in my life. I've been meaning to tell some stories from this area for a long time, and now, I think, the most fitting moment has come.

Everything you read here is true. Treat it however you like; nevertheless, the fact is that this is my personal experience. In places obtained through bruises and bumps. Some stories are funny to remember now. Some still freeze me to this day. I'll try, where possible, to tell you what happened with maximum anchoring to reality. So that, should you have such a desire, you could verify this information. In a number of cases there are witnesses to what happened. In some — only me.

In general, take all this as you will. As personal ravings of the author, or as a reason to seriously consider — are we alone in our world?

I consider it important to note that historically I perceive all such things from a strictly scientific point of view. Specifically scientific. I haven't seen confirmation of the existence of torsion fields. I generally don't accept the words of various "contactees" about "astrals" and "subtle bodies." Only what scientific parapsychology has built up over this time. I.e. the discipline studying paranormality from strictly classical positions.

Introduction

Let's start with the fact that I never particularly chased after devilry. Well, it just doesn't do anything for me. It's only in words that a ghost is wrapped in an aura of mysticism and mystery. Various irresponsible directors even show us films in which various friendly and non-scary "Caspers" play merrily with ordinary kids. In reality, if you haven't soiled yourself simply from the presence of a ghost nearby, you already have an awesomely strong will. Spoiler — I held out. The first couple of times were hard and quite freaky. Then nothing — got used to it.

Only a psycho can think that meeting a poltergeist is an amusing adventure. I personally assure you — it's rather quite dangerous and extremely nerve-wracking.

A house spirit is not Kuzya with a chest of fairy tales. It's at the minimum a completely alien entity inhabiting some hidden layers of our reality. And periodically sticking out from there. Trust me — when it sticks out in front of you, you'll regret not being a couple of thousand kilometres from your native home at the time.

The reader has the right to ask — why haven't I encountered such things? I'll try to answer clearly.

It's just that historically, everyone in our family has paid tribute to paranormality. No, in our lineage, as far as I know, there were no witches and sorcerers, and my grannies and great-grannies didn't boil black cats on Friday the 13th. Nevertheless, in the academic sense, all my relatives know a thing or two about devilry. For example, dad, besides working in the rocket sphere, spent considerable time in the storerooms of the Lenin Library, where besides specialist literature he sometimes leafed through collections having nothing to do with traditional science. Mum — generally, worked in her time at Komsomolskaya Pravda, and as a special correspondent. Which meant she raced around the whole country as soon as a report came in about something worthy of attention. So when in the early 80s there was serious talk of devilry, it was she who set off from her paper to investigate on location. Often taking me with her. Ancestors on dad's side seemed to have had some relation once to the early Inquisition... But the main thing perhaps isn't even that... I think one of the main reasons is that since childhood I've been quite observant. This isn't bragging, and my observance isn't entirely usual. I notice the least eye-catching phenomena and events, often missing "elephants." Friends and acquaintances constantly tease me on this topic; nevertheless, if some very subtle connection or some entirely non-obvious things need to be found, it's me they call.

Mum, having driven herself ragged on reports about all sorts of yetis, mischievous spirits and other Koshcheis the Deathless, frankly drifted on paranormality. All sorts of psychics of all stripes started frequenting the house, who en masse broadcast in beyond-the-grave voices about all sorts of mysticism, astrals, portals and other auras. To be honest — my negativity toward all these charlatans is probably from then. Plus these guys wore me out by, apparently to please mum, displaying wild admiration at the sight of me, waving their bent metal pieces in front of my nose and yelling that they'd never seen such wild power, and prophesying me to be either a messiah, or an antichrist, or both together. I'll be honest — being a messiah never appealed; these guys have an unpleasant property of dying quickly and agonisingly. For an antichrist, I clearly lack misanthropy, general embitteredness and villainous charm, so from this whole psychic brotherhood I tried to keep my distance. Possibly more than anyone else — I was finished off by Pavel Globa, who from the threshold launched into the old song of the main thing, additionally making far-reaching conclusions that a person born on Friday the 13th, plus at one in the afternoon (not to mention some wild stellar arrangement), couldn't be a normal person by definition. In response, I politely suggested the "astrologer of all Russia" estimate how many children in total had been born at the same time in the same parts. After which I tried to bugger off quickly.

On the matter of national carpet-making

My first encounter with devilry took place when I was about five. The thing is, from another business trip Mum brought back: an article about an embezzling chairman of a Central Asian collective farm — one piece. The freshest, lamb-fresh mutton from grateful collective farmers — ten kilos. And an antique handmade carpet, made in the same Central Asia, from the local chairman of the executive committee — also one piece. The collective farm chairman, on the results of journalistic investigation, as is right and proper, went to increase the welfare of our Soviet motherland by logging beyond the polar circle (the OBKhSS knew how to work, and journalists in the USSR really were a powerful organ of control), the collective farm sharply began to prosper under the leadership of an already Russian chairman, and the carpet was mounted above my bed.

Already the next night, I understood it was given to us not for nothing.

In general, since childhood I usually sleep "an infant's sleep." I.e., if I've fallen asleep, you can only wake me if you know how. To extraneous noise I simply don't react, and I can sleep in the most unsuited conditions for that. Nevertheless, that time I woke up around one in the morning. There was a strange sensation that something here was utterly wrong. To understand what exactly, one glance at the carpet was enough. In general, in its original state, this lump of wool was woven in some complex geometric pattern. Abstraction almost in pure form.

Now the carpet was glowing. Not that it was downright shining, but quite jauntily fluorescing with a dim greenish light. Over its whole area. Moreover — there was no abstraction at all!

On the carpet, in a perfectly natural way, were intertwined perfectly normal Chinese dragons. No, I understand now that they were dragons, and Chinese ones at that. Moreover — for me now a Chinese dragon is, perhaps, even more natural than a European crocodile with little wings. But at five — I assure you, no familiar Chinese dragon was known to me. And generally, of cryptoreptiles I at best knew Gorynych. From the old film "Ilya Muromets."

Clearly, such a dirty trick from the carpet rather upset me. To be honest, I'd have been glad at that moment to be somewhere far from there. Only screaming wouldn't come out. My throat seemed to be cut off. Climbing out of bed and bolting somehow didn't appeal. The aforementioned dragons inspired no confidence, and who knew what they might do if I imprudently drew attention to myself. However, simply being engaged in contemplation of this zoopornography also didn't appeal. Feeling around, I located a book hidden under the pillow (I learned to read at about three, and so loved this activity that without a book I didn't fall asleep. Characteristically — I read not fairy tales, but mainly encyclopaedias and popular science. And stop laughing so loudly) — I sharply launched this book at the flower standing on the table. According to all the laws of physics, the cactus planed onto the floor, where it finished its mortal existence with a loud "bdynts." The carpet dances instantly ceased. Both figurants stared with extreme interest at the freely-flying pot. As I'd planned, these guys demonstratively ignored me.

Clearly, the folks simply could not fail to react to such a statement of problems, so they appeared in the room a minute later. As soon as the door to the room flew open, the carpet quieted, again becoming simply a woollen product. In the room, the parents discovered: One prematurely deceased pot with a cactus. The cactus itself (still alive) — one piece. Well and their own son in a slightly stunned state — one person. This person, moreover, fairly clearly tried to state what had happened approximately as follows: "You know, Mum, it's good that we have an electric stove. If we had gas — I'd burn this rag to hell!"

In general, when passions subsided, the pot was given last honours and the cactus was temporarily transplanted into a milk carton, we arranged a family council. To my parents' credit, in my honesty and mental health, no one ever had any doubts, and the attitude toward paranormality at the minimum reduced to the fact that it exists. So at the family council at three in the morning, it was decided that the carpet would be experimentally rehung in the parents' room.

Accordingly — the day passed normally, but in the evening... Father was urgently called in to the research institute, the test of an engine for our "product" was scheduled, so there was a mountain of work before launch.

Dad promised to return around six in the morning, jumped into the institute car, under the protection of two unsmiling lads in plain clothes (who wore me out trying to feed me "Mishka" sweets. Well, I just didn't like those sweets, and politeness didn't allow me to tell them so.), and whizzed off to his institute. Mum and I watched "Good Night" and turned in.

Word of honour — I don't know what happened there, and questioning her was somehow tactless. In the morning, dad got us up around eight. More precisely got me up. Mum, by all appearances, hadn't slept the whole night.

So at breakfast she was extremely pensive, and moreover not particularly talkative, which is utterly not like her.

Right after breakfast, the carpet was packed up. First rolled into a tube, then wrapped in cellophane. All this joy was wound with electrical tape until the carpet became simply indistinguishable, after which in this form it was delivered to grandma in Lyubertsy. She has a wonderful storeroom, with which many stories are also connected. Be that as it may, the carpet was placed there, with the note "for eternal storage." As far as I can judge, it's still there to this day.

I don't know what it was. I don't know how to relate to this. The fact is that it really happened. Grandma showed no displeasure about the presence in her possession of this masterpiece of weaving art. On the other hand, in that storeroom there are things more interesting than this carpet, so I think that's its proper place. The only thing that bothers me is what to do with this little carpet if something happens to grandma. It must be said that moths, as well as any other still-living creatures, don't live in this branch of a black hole misnamed as grandma's storeroom, so the carpet lies there... And awaits its hour...

Shattered heads, black hands and other dismemberments

...As I've already said, encounters with the Unknown became the norm for me from the youngest age. Word of honour — I don't know the cause of that. Perhaps readiness for such encounters, perhaps absence of any desire to meet the unknown. Perhaps even just that the house stood on some geological fault... The fact is that the carpet was only one of the episodes...

The first and one of the most unpleasant guests from the otherworld, in my home besides the carpet, became the "Hand." What it was I find it hard to say. In principle, systematising the data accumulated to date and comparing with my childhood impressions, with a known degree of probability I can suppose it was a so-called Hoog. In Russian there's perhaps no equivalent. Bogey? In general, however that may be, the creature was extremely malicious and unpleasant. If I was left alone, I had to keep away from the gaps between the backs of sofas and the walls. For one simple reason — if I foolishly found myself in the epsilon-neighbourhood, from this gap a gnarled hand of anthracite-black colour shot out like lightning. To dodge — simply didn't work. The hand lightning-fast grabbed the throat and began to strangle. What's funny — the gap could be literally a centimetre. That was enough. Less — even it couldn't apparently squeeze through. The strangling stopped as soon as adults appeared at the threshold. The hand lightning-fast hid back. Once I tried, immediately after, to stick my head under the bed with a torch in hand (to overcome fear, one must above all be rid of the unknown. I knew this since childhood). Let's say — I saw something there... What — I can't reproduce even now. I remember some piercing gaze and the sensation of a window into some alien SOMETHING. Further I don't remember a damn thing. I came to a couple of seconds later, looking at the dusty and empty floor under the bed, with the feeling that I'd been screwed right in the brain. Well, then I perceived this differently, of course.

In general, similar leapfrog continued for a year and a half to two, until on my eighth birthday grandfather gifted me a dagger. The trick was that the dagger was German, trophy, with runes, and, as turned out significantly later, of "cold iron." If anyone doesn't know — cold iron isn't a nail from the freezer, but metal worked without the use of fire. By pure forging. According to beliefs of all countries of the world without exception, it has monstrous killing action on the otherworld. Why — hell knows. Given the labour-intensiveness of manufacture, why the hell some now-deceased SS-man needed it is a mystery to me. Be that as it may, the dagger came in very handy. Playing with it once again (grandfather prudently DULLED the blade enough that the grandson on one hand would get used to holding a weapon in his hands, and on the other wouldn't accidentally cut himself) — I climbed onto the sofa... And found myself in the vise of my old acquaintance. True, this time I didn't panic. With cold-bloodedness surprising even for me, I simply clearly swung the dagger (which, given my childhood miniature size, qualified at minimum as a short sword). The dagger, despite being dull as a felt boot, passed through the hand like a red-hot knife through butter. To be honest, in my view it even glowed. The howl was such that both papa and mama came running. The cut-off part of the hand simply crumbled into fine black powder, which then simply evaporated. What happened with the rest of the monster I don't know. But I never saw it again. Mum and Dad, who'd previously been quite sceptical of my stories about the Hand, this time simply could not allow that their only son could howl like that. Neither in strength nor in tonality did I match. Years later, by the way, when in my life a series of moves began — I handed the dagger over to our family "special storage." So as not to lose. Perhaps someday I'll take it from there. Perhaps. Or perhaps not, because I strongly suspect it's not only a weapon but also a beacon. A key opening doors to the unknown. Or perhaps doors — to the unknown?

The next amusing story happened in the summer of 1990. In our then-Krylatskoye apartment there were two rooms. One was the living room. In it stood the parents' bed, the TV, the wall unit... And there was eternally a kind of half-light and stuffiness. Why — hell knows. Maybe the carpet hanging on the wall affected it? (not that one, of course). I had a separate room. Entirely mine. And it was just sunny and airy. Plus there stood a fairly wide and comfortable sofa-bed. Mum, a lover of reading books in free time, liked to laze on this bed with another classic in hand. There was enough room for me — in case I got bored of playing, I could freely go and sleep beside Mum. Family idyll, in a word.

So, June 1990 was underway. House number five on Autumn Boulevard dozed in anticipation of the Moscow siesta. Little Eugene returned from outside, played his fill with his toys and decided to flop down for a nap... So, I climbed onto the back of the bed and, as I liked to do, tried to plop down on it flat (the sofa-bed was extremely soft, and at 9 I was still extremely light and compact). Can you imagine my state when I, already having lost balance but not yet fallen, saw that on my half of the bed, beside Mum, lay flat a certain man... The man was black. Very clear. But somehow contoured. And the main thing — he lay face down, and on his crown gaped a wound... No, rather a great wound! In general the man, to put it mildly, didn't look alive... And I was slowly and inexorably falling on him!

In general, I emitted a howl louder than an ocean liner. And waved my arms so that, put me next to Baron Pampa and the prize for helicopter-likeness would unambiguously be mine.

Nevertheless — my otherworldly gift is apparently limited to the ability to see and interact with the Unknown. Levitation isn't included in its supply package...

So — I crashed right onto this... this... In general right there. Nose clearly into the shattered crown. The sensations were... Mildly speaking unpleasant. I experienced something similar five years later, when I dove with a kayak into the icy March water of the rowing canal...

Mum barely had time to leap from the bed so I wouldn't break her nose.

I myself flew out of there after her, with clearly noticeable tachycardia and choking from horror and disgust. On the bed, as one might expect, no trace of the ghost remained...

And a couple of hours later, our neighbour called us — "Aunt Rita" if memory serves — who, oohing and lamenting, told us that in our basement of the house a murder had occurred!

1990 wasn't 1993 yet. Murder was something awful and rare. So the whole house ran to look at the operational group's work. I'll honestly say — I'm not in on the details of the case. I know only one thing. When from the basement they carried out a stretcher covered with a sheet, the fresh summer breeze that almost always blows in Krylatskoye blew a little stronger. The sheet fell, revealing in God's daylight a horrifying corpse. Evidently he'd first been strangled, and he'd lain in the basement for considerable time — because the skin was a bluish-black. And on the back of the head gaped a horrifying wound through which one could clearly see fragments of bone and brain...

...Smart people who deal with research into the supernatural constantly come up with various ways to classify and lay out on shelves these phenomena. According to one of them, ghosts can be divided into types. One of which (and probably one of the most widespread) is the so-called White Lady. As a rule, it's a ghostly silhouette of a woman dressed in a long white loose garment. Ghosts of this type are most often heralds. They appear to warn about something, to report something. They speak rarely. More often they communicate by gestures. For a person they're considered not dangerous, and often even useful. However, this doesn't cancel the fact that the news they spread is predominantly of the most evil sort.

Once I had the chance to encounter something similar...

I was about 10. By this time I'd already managed to be on the other side (due to lobar asphyxia), seen so much of everything that not every adult is granted. I was spending the night that time at grandma's. In Lyubertsy. Yes — in that very artifact apartment. To be honest, I couldn't stand staying at grandma's, but sometimes such a necessity arose.

And so, having walked my fill (and there's nothing else to do in Lyubertsy, then or, much more so, now), I trudged home and crashed to sleep...

I woke from cold. Given that I was covered with a down quilt — mildly speaking, I was surprised. Nevertheless I carefully opened one eye and exhaled. Above my face formed a cloud of steam. And meanwhile an involuntary nervous trembling began to beat me. The sensations were not the most pleasant. Plus there was also the factor of fear. Gradually I felt a strange, piercing gaze.

There were only two options. Curl into a ball, turn to the wall, and tremble, hoping for the best. Or turn toward the source of discomfort. I chose the second.

Against the window background, right in front of me, at a distance of five to seven metres from the bed, stood a woman in a wide-brimmed hat with a veil, and a long grey-white dress (???) or cape. Remember the intro of the "Good Night, Little Ones" programme, around 1993? Where Aunt Night was. Well, something in that spirit. Very similar.

This wondrous creation, seeing that I'd woken and was staring at her, stood another couple of seconds, then slowly and smoothly moved toward me. Remember the film "Mars Attacks!"? There was a moment when a "spy" was sent to the White House. This one moved the same way. And I was generally paralysed. I couldn't even rasp a word. All I could do was close and open my eyes. Closed-opened — figure six metres away. Closed-opened — four... In general, I don't know what exactly she intended to tell me, and why she so strove to come closer, without hurrying, understanding I wouldn't run... I don't know. I allow that just to whisper in my ear that "the treasure is buried under that oak." I don't know, and to check, I didn't want to then nor does it appeal now. In general, all I could manage was to squeeze out "Begone." You should have seen what her face became at this. I just felt a gust of wind and power from behind my back. The nasty lady was simply BLOWN AWAY. To await the battle's end I didn't bother. I simply understood that now everything would be alright, I was under protection, and moreover wanted terribly to sleep... So I turned away from the "aunty" receiving preventive thumping from the friendly collective of my Russo-German ancestor-spirits, sighed heavily and conked out...

The practice of investigating haunted houses, or on malicious real estate

It must be said that historically, the Anglo-Saxons clearly encountered devilry far more often than us. At minimum, the number of lexemes describing one or another typical case is far greater with them than with us. For example — there's such a phenomenon: a house captured by a spirit. Not just a ghost — that's nonsense. But specifically when a spirit becomes essentially the soul of the house. In Russian there's no word describing this. In English — the haunting. That says it all in general.

In my personal experience there was such a case. Which I'll now tell you about.

It must be said that I came up with a classification of manifestations of the other world by their degree of interactivity. The ability to interact with our world. The first degree is manifestations in the form of sensations. Anxiety, the feeling of being watched from emptiness.

The second is visual unclear or auditory manifestations. Flashes, movement at the edge of perception, knocks, steps...

The third is already recognisable images. Essentially classical ghosts. Often even with sound accompaniment. Always accompanied by icy horror. If the first cases can be investigated relatively calmly, then starting from the third degree, the paranormal reaches your consciousness. Characteristic is a sharp chill, a sense of unreasoning fear, the sensation of something viscous and sticky in the air...

The fourth is poltergeist. Can be accompanied by the previous three.

Well and the fifth — this is perhaps the most terrible. This is essentially when something completely alien to us turns out to be nearby, and the most awful thing — capable of acting on you directly.

Most often, the haunting doesn't step over the fourth threshold. Cases with the fifth are often passed down as legends.

I'm lucky in this regard. I'm the owner of a house in the countryside. Only there I don't have milk, but a most genuine haunting. Of the fifth level.

So, Ryazan region, Pitelinsky district, village of Penki. Time — 07:00, June 12, 1998. Cast — two young scoundrels, who decided to rest from school in the village. Specifically Tviksel, and your humble servant. Though — I should probably start from the very beginning... About ten years before the events described...

It all started with the fire victims. Specifically this was one of mum's last articles in Komsomolskaya Pravda. By that time, father had taken up business, the need for work fell away, and the younger sister, who was then two or three years old, demanded a mass of attention. Nevertheless, mum agreed to drive out to those unfortunate Penki and write an article about the first Soviet farmers and their lives.

The article wrote itself instantly. Everything was obvious. The guys tried to spread their wings, the neighbours didn't like it, so they let the red rooster loose on them. Everything was insured, so everything was more-or-less in order.

But the main thing wasn't that.

Mum's and my eyes met a surprisingly pleasant little village. About two hundred houses, huge water-meadow fields, in three kilometres from the village — a most clean and transparent lake. The air — it was simply impossible to imagine cleaner.

In general, in the head of my enterprising mum, a plan instantly ripened.

I don't know by what truth or untruth, but mum managed to acquire three houses in the village.

Don't ask how and for how much. This is still a secret behind seven seals. The only thing I can say is EXTREMELY cheaply. One — an ordinary good village house (subsequently went as an additional payment when exchanging the apartment). The second — small, neat outside and completely empty inside. Not even a floor. Moreover — ten metres from the village cemetery. The funniest thing is that this isn't about it.

The third stood closer to the village middle. It was old as mammoth shit and ugly as mortal sin. But it sold with an enormous plot. Essentially — behind it began an enormous meadow, entirely belonging to the house's owner.

Mum quickly formalised the deal and whizzed off to Moscow.

To the village we didn't get out until the early 90s. There we quickly hustled and moved to the neighbouring house with apartment expansion to three rooms. Then, already in a new place, we met a wonderful neighbour, an old man nicknamed Vasilich — a jester, scoundrel and real Russian Kulibin. To grandpa was instantly issued a power of attorney, after which he was placed as governor over both remaining plots.

The result — we temporarily forgot about the existence of the plots. Vasilich, appearing in the autumn, briskly reported on how he was redoing the little house by the cemetery, which he'd chosen as his residence. And overall everything went its course. The house on the third plot stood empty and abandoned and smoothly continued sinking into the ground.

Until two scoundrels from Moscow decided to set off to the village. Specifically the young master, with his faithful clevret-confidant. Me and Tviksel.

Vasilich was hurriedly yanked to Moscow, where he was handed a round sum of money, two young oxen, and instructions that the oxen had to be returned in a week and preferably not in pieces.

At 6 a.m. we were already unloaded on the Sasovo platform, and catching some shahid-old-man on a Moskvich with number 412, dashed off to our base.

We were on site somewhere around seven in the morning. Vasilich's little house sparkled with fresh varnish, smelled of ozone and safely de-energised a couple of neighbouring towns. Something there sparked, squealed, sawed... In general the house lived its rich and independent inner life. At Tviksel's attempt to open the door, Vasilich howled like a wounded cat and flung the poor man into the nettles. I detachedly watched the trajectory of my friend, the lightning broken off from the spark gap and passing a couple of centimetres from my ear, phlegmatically heaved the rucksack onto my shoulder and trotted to the second house.

Tviksel climbed out of the nettle thickets, picked up his share of the load and joined me.

Our "new old" house impressed. Firstly, by all appearances it had been built in the pre-Petrine epoch. A real village log house had sunk into the ground up to its windows. The windows, by the way, were glazed with practically real mica. Despite the in general small dimensions, the house was as concrete as you can possibly imagine.

I couldn't help myself and snapped it on a Polaroid. The shot crawled out, lay a minute... a third... a fifth... In general in reality — probably I should have been on alert already then. But I didn't twig... Came to the conclusion the film was bad. The shot reflected natural white noise. The house never developed.

Dumping our things on the embankment and dispatching Vasilich, we tried to get inside. The door stubbornly refused to open. Grown in. In general it ended with me simply ramming it with my shoulder at a run. I flew inside only to, a second later, fly out of there with a howl. Not every day, entering an uninhabited premises, do you discover a coffin standing vertically right on course. With the lid neatly set aside.

Tviksel admired my crooked physiognomy a couple of minutes, laughed at my superstitions and hallucinations, dragged on a cigarette, hoisted both our rucksacks on himself and calmly headed inside... To emerge outside a second later, without rucksacks, without cigarette and in, to put it mildly, a very puzzled appearance.

— Coffin red, like? With frills? — slightly nervously inquired the confidant.

— Aha. Characteristic shape. — I'd already caught my breath and more-or-less collected myself.

— So not a hallucination — Tviksel mumbled in a fallen voice and nervously lit a new cigarette from the filter.

— So not a hallucination. Have to get it out of there.

Tviks threw out the spoiled cigarette, swore and, hunching, went after me.

In the corridor — there was no coffin. Nor a lid. There stood a pair of unplaned boards. Clearly, of no red material with frills either.

I somewhat nervously fingered my little cross and proposed settling in. In the living room we discovered an iconostasis with icons fallen face down, a censer with incense lying on its side, a cracked Russian stove that played the role of a column on which essentially the whole construction rested, and a bed.

Vasilich was given a dressing-down on the matter of complete absence of attention to the real estate entrusted to his care, a promise was wrung out to promptly run light into the little house, and a kerosene lamp was shaken out.

Tired out for the day, we conked out like the dead, so you can imagine our amazement when we woke around one in the morning from such a sharp temperature drop that words flew out of mouths literally with steam. Characteristically — outside it was even hot.

Cursing and remembering Vasilich with a bad word, we extracted sleeping bags from the rucksacks. And this is where we got twisted up...

Believe me, lads. If you've never found yourself in the epicentre of a fifth-class phenomenon, you don't know what fear is.

You can parachute-jump, you can be under the muzzle of a pistol, you can admire a close nuclear explosion... It's all nonsense. Real fear — it defies description. It's irrational. It's HOPELESS. Tviksel and I died. And remained alive... We simply lost the sense of reality. Fear drank strength from us. Fear drank life itself from us. It was the feeling of absolute hopelessness. Of absolute and infinite horror... And it continued an entire eternity.

In reality — to convey this in words is impossible. There was no talk of any panic. There were no spiritual strengths to try to run somewhere, to do something... We simply lay on our beds and went mad from horror.

I managed to bridle it first. Trivially, experience told. I shut down on myself. And began little by little to recover from this psychic blow. As soon as I managed to restore at least a little control, I clutched at Tviksel's hand and began muttering the mantra of the circle of power. Tviksel quickly cottoned on and joined my muttering.

In general this turned out to be enough. Convinced that we couldn't be broken by the first blow, the nightmare retreated.

The second day we decided to spend outside the little house. Moreover, aborigines appeared, with whom contact was instantly established. So already by the middle of the day we set off to the lake. There we bathed, rested, fried shashlik... In general we headed to the little house already in the dark.

Along the way, going through the meadow, a little dog tagged along behind us... Such a small one, the size of a shepherd. One trouble. The little dog was pitch-black with yellow, glowing eyes. Moreover — it was darkening, but not so much that you couldn't see that the meadow around us was covered with grass somewhere up to my knees. The little dog, however, managed to dissolve in this grass. Moreover, it looked interesting. You looked at the little dog, at some point your vision slightly clouded, you blinked, and the little dog was no longer there. But it was quite calmly discovered on the opposite side of the road.

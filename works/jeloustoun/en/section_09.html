Chapter 9

...Hans was looking at the field with a lost expression. Just yesterday, yellow with rapeseed — today it was ashen grey. However — not only the field. Grey was his car, his house, the road... From the sky, swirling like snowflakes — flakes of ash were falling. And, judging by the density of the clouds — no end and no edge to this "ashfall" was in sight...

...The trawler "Northern Prince" rocked on the leaden, grey waves of the Norwegian Sea. The leadenness, as is not difficult to guess, was lent to the sea not by the cold. It was simply that its surface was covered with a disgusting, grey film of ash. Light volcanic ash did not wish to settle to the bottom. Instead — it felt perfectly fine in the surface layer of the water, cutting off the ocean from sunlight, poisoning the water, killing the plankton.

Captain Ulrich Carlsen tiredly rubbed the bridge of his nose. Possessing the appearance of a fantasy Viking berserker, he was an educated and extremely well-read man. So now, as soon as he had learned of the events in the USA — he had buried himself in scientific literature for several hours, to understand what he and his crew should prepare for, and whether to turn back to port immediately. After all, in the event a "global tsunami" awaited them, the further from the mainland, the greater the chance of their vessel surviving.

However, from the books he had grasped only one thing: it was unknown what would happen with a tsunami, but the dying-out of the ocean biosphere — was a matter of time.

...Beatrice was in Naples for the first time in her life. In general, surprisingly, even while studying at the University of Florence as a historian, the girl had practically not left her native city, until her academic supervisor practically forced her, almost making a scene, to embark on a journey through the places of "martial glory" of the central figure of her scholarly work. That was approximately how she had ended up in the Piazza Dante. Of course, Alighieri had had little to do with Naples, so visiting the square named after him — was, to a large extent, an amusing whim of a student who had simply taken to travelling. However, the trip had not disappointed her. The ancient and beautiful (although for a native of Florence this was not the most significant merit), square breathed history. At some point, it seemed to Beatrice that the city was indeed breathing: the tiles beneath her feet were palpably moving up and down. It became hard to stand — the city, it seemed, had begun a deep, deep exhalation. Everyone present heard it — it sounded like a terrible, low-frequency howl. And then hell began...

The Phlegraean Fields — as if had sensed the awakening of their kin in North America. The first to crash into the abyss was Pozzuoli. The crust, breaking apart before their eyes, swallowed by the chasm — slumped downward, with all the structures standing upon it. Down there poured also millions of tonnes of water from the Mediterranean Sea, which, in turn, gave rise to a terrible steam explosion as soon as the cold water came in contact with the fiery interior of the Earth. Naples did not wait long for its fate. Nor, indeed, did all of Italy. On the world map a new fiery cauldron appeared. The collapsed crater of a supervolcano.

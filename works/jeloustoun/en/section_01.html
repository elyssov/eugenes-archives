Chapter 1

...The birds came first. No, perhaps the bison and other horned-hooved creatures had sensed something wrong earlier, but wings, in the matter of fleeing the approaching horror, were more practical than legs. So — the birds came first. The sound of tens of thousands of wings over Jackson, Wyoming, drew little attention: it was the depths of night — people were sleeping. They woke up because they were jolted. The first time, not too strongly. The second time, many fell out of their beds. Those who rushed to the window did not catch the birds. The feathered ones had already raced away, burning every ounce of strength to put as much distance behind them as possible. But overlaying the strange, low-frequency, growing roar from the windows came another kind of thunder. Those who managed to look in time — saw herds of bison stampeding at full tilt through the streets of the small town. And then nothing was left... ...To the northwest, the edge of the sky was slowly turning red. I sat down on the porch steps and lit a Marlboro. The watch could be read even without backlight: 3:17 AM. Along the whole avenue, lights were coming on. People were waking up. Most were being roused by calls from relatives. Some were belatedly rubbing their eyes after being yanked from Saturday sleep by the howl of sirens. The horizon was filling more and more with red. By now it resembled a rising sun. If that sun had stretched from one edge of the sky to the other. I gauged the distance to Yellowstone and snorted. Fido pressed against my leg and trembled all over. Behind me, on the threshold, my wife appeared like a ghost. She had already put on her tactical vest and stuffed it with ammunition and useful household items. In two pockets, for instance, lay a Russian "individual first-aid kit." The pistol grip of a Benelli peeked over her shoulder, and an AR-15 hung on a strap across her chest. — Where's Mike? — I asked, shifting a bit to make room. — Starting the car and double-checking the emergency stash. Sarah and Mary are packing whatever might be useful from the fridge. — You know, dear, you and I have wonderful kids! The horizon was blazing in its entirety now. A police car shot past, shrieking and flooding everything with light. My wife and I exchanged glances and went into the garage. Mike had already finished his check and was now helping the sisters drag belongings into our RV. In its day, modifying this machine had cost me far more than buying it; but now it had an FVU from a "Bradley," fully sealed windows with bulletproof glass, foil-lined walls protecting from radiation, a beefed-up engine, and a high suspension. In short — it was no longer a home but a bunker on wheels. As designed, in general. We had even managed to attach retractable support "legs" to it, which made it look vaguely like a lunar lander. Sarah carefully brought out the youngest. John slept soundly in his cradle, not even noticing what was going on around him. I waited until our resisting Fido was dragged into the vehicle, and we set off. At the highway on-ramp, of course, there was already a small traffic jam. Nevertheless, we were among the first, and the estimated time of arrival of the pyroclastic cloud gave us enough lead time; meanwhile, however, the tail of the jam was growing impossibly. Michael and Sarah were monitoring the situation from their iPads. I kept the radio on. Apart from insistent urgings to immediately proceed to shelters, there was nothing else. Linda and our youngest, Mary, were preparing breakfast — sandwiches. Obviously, sleep was something to forget about for the next twenty-four hours. I reached out a hand and snapped my fingers — Mike, without looking up from the news, tossed me a can of Adrenaline. — You know, Dad, looks like it's truly massive. No signs of life across the entire north. The last video on YouTube was from York. About ten minutes ago. You can already see the edge of the oncoming clouds. I calculated — about seven hours had passed since the explosion. Yes, York should have been hit by now. This was only the first wave, naturally. We had left the highway over an hour ago, plunging deeper into our southern forest. Few knew of the place we were heading; and among those who did — I really hoped to see them all upon arrival. Another turn, another animal trail — and we ran up against an old concrete bunker, left over from those days when we considered death would come to us from Eastern Europe. There had once been a missile position here. Some thirty years ago, in the framework of disarmament, it had been decommissioned, and the remaining bunkers and equipment had been sold off to a little group of madmen. As in — our fathers. Who, in turn, had bequeathed this place to us. Of course, all those thirty years, work on our little secret had never ceased. To a fair-sized boulder ran a fresh wheel track. By the tyre width — that was Peter and Helen. The others were not yet to be seen. Perhaps they were already inside? I pressed a button on the fob and the rock parted. Already on the way into the underground garage I saw two motorcycles with cartoon skunks painted on them. Evidently Alex and Helga were already here. The Browns and the Vostryakovskys still remained. But, alas, by all appearances — they weren't going to make it. By now even the naked ear could hear the howl filling the heavens. The Yellowstone supervolcano had finally squared its shoulders and was now staking its claim to the Earth.

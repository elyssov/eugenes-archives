Chapter 7

The submarine approached the city without particular concealment. Even before reaching US territorial waters — they had surfaced and were thereafter moving openly, switching on the searchlight (it helped little in the solid pall of ash) and sending radio messages on every possible frequency. About ten kilometres from Port Orford, the radio operators picked up an amateur signal. An American was reporting that he and his family were alive, had received the signal, and were asking to be rescued. The fact that the ones on the line were Russians did not bother him in the least. Half an hour of conversation later it became clear that they had managed to take shelter in an old homestead bunker from the Cold War era, with his wife and three children. Owing to the natural disaster — the pressure door, it seemed, had been twisted out of alignment, so there was no chance of getting out without outside help. First-rank Captain Khryashch rubbed the bridge of his nose. In general, the order from Moscow demanded reconnaissance of the utmost caution. However, the spirit of a Russian sailor simply could not permit him to abandon an entire family in trouble. The captain glanced at his XO. They had been serving together for five years now and had long since understood one another without words. — Yes, sir, Andrei Borisovich. I'll go gather a landing party...

...The city was frankly terrifying. Cracks in the asphalt, collapsed buildings... 1,000 kilometres for a supervolcano turned out to be quite a close distance. In principle, on the approach to the shore the submarine had found itself in a kind of ashen marsh (water mixed with ash falling into it had literally come to resemble in consistency a sort of liquid mud, seriously loading the engines). The city itself was simply buried to the knees, and in places to the chest. The volunteer lads put on "chem-suits," respirators, and took with them closed-cycle apparatus, in case the respirators failed, clogging up. It was a relief that the town was quite small, so the mission promised to be brief. On the embankment, they managed to find an abandoned old, rusted but large pickup with the key in the ignition, into which they loaded a gas-cutting torch. From an offline copy of Yandex maps — they printed an approximate map of the town and tore off... ...The door turned out to be truly massive. Although the owner had long unscrewed the internal locks — opening it was not possible. Four sailors, with the boatswain swearing in Russian, tugged at it for about three minutes, until a bright idea came into the head of one of the lads: searching the ruins (the roof had not withstood the mass of ash) of the house allowed finding several ropes, from which they cobbled together a cable. This cable was attached to the pickup and to the door. At the same time, for reliability, they cut off the outer hinges with the torch. A jerk! The door, not at all elegantly, slammed into the pickup, depriving it of its rear door and lights. The sailors rushed in together, pulling breathing apparatus onto the civilians, not letting them take an extra breath... ...The submarine was returning to the ocean. They needed to get into the field of visibility of the "radio relay" organised by several more ships and submarines as far as the "clean sky." What had been seen — evoked the sensation of despair... And one could say they had gone in vain... If not for the living, though still coughing, family in the medical bay. Five civilian lives saved, first-rank captain Andrei Borisovich Khryashch considered his small but very valuable victory. By this alone — the submarine had justified its existence... ...

Chapter 5

...The satellite slightly changed its position, retargeting its antenna block. Another series of pulses was sent into the impenetrable darkness, swirling beneath the spacecraft. On the face of the once blue-and-white planet a coal-black spot was expanding, stirring tentacles, illuminated by flashes of multiple atmospheric discharges. The pulses, aimed downward, never came back. The cloud, having become a kind of ersatz ECM cover, had successfully absorbed and dispersed all of them. The infrared mode turned out to be no more productive. All that could be said from its data — was that all of North America was burning. Or had been flooded with lava. The reconnaissance satellite saw only a single solid hotspot of high temperature. President Volkov waved his hand and the technicians removed the image from the central screen of the situation room, replacing it with a map of the USA. — What about the White House? — Still the same — no contact. — The aide set the direct-line telephone aside. — You said it was outside the strike zone? — Volkov looked into the eyes of the seismology professor who had been hauled out of bed an hour ago. Accustomed to questioning Moscow State University students rather than answering them, the scholar hesitated, but then advanced a version: — Well, technically, it shouldn't have been hit... But residual seismic waves could have damaged some cables, substations... — And what are we to do? I need to understand whether anyone is alive there, whether we can render any help to people at all. Alexander Dmitrievich, am I understanding correctly that not a single flying machine can get there? — Yes, sir, Mikhail Yevgenievich. A jet aircraft would die even before reaching the visible cloud, we simply don't have piston machines of such range, and even if we did — the filters would quickly choke on the ash and the engine would suffocate. — Electric aircraft? — Battery capacity won't suffice. And solar ones, for obvious reasons, are inapplicable. — What a nightmare... — the President clutched his head in his hands — Does no one have a single idea? — A dirigible? A dirigible and a Stirling engine, or fuel cells outright? — the young assistant professor intervened in the conversation unexpectedly for everyone, causing the commander of the Space Forces to perk up. — Now the young man speaks sense, Mikhail Yevgenievich. No, to the USA, of course, we'll have to send a submarine — we simply won't be able to make a suitable dirigible quickly enough. But the fact that we urgently need to deploy production of them — no need to ask grandma. Very soon the ash will reach us, and then we simply won't be able to fly on anything else. Entire population centres will be cut off... — Right. So begin design, Alexander Dmitrievich. Ivan, what's happening with the conference with Europe?

Chapter 2

Caitlin couldn't sleep, despite it being only March on the calendar — it was insanely stuffy. She tapped the display of her "weather station" and stared in surprise at the temperature scale: it was 107 degrees in the room. Frankly, that was too hot even for July. Kate slipped out of bed — her throat was dry, she desperately wanted to drink, and even with her parched nose she could detect a faint smell of burning. As soon as she stepped out of her isolated room (back when, suffering from frequent insomnia, she had ordered a bedroom in the house without windows and with excellent soundproofing) — Caitlin understood the cause of the high temperatures: the kitchen and living room were bathed in crimson-red light, the tulle curtains were smoking... And toward the city, with the speed of a freight train, was rolling something the girl at first took for a tsunami — only two heartbeats later realising that it was a wave of ash, fire, and stones approaching. And above it, in the distance, towered a colossal column of fire connecting the sky and the earth... ...When the elemental force struck Casper, no one was ready yet. Perhaps that was for the best. There was nowhere to flee anyway. The pyroclastic flow was moving at over 700 kilometres an hour, and a shockwave raced ahead of it. The shockwave crumpled houses as easily as we crumple cardboard boxes. But most people had died even before it — the simple frame houses had been hit by a seismic strike of magnitude 8... And those "Three Little Pigs" lucky enough to survive the first blow had to wait approximately ten more minutes, buried beneath the floors of slightly sturdier concrete buildings, before they were overtaken by a flow of incandescent, toxic, suffocating gases belched by the supervolcano from the very abyss of Hell. Human lungs are not adapted to breathe sulphur, carbon dioxide, and the rest of Mendeleev's bouquet. Anyway, even had they been able to, there remained the problem of temperature. Even after travelling over a hundred and fifty kilometres, the pyroclastic flow had hardly cooled. Breathing something with a temperature of about 1000 degrees Celsius — was, to put it mildly, problematic... ...Surprisingly, the technology proved hardier than the people. A base station placed in the old concrete tower of a NORAD radar system, standing a little apart from the town — by some miracle retained its functionality, as did the buried optical fibre running to it. And ten minutes later, when FEMA had come to itself and switched on the emergency alert system, over the ashen grave that had once been a town there came a barely audible, multi-voiced chime of melted but still living mobiles — unlike their owners... ...The chime of a mobile, set to silent for the night, yanked Simon from a pleasant dream. In it he was admiring a brand-new boat in the Wood's Bay marina, on Flathead Lake. The boat had been spotted in the glossy magazine Cee-Cee read — perhaps the only printed material this silly girl read on principle, apart from recipes on the backs of breakfast cereal boxes. As a result, the first ten seconds, Simon was trying to work out why the hell he had put the boat in Wood's Bay, when Big Fork was much closer to his native Ferndale. The second ten seconds — why the hell a previously turned-off phone was even ringing. And only half a minute later did he think to grab the handset, realising that this "buzz-buzz-buzz" was clearly not for nothing. On the screen, against all common sense, what was burning was not the standard call interface, but the FEMA emblem with text below. — Dear! — drawing out her words capriciously, Cee-Cee, simultaneously stretching, poked him in the side — Turn off your toy at last! I asked you a hundred times, turn the sound off. I worked half the night at the club today. Three privates — let me sleeeeep! — But I didn't turn it on! Some kind of FEMA blast! — How is it ringing then, if you didn't turn it on? — the girl objected reasonably. The thin to total transparency negligee emphasised every curve of the stripper's trained body, leaving not a drop to imagination, so Simon's thoughts nearly drifted, but he managed to concentrate and read the text after all... ...And again, because the first time was impossible to believe: "Attention! Emergency situation. You are advised to take immediate shelter in the nearest secure refuge and, if possible, switch on the radio there for further instructions from FEMA! Yellowstone eruption!"... ...The former Marine spent a few more seconds blinking in confusion, after which his reflexes kicked in: the order had been received and was to be carried out. With ease, Simon grabbed the girl, who shrieked from surprise, slung her over his shoulder (her ample silicone bosom served as a convenient pillow, easing the load) and galloped into the street, toward the door to the "cellar," which in his grandfather's lifetime had in fact been a fully fledged anti-nuclear shelter. As soon as he burst out into the street, the lad realised that the warning was no joke: the sky was blazing, and this heavenly fire was approaching. From the unexpected sight, Cee-Cee finally fell silent and stopped pounding his back with her little fists. Without glancing at the neighbours spilling onto their back yards (who too, in general, somehow weren't particularly interested in the burly Black guy hauling a fragile and practically undressed, curvaceous blonde on his shoulder), he yanked open the "cellar" door with his free hand, and practically tumbled head-over-heels down the stairs. The pressure door was traditionally flung open, so he tossed his bundle, which squeaked at such rough handling, inside, after which he gathered all his strength and with several heaves forced the door to swing on its rusted hinges. Another minute went to blocking it. All this time Cee-Cee sat in silence. But when Simon raced past her into the depths of the room — to start up the generator and the FVU — she could not contain herself: — That's a nuclear war, isn't it? — No. It's a volcano. Yellowstone — have you heard of it? — Oh, yeah. They took us on a school trip there... Right before I dropped out. Of school, not the trip, silly! I didn't drop the trip. It was cool. There were these little things, gay-zee-ro... Or something. Wonder why "gay"? — Because they're called geysers — Simon had been dating Cee-Cee for three months now, had come to love her, in part for her sweet stupidity (let's be honest — he was no genius himself), but sometimes her dumbness drove him mad. Like the time she abandoned his pickup in the middle of the main street because she couldn't park, and her nail appointment was approaching. Still, the very fact that he was not alone here lent the lad strength. He was used to being responsible for someone, and now he had a serious reason to do everything to ensure they both survived this calamity. ...The generator started up a minute before the strike. The FVU howled, driving air through the old but, strangely, still living, if slightly compacted, filters. And then a jolt struck the shelter, hurling them against the wall. First, as it should, came the seismic shock. Stunned, they splayed their arms and legs, trying to hold on to the floor that was rocking like a ship's deck. Suddenly — the rocking stopped, and over their heads came a thud, a screech, and a horrible howl — the shelter's roof element, it seemed, had been sheared off... All that could be hoped was that when this was over — they would be able to dig their way to the surface...

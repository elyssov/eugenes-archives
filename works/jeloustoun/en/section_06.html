Chapter 6

...The ground had stopped shaking about a day ago. The shelter had passed the test with flying colours. Even the old periscope, intended for assessing the consequences of a nuclear strike on a positional area, turned out to be in working order. Linda extended John a mug of hot chocolate, which he intercepted without tearing himself from the periscope. — Well, I'd even say that's beautiful. If it weren't so deadly. Alex, turn off the perimeter lighting. I've already seen everything I could. We shouldn't draw extra attention to ourselves. John folded the handles and the periscope column retreated into the ceiling of the cabin. — So, boys and girls. We of course were preparing for nuclear war, but got this instead. I'll note — better had it been nuclear war. It would be clearer what to do. And the consequences would be multiply lighter. In any case — we successfully survived the harshest phase. — Dad, will there be others? — Michael leaned forward, interested. — There will be. And possibly even worse than this one. In particular, we can proceed from the fact that the surface is no less poisoned than from a nuclear strike. And the rate at which its deadliness declines — will, if anything, be slower. Practically the whole periodic table is out there now. Besides, there is "the darkness of Egypt" out there, and I suspect that we won't see the sun for a very long time. Breathing this air will likewise be EXTREMELY difficult. In particular, for this reason, Alex, Peter and I will need to organise an additional stage of aqua-filtration for the ash. Otherwise our FVU will very quickly give up the ghost — it was designed for slightly other things. — Well, OK. We've survived, we'll redo the FVU, we'll hold out on our stocks for a year, maybe longer. What next? — Michael was not trying to dispute John's opinion, but rather inquiring about far-reaching plans.

— Next? Next we'll come out and go to Brazil. Or strike out for Europe altogether. Here — is scorched earth. There's no point staying. John spread his hands, ending the impromptu briefing. The well-coordinated team of survivalists knew that a sea of work awaited them.

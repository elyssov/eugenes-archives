Chapter 1

The PAZik bus skidded to a halt with a small lurch before the hospital building, leaving deep tracks in the sodden earth. Right behind it, Cat's jeep nearly rear-ended it. Not that Cat was a great individualist, but for games — and especially for tryouts — he preferred to drive himself.

From the bus doors, the stiff-legged fighters clambered out unhurriedly. On the move, they adjusted their chest rigs, checked the "replicas" already pulled from their cases. The commander, Viper, stepped before the formation and cleared his throat sonorously. The squad listened with tripled zeal.

— Right, fighters and noobs. This is a training location. Next game, we'll be re-enacting the hostage rescue in Lima, the 1996 operation. Everyone's read the briefing? So — this is an abandoned hospital. As far as we know, it's more or less similar in layout to the building selected for the game. We're drilling closed-quarters operations. Splitting into two teams. Noobs and veterans split evenly in each. Senior officers will conduct selection after the briefing. Next, safety: the staff and I have already inspected this middle-of-nowhere dump. Preliminary assessment — all clear. Stairs haven't rotted, floors don't collapse, and besides, it was only closed in the mid-nineties, and it's remote enough that the locals haven't stripped it yet. Nevertheless — watch your step, don't play hero, don't jump out of windows, and no other idiocy. Furthermore — I've already warned you, now I'm repeating for the especially gifted. Like you, Cat! Grenades stay on the bus. If anyone doesn't understand, I'll repeat. We're playing in an enclosed space. We have four newcomers. So grenades — on the bus. Just in case. Noobs separately — mobiles, if you don't want to lose them, also dump them on the bus. Same goes for all personal items. We all chipped in for this PAZ specifically for that purpose. In short — fifteen minutes for gear check and team assignment, then we start. Officers with site maps — to me!

Cat stretched lazily, then cursed and climbed into the PAZ to surrender his grenades. Well, at least the ones hanging on his chest rig. In his backpack sat one more — a flashbang. Obviously, using it would be unwise (the commander had long been on the verge of booting the maniac from the team, restrained only by Cat's off-the-charts combat performance), but without it, Cat would have felt utterly defenceless.

Having deposited the grenades, Cat thought for a moment, then strolled to his SUV, fished a backup sidearm from the glove compartment, and with a heavy sigh tucked it into the belt holster under his backpack. Gas-powered replicas weren't exactly famous for reliability, and in certain situations, in tight spaces, a pistol could prove far more effective than an assault rifle. If only for manoeuvrability.

The game had been going for half an hour now. The squad had split into two parts, and now both fives, weaving through the abandoned floors of the ancient psychiatric hospital, were trying to catch and shoot each other. In the grand scheme of things, over this half-hour, nobody had managed to do so yet.

Cat, it must be said, had felt slightly off since the first minutes of the game, from the moment he'd stepped inside the old, bright-red-brick structure that still reeked of formaldehyde and bleach. Hard to say what affected him more — the aforementioned smells, or the general atmosphere of the abandoned building (though this was unlikely; Cat was a seasoned urban explorer). Or maybe it was that the abandonment felt somehow... incomplete. Unlike the gutted buildings where other games were held, in this one — everything was still in place. Beds with restraints stood in the wards, syringes and even

some ampoules lay in the cabinets. Some beds were even made. On the tables lay sheets of paper scrawled with strange, morbidly mesmerising drawings. One portrait in particular — depicting in cheerful watercolour a strange, grim mustachioed man — especially caught Cat's attention. The drawing was extremely talented. One could feel that the artist, having access only to cheap, primitive watercolours, had spent hours painting the tiniest details with the very tip of the brush, letting them dry and returning again to his monotonous labour. The result was a face that was utterly alive. But at the same time — there was something about it... Something unnatural. Simultaneously repulsive yet drawing the eye, the way human remains at a crash scene draw it. Cat wrenched his gaze away from the vile picture with a noticeable, almost physiological effort and spat. The incident didn't escape Flash's notice. Flash was, broadly speaking, Cat's best friend on the team, and just as much of a maniac as Cat himself.

Flash approached the table, picked up the sheet of paper and turned it before his eyes. Disgust mingled with incomprehension on his face. After about a minute, Flash flicked out his "trophy" lighter from his pocket and in one motion set fire to the picture. It caught as though it hadn't been damp in the rain-blown, draught-riddled room with its smashed windows, but had been drying for centuries in the depths of a pharaoh's tomb.

At that very moment — from the direction of the group that had moved somewhat ahead — came the sound of gunfire.

The firefight lasted about twenty seconds. Then both squads rolled back, each into their own branch of the corridor, and began counting losses. Judging by the sounds, neither group was planning to retreat. Cat's squad had two KIAs. The lads got up cursing. By the synchronous sounds from the other side, the score was even. Bee, getting up, stumbled and braced herself against the wall with her hand in its fingerless glove to keep from falling. She was, in fact, the one who first delivered a tirade of profanity, full of outrage and disgust.

— What the hell are the walls smeared with?! — concluded the squad's chief beauty (dragged along by the team more for aesthetic than tactical reasons).

The response to Bee was sniggering from around the enemy's corner. The sniggering, however, very quickly turned to coughing, followed by an equally outraged yell:

— So which bastard farted so thoroughly? They didn't use chemical weapons in the Lima assault, did they?!

— Is that you lot so scared of us that you're already gassing your own? — Cat's squad commander didn't miss the chance.

Cat himself, meanwhile, was working his fleshy nose, sensing that the smell seemed to be coming not only from the opposition. The dubious aroma was intensifying, and appeared to emanate from — the walls!

Weighing the probability of catching a stray BB from around the corner, Cat, out of sheer curiosity, switched on his under-barrel LED. And froze.

Up the wall crept brownish-maroon streaks. And while the nature of the brown streaks, judging by the smell, raised no particular questions, the maroon ones looked very much like... blood. However, the shocking part wasn't that. The main problem, as already mentioned, was that the streams were quite briskly crawling upward, defying gravity. Cat unconsciously stepped forward to get a better look, and barely managed to dodge an arterial-bright scarlet drop that shot ceiling-ward. Looking down, he saw at his feet a small pool of fresh blood, in which someone's severed hand was sluggishly wiggling its fingers. Evidently, he wasn't the only one watching the spot of light from his torch, because next came Bee's bloodcurdling shriek. Cat, hearing this acoustic assault transitioning into ultrasound, actually felt considerable relief. "Not hallucinating!"

— Holy mother! What the hell is this! What the—!!! — The exclamations of the turning fighters were eloquent enough. The commander's shout summed it up:

— Game over! We have an emergency. Everyone, torches on if you have them! Something's wrong here! A horrific shriek from around the enemy corner confirmed that the other side had torches too...

A couple of minutes later, the divided group had clumped into one mighty huddle. Both girls were shaking slightly; the commanders tried to maintain composure. The detachment's commanding officer, who'd been accompanying them as referee, took back his leadership functions. Now, for reconnaissance purposes, he was sweeping the dramatically altered space with his tactical torch beam. The bright light picked out pools of blood here, pieces of someone's bodies there. Moving pieces, it should be noted. The commander was only not screaming in horror by virtue of his reserve of combat experience. Now he felt himself back in the mountains of the North Caucasus, and considered himself obligated to lead the people he was responsible for out of this strange and terrible hole.

Cat had taken up position in the rearguard and was now watching with a somewhat morbid attention one of the pools of blood and filth that had materialised on the corridor wall and was now, as if it were the most natural thing in the world, dripping onto the opposite wall. Across the entire corridor.

— Attention, squad! We move calmly, in a tight group. Girls — put them in the centre. Fast but organised. Everyone clear? Then let's move. Try not to fall behind.

Cat nodded at the commander's words but, out of sheer curiosity, pushed open the door of one of the wards with his barrel. In the window, instead of the sunny, if autumnal, day that had reigned there just an hour ago — stood a wall of rain. Visibility was ten to fifteen metres, and from the sky, sealed by the densest veil of cloud, practically no light filtered down. Cat swore and at a quick march caught up with the group, screwing himself into his usual position at the head of the squad. He'd long since taken his replica off safe. Unlike most players, Cat, on some subconscious level, perceived the game as perhaps too real. And now, even if in his mind he probably understood the foolishness of this naive, childlike belief, deep inside he treated his gas-powered replica as a genuine combat weapon... Probably with the same feelings that little boys, when their parents turn off the bedroom light, pull their plastic rifles closer, ready to shoot the wicked bogeymen as soon as they crawl out from under the bed or from the wardrobe. Cat's rifle was anything but plastic. It was a full-scale, weight-and-dimensions replica of an American M4, but in all other respects the phenomena were, undoubtedly, of the same order.

Inside the hospital, it was darkening rapidly. The darkness advanced from somewhere behind them, from the depths of the corridor. It was dense, tangible, alive. Or perhaps not entirely alive. But unequivocally — it was not simply dark emptiness. It was something alien and hostile.

A light chill seized the squad. From their mouths came small puffs of steam.

The group had been walking briskly toward the exit for about ten minutes now. For the last five, the gasping wheezes of the newcomer, Winnie, had been audible from behind. Cat had assumed from the start that his lungs were weak. Though the lad was built like a fairy-tale hero, he was flabby, pale-skinned, and generally gave the impression of a typical office plankton with good potential. When Cat had first

seen him, he'd decided this was a lad who needed to be run and run again. Then he'd make a good fighter. But for now... Now it was showing.

The group had been walking briskly toward the exit for ten minutes. For the last five, Winnie's gasping had been heard from behind. One more staircase, one more turn. The darkness behind and the unhealthy, spectral lights ahead were ratcheting up the fear. People were still holding it together — like all herd animals, having bunched into a pack, they could resist the breath of the supernatural more effectively than alone. But each one felt at their back the breath of an unknown death. And that was quite a good incentive to move their limbs as intensively as possible. An additional incentive came when the under-barrel torches first began to flicker and then went out entirely... Flash, it seemed, was the first to grasp what this might mean, and raising his "Kalashnikov" overhead, tried to fire a burst. Instead of the spit of a few plastic BBs and the whirr of the mechanism, the replica produced only a dry click. Cursing, Flash slung the assault rifle over his back and drew the gas-blowback Colt from his holster. Apparently, the old friend shared Cat's childlike faith.

The commander also understood the two maniacs' body language and, on the move, surreptitiously clicked his radio's PTT. As he'd already expected — the radio showed not the slightest sign of life.

After roughly another ten minutes, having once overshot the right turn and backtracked practically under the nose of the advancing darkness, to the ragged squealing of both squad girls — the commander finally led his people into the lobby. That's when they discovered that for the past five minutes or so, nobody had heard Winnie's wheezing. Exchanging glances, the maniacs moved to the tail of the column. The commander, meanwhile, quickly rechecked the headcount. All fighters were present. Except Winnie.

— Right, everyone, we're going through the door. Quick and without stopping, but no bottleneck. Don't linger near the building — move straight to the bus.

— Cap, we've got a man missing here, you know! — Cat objected.

— We're all at risk of going missing right now. First we get the people out. Then we come back for him.

— You say that like he's not a person — Flash snorted, spitting.

— I've got nine souls on my hands, counting you two. We'll argue later.

— Right, how about this. Flash and I will look for Winnie. You get the people out and organise backup. As soon as we find him, we'll try to get him out.

Viper hesitated for mere seconds. The commander was a realist, but he wasn't accustomed to abandoning his people without any chance at all. So the proposal was a perfectly acceptable compromise.

— Proceed. But wait one minute. Everyone — the commander raised his voice — hand over your chemical light sticks if you have any. The lads will need them more.

A couple of minutes later, Flash and Cat were stuffing thin plastic tubes of luminous compound into their pockets. Behind them, the squad was quickly filtering through the wide doors.

In the first second, Bee didn't even understand what had happened. She stepped through the door, felt the wind and raindrops on her face, then felt her head spin as if from a good carousel ride, took several unsteady steps and fell into Dan's arms. Already falling, she saw Viper — who'd been last out — stumbling back inside through the door with a dazed expression.

Inside.

They were still in the lobby. Only now the awful darkness was gone. Though "normal" would have been a stretch. On the wall, in a frame, hung a portrait of Lenin, next to a grateful "Prayer for the Healing of Afflictions of the Mind." Opposite the entrance stood a nurse's station, on which burned an antique kerosene lamp with its wick turned up to full. Behind the station, with an eerie creak, a tower clock was turning its hands backward. Underfoot lay dozens of patient files. Some were leather-bound, parchment, with inscriptions penned by hand with a quill. Some were bureaucratic, cardboard, peppered with pre-revolutionary orthography. Right under Bee's foot lay a grey cardboard folder with the faceless inscription "Case No." and a hand-filled number.

And every folder was breathing.

Some opened and closed rapidly, like fish thrown ashore. Others — calmly, like the chest of a knight sleeping a righteous sleep. There was the breathing of a schizophrenic in the grip of an especially acute episode. And the ragged breathing of a man suffering withdrawal in the absence of a life-giving drop of ethanol... At some point, Bee realised she was beginning to distinguish faces, growing increasingly three-dimensional, as if straining to push through the covers of the folders.

The girl was entirely overcome with fear, and didn't even try to resist when someone grabbed her elbow and shoved her behind the backs of the lads, who bristled with useless but confidence-giving barrels.

— Right. We exit in groups of four — Viper commanded. — First out: Dan, Bee, Maverick, Jurgen. Arrow, Harp and Dog — stay with me. As soon as the door shuts — we exit too. Clear?

— Copy! — The group answered in unison, though on the thinnest possible nerve.

— Then go. First team — move! Harp, Arrow — cover them. If they get spat back into the lobby.

After another thirty seconds, satisfied that nobody had come flying back through the lobby doors, the commander ordered withdrawal. The remainder of the squad slipped through the door, Viper bringing up the rear. They slipped through — only to find themselves emerging from the doors of an unknown ward on an unknown floor. At the very least, a placard above the door couldn't be found. Now there were two squads of four wandering the hospital, and a pair of suicidal volunteers trying to find and rescue another missing person. The group had committed the classic, mid-budget horror movie mistake. It had split up. Or been split. Possibly the latter.

Cat had been weaving through the corridors for a good half-hour now. Said corridors presented an insane tangle more reminiscent of Escher's paintings. Actually — why "reminiscent." He and Flash had literally just emerged onto a flight of stairs that managed to lead back to itself. Describing it was difficult. Though equally difficult was simply grasping the meaning of the phenomenon. All that remained was to accept it and, trying not to consciously process the mind-breaking spectacle — sidle away somewhere far from it.

The darkness, after most of the group had gone outside, had retreated. However, in its place came a kind of eternal, stretchy-as-flu-mucus twilight and gradually intensifying sounds rolling in like a murky, heavy wave. Among the sounds, one could already distinguish someone's groans, wailing, the clink of medical instruments. At the boundary of visibility, vague shadows had begun to manifest — hobbling, crawling, dragging, for some reason, severed limbs... Honestly, Cat had already regretted volunteering to save the hapless newbie ten times over. On the other hand — honestly, he couldn't have done otherwise. Perhaps he was just an ordinary office drone who happened to be into guns and everything adjacent. But perhaps because he had no actual combat experience yet, he had not yet lost those feelings of martial nobility and idealism that in actual combat atrophy fairly quickly and, strictly speaking, don't much contribute to their bearer's survival.

And so — he couldn't abandon one of his own. Which didn't prevent him from remembering the hapless newbie with extensive profanity and periodically devising the cruel punishments he'd subject him to if he found him alive.

The first of the "patients" was spotted by Dan. A strange, humanoid figure burst from a ward they were passing and lunged at Bee. Dan barely managed to shove the girl off the giggling creature's trajectory, and Jurgen finished its run with a devastating butt-stroke of his Chinese "Kalashnikov" to the head. Unfortunately, the replica did not survive this treatment and fell apart right in Jurgen's hands. The fighter gazed mournfully at the wreckage of his not-inexpensive sporting equipment, while Dan stared with interest at the creature that had fallen at his feet.

It was clearly human. Though Dan could not have imagined a more repulsive human being. A hairless, somehow lumpy skull, with bristly tufts sticking out here and there, somehow perched on a long, scrawny, chicken-like neck with a disproportionately large Adam's apple. Enormous brow ridges, combined with a sloping forehead and a massive, drooping jaw, completed the picture to clinical idiocy.

The imbecile was dressed in a filthy grey straitjacket, whose tied sleeves wrapped around his body and fastened at the chest. Straps from the same jacket immobilised the torso and pelvis. So it remained somewhat unclear how he could have been running.

While the lads examined him, the strange person suddenly snapped his eyes open. The eyes were insane, looking in different directions.

And then — he vanished, dissolving into a dense, thick, whitish fog that flowed around the people's legs and seeped somewhere into the floor.

Viper had barely caught his breath and wiped his sweating forehead with a torn sleeve. Beside him, Arrow groaned weakly — the "nurse" had slammed her into the wall. Oddly enough, what helped was an ordinary, mass-produced knife. When THIS stepped literally out of the wall — a desiccated mummy with long, withered, root-twisted limbs, grotesquely clad in a short medical smock with a plunging neckline revealing black, sunken skin from which rib fragments protruded — nobody managed to react properly. The mummy seized the girl and began pulling her into the wall. And Arrow — began seeping into it! Dog, who rushed to help, caught the monster's right hand full across the face and rolled across the floor, covered in dried grime and stains whose origin Viper tried not to think about. Arrow took advantage of the loosened grip and tried to wrench free. At first unsuccessfully, but then her hand slipped inside the hole in the "nurse's" chest. The monster howled and flung Arrow into the opposite wall. The girl hit it with the back of her head and slowly slid down. Meanwhile, Viper had finally freed his knife and drove it straight into the creature's forehead. To his surprise, the knife went into the flesh like butter. Moreover, the monster's flesh lost its solidity at the knife's touch, "smoked" as it were, then finally crumbled into fine, powdery black ash that settled on the floor. Victory? Perhaps. In one battle. And at what cost? Two fighters appeared to be down. Dog was nursing a right arm hanging at an unnatural angle. Arrow was out cold entirely. On the other hand — the monsters were mortal, and that was encouraging. Viper weighed the monstrous stamped metal-ceramic "Bowie knife" in his hand and forced himself to stand. He had to get his people out. A plan was already forming in his mind — find the way to the lobby, set up a defence there and wait for any break, any chance to slip away... Or at least send word...

When two of those things leapt at them simultaneously, something clicked in the airsoft players' heads, so that instead of being terrified by the sight of humanoid creatures running now along the floor, now along the wall, in torn and blood-spattered old-fashioned hospital gowns — Cat and Flash raised their toy weapons and opened a withering barrage. And weren't even surprised to find that under the hail of "white cranberries," the monsters jerked as if hit by real bullets. However, they were finally laid to rest only after half a magazine from Cat's rifle and Flash's fully emptied Colt. The sign on the dangling, smashed placard overhead read "Surgical Ward." Cat and Flash exchanged glances and, stepping carefully sideways, entered... Though not before Flash reloaded the pistol.

Before the friends' eyes opened an expansive arcade, littered with rubbish and the same pools of blood. From behind one of the doors, a beam of light filtered, and someone's groans could be heard. Along with a strange, steady buzzing.

Cat rose on tiptoe, reloaded his magazine, then thought better of it, and carefully extracted the flashbang from his backpack. After that, he flowed up to the operating theatre door.

He suppressed the scream only by an incredible effort of will.

On an antique operating table, the kind Cat knew only from newsreel footage of tsarist times, Winnie was crucified. His head was fixed in a strange wire frame. From the half of his face turned toward the door, strips of skin had been neatly peeled and braided into a plait. The eye had been just as neatly extracted from its socket and mounted on a stand, turned to face the door. Judging by the fact that the eye was still connected by a taut string of nerves to the poor man's head, and the pupil was alternately dilating and contracting, Winnie could still feel it. Winnie's left arm stuck absurdly from a tin basin placed against the operating table, like an exotic flower in an exotic vase. His shoulder ended in a hideous, hastily stitched stump from which protruded an enormous lancet gleaming with surgical steel. Wires ending in electrodes ran to his temples. The wires themselves led somewhere beyond the field of view. But the most terrible thing was that on the far side of the table, over the novice's right hand, three-armed, withered creatures were at work (two arms grew from their shoulders, while the third extended from somewhere behind the back, looming like a scorpion's stinger). The creatures wore blood-spattered smocks, once perhaps white but now rather

brown. On their peeling skulls, with bones showing through gaps in the skin, spotless white starched "Dr Dolittle" caps with red crosses perched in ghastly dissonance. Overcoming his disgust and peering closer, Cat noticed the creatures had no lower jaws. Instead, growing directly from the dead flesh, protruded glass multi-millilitre syringes, monstrous mandibles. During the operation, the syringes moved. Apparently, the creatures were talking, communicating amongst themselves. While the lads watched in horrified fascination, a rather pretty golden-haired nurse appeared from beyond the field of view, clutching yet another enormous syringe, and approached the operating table from the head end. But the illusion didn't last long. The nurse took aim to inject the syringe's contents (some monstrous bloody mess in which something writhed and squirmed) into Winnie's head, and for an instant turned three-quarters toward the door. Cat heard loud retching beside him. Honestly, he himself barely held it together. The face that had been pretty in profile turned out, from the front, to be a maggot-and-fly-covered mask of meat devoid of eyes. However, the unknown creatures apparently had keen hearing. At the very least, they were visibly alerted by the retching and turned toward the door. The nurse, setting aside her revolting injection, headed briskly for the door.

There was no more time to lose, so Cat pulled the pin from the grenade, lobbed it through the door, grabbed his friend by the rig strap and sprinted away. They could no longer do anything for Winnie. Except finish him off. But for that, something more than an airsoft replica would be needed.

The clarity came instantly. Just a moment ago, behind Jurgen's back, they'd dragged Maverick into the wall, and he himself was now carrying on his shoulder the desperately screaming girl who'd twisted her ankle, trying to flee from three naked but genderless people (where genitals should have been, each had a hideous triangular scar, stitched with a crude seam, and the chest was a neutral "first" size that could have belonged to a woman or an intemperate man), each missing one hand. In the other hand, however, they gripped their severed hands (there was no system here: one was missing the right hand, two the left) by the protruding bone. And the severed hands were fully functional, clenching and unclenching, trying to grab the fugitives.

The creatures' faces were likewise devoid of any hint of gender. Heads were completely hairless. Thin lips — sewn shut. Instead of eyes — gaping bloody wounds, though on the forehead, where Eastern teachings place the third eye, blazed a cut, clearly made in haste. In this gaping wound, an ordinary human eye was crudely fixed, rolling wildly in its unfamiliar socket.

Maverick, covering the retreat, hurled a chair at the trio. Two of the freaks caught the chair with their severed hands and with some incredibly synchronised motion hurled it back. Maverick barely managed to shield himself with his arms and... Everything passed. They were standing on the third floor of an abandoned hospital. Through an open ward door, the sun shone brightly. It was a clear autumn day. Papers rustled underfoot. The lads rushed to the windows. Outside, just a hundred metres away, two jeeps (the commander's and Cat's) and the PAZ were visible. A hundred metres... But the entire hospital stood between them and the entrance... And the darkness could return at any moment.

The guys exchanged looks. It was about ten metres to the ground. In principle, not that much... But below the window was an abandoned garden, covered with tree stumps and upturned lumps of petrified clay. The lighter Jurgen shuddered. Looked at the others again. Maverick and Bee looked back at him. Then Maverick reached into some inner pocket of his rig and produced a small syrette.

— This is promedol. On the jump — you'll definitely break your legs. So — you'll need this. As for me — jumping's pointless. I'm too heavy.

— And you guys? Maybe we should all try jumping?

— There's only one dose. And Bee's been afraid of heights her whole life. It's got to be you. If you can break free — call for help. Anyone, anyhow. All right? And we'll try to survive in here. Who knows — maybe we got lucky, maybe the nightmare's over?

That the hope was futile was clear even before Mav finished speaking. Outside the window, the same dismal fog was visibly gathering. There was no time left. Jurgen grabbed the syrette, ditched his backpack, and with a cry of "Geronimo!!!" dove through the broken window frame... And already falling, he realised he'd passed through phantom-but-real glass, behind which remained his friends' faces. He still managed to tuck, take the impact on his feet, hear the treacherous crack, and roll onto his side — then came the sharp pain and reality blinked out for a second.

He wasn't out for long. He came to from monstrous pain. Looked at the bone shards protruding from both legs. With a kind of detachment, injected the narcotic into his forearm. Then, as if executing a pre-programmed routine, tightened the rig straps around his legs, stanching the blood. Waited a minute. Realised the pain had ceased to exist for him. Rolled onto his elbows and methodically, like a toy crawling soldier, began his endless journey toward the vehicles. He didn't yet know how he would climb the steps into the bus, but his whole world had contracted to this simple, comprehensible goal.

The last autumn sun shone brightly, almost like summer; birds still sang in this remote place, having forgotten about fear and about the south. And from the abandoned building of the former psychiatric hospital, toward the bus, a man in camouflage crawled. Crawled, leaving a bloody trail, dragging legs held on only by scraps of muscle and skin. Crawled not for himself. Crawled for those who remained in the hell behind him. Compared to them, he was already in paradise. But for their sake, he had no right to let himself stop. Monstrous pain broke through the promedol blockade. His brain was being torn apart by the signal from his ruined legs, yet he crawled on and on, and the sun, indifferent and warm, lit the clearing...

— Gamma Team, emergency deployment!!! Repeat — emergency deployment! Team to the helipad!

The metallic voice of the alert rang through the entire Moscow base of DPPI. Serov, sitting at a table in the cafeteria, nearly choked on his black caviar sandwich. It was annoying, to say the least. Primarily for the impression it made — he wasn't dining alone, but with Marina. Yes, of course, romance on duty was frowned upon, and Marina had recently lost someone close... But you can't order the heart, and besides, the team commander himself, at that same moment, in that same café, was drinking coffee with Olga. And if with Marina, formally, this was a friendly catch-up, then Oleg and Olga were on a perfectly official date. And not even their first, which was the most interesting part.

However — if the general alert was used, then (Ilya already knew this) the matter was very serious. It was a question of life and death. Or rather — lives and deaths. In short — no time for philosophy.

He and Marina leapt from their seats instantly. Oleg even managed to peck Olga on the cheek in farewell, then picked up speed so as not to fall behind anyone, nearly bowling over Galatea, who barely managed to dodge while miraculously keeping her food tray balanced. The former anomaly let loose a volley of the choicest profanity after him, which Oleg, on the run, couldn't quite make out. But Pavel was first. He was already seated in the chopper's cabin, turning his UGT unit over in his hands.

The chopper lunged upward before the team had even settled in. Oleg and Ilya, accustomed to such things, didn't bat an eye. But Marina had to be caught by Ilya. Not that this provoked any protest from him. Quite the opposite.

While the helicopter gained altitude and locked onto the correct heading, General Samoilov's face appeared on the team's UGTs. By DPPI's venerable tradition, briefings for Moscow operational groups were usually delivered by him personally.

— Right, troops. Fifteen minutes ago, the duty officer for the Northern District received a signal from citizen Talkov, Yuri Semyonovich. Twenty-eight years old. Member of the national airsoft team. You know — those guys who never grew out of playing war. Anyway, these idiots staged a shoot-em-up on the grounds of a former psych ward. An ANCIENT (the general very distinctly emphasised the word) psych ward. Pre-Korsakov era. ("Oh dear," Marina murmured knowingly.) If you're brief — from those times when the methods of treatment included, for example, the therapeutic amputation of limbs. Who knows — maybe the madness lives in the limbs? Funny thing is — it even sometimes worked. Though, I suspect, largely due to the stress the body experienced. And possibly fear. If you don't get better — what'll they saw off next? — The general was cracking jokes, but his eyes were frozen. Apparently, the situation was truly critical.

Anyway — if you boil down the victim's stream of consciousness — his entire squad is still inside. He himself jumped out a window when a chance came, to call for help. By the way, Marina — provide him medical assistance at your level. "Conventional medicine," as I understand it, can't save his legs anymore.

— General, sir, what awaits us inside? — Oleg was composed and ready to act swiftly and decisively.

And from his chest burst two blades, on which the creature that had once been Winnie lifted the agent like a pitchfork. Pavel was still twitching, sliding down the steel lancets toward the monster's stumps, while Cat was already punching precise holes in the creature's chest with the pistol. Winnie absorbed about a dozen hits before falling first to his knees, then face-down. Cat approached the agent, holding the weapon in both hands, ready to finish the monster. Winnie showed no signs of life. The agent — also. No pulse at wrist or neck. Cursing, Cat picked up the agent's strange rifle, holstered the pistol, and went to check on Harp.

Ten minutes later, they stood before the hospital doors. But apparently the two remaining agents hadn't yet destroyed their "core," whatever it was. Which meant nothing but holding position and praying the ammo would last. Especially as someone's stealthy footsteps were already approaching...

The "pulse lost" icon with Pavel's number popped into Oleg's UGT field of view as they reached the attic. Before them — one scuffed green door with a round brass knob and a brass plaque reading "Professor Polukhov Ye.P." Receiving the message, Oleg tried to call Pavel. Naturally — nothing. A small hope remained that the report was merely a glitch from the barely-coping device in the paranormal torrent. But Oleg's sixth sense already knew it was true.

Lowering his head like a bull — he kicked the door open. Ilya, covering the rear, flowed in behind.

Beyond — a fairly spacious office. At the far end, a massive desk covered in green baize, lit by a kerosene lamp. Bookshelves along the walls. Several comfortable antique armchairs before the desk. In the wall — a large, arched mansard window. And before the window stood a figure. Or rather — the embodiment of the horror that currently reigned in the hospital.

It didn't even glance at the newcomers. Merely waved a hand in their direction. Ilya seemed to stumble, then a wild grin crept across his face.

— It's all about black hole theory. They pull out the brain. — The operative mumbled, a long thread of drool dangling from the corner of his mouth.

Oleg fired a burst at the desk. The desk crumbled to the familiar ash. The office's occupant, however, turned to the agent with noticeable interest. Had the agent been here from the start, he would have noticed the enormous resemblance between the ghost and the portrait Flash had burned.

— Strange... You haven't gone mad, even at the heart of my power... This is... An interesting case, I think... I shall attend to you...

— You won't attend to anyone. What holds you here, spirit?

— Spirit? There are no spirits... I work... I must heal everyone... They are all sick... Even those who think themselves healthy... You simply don't know it yet... Five cc of novocaine to the heart. Nitrous oxide — fifteen injections... And you will be healed... All of you... The pain will go away...

— Hmm... A deranged ghost... Something's holding you here... Wonder what... — The UGT had finally failed, so Oleg had to remove it. He could already feel the monstrous psychic currents around him through his very skin. But he was a "safe" — a person with psychic immunity. This had its downsides. But in this situation, also its advantages. Not waiting for the undead thing to grasp all the nuances, Oleg jabbed the stunner into his compromised partner's neck. Ilya grunted and lost consciousness for the regulation twenty minutes.

— You are clearly antisocial, young man... — the ghost creaked — I shall prescribe for you, to begin with, wet-sheet wrapping. That very often helps... Though... Your case may be advanced...

— No time to chat with you — Oleg waved him off, scanning the office. Nothing obvious. Except... Oleg spotted a door in the far wall, nearly identical in colour to the wallpaper. In fact, if time hadn't peeled back some of the paper, he might not have noticed it at all. Oleg strode quickly to the hidden room. Behind him, a door slammed. Without turning, he fired a burst. Someone squealed in a female voice. As he'd supposed — the hospital had accumulated a great many "nurses."

A bookcase crashed directly in his path. Another swayed. Oleg cleared the remaining distance in two leaps and shouldered through the door. The flimsy plywood gave, and Oleg rolled into the secret room, flowing into stance.

Directly before him — a sarcophagus. A glass sarcophagus containing a desiccated mummy. Judging by the goatee — this was indeed Professor Polukhov. On a table — or rather, a tall carved stand at the sarcophagus's head — sat a sealed jar containing a brain in formaldehyde. Oleg didn't even need to examine the mummy's head to know whose brain it was. In the front corner, beside the brain, thick white smoke began rising from the very floor, twisting into dense, intertwined ropes. Ten seconds — and between the brain and the agent stood the ghost (and judging by the materialisation, it was precisely a ghost) of the professor. Everything happening in the hospital fell into a completely different light.

— No! I won't let you do this! They are all still sick! I cannot release them! — The professor even thrust out his palm for emphasis — I am treating them! I am caring for them! I want only the best for them! When they are healed — I shall release them all! But the treatment cannot be interrupted!

— What, even your staff is sick? The doctors, the nurses? — Oleg grinned predatorily, extracting from beneath his coat a device resembling a thermos, unscrewing the cap, and attaching another piece drawn from his belt — one with a grip and trigger.

— You mustn't! Don't you dare! I WON'T ALLOW IT!!!! — The spirit acquired a terrifying solidity.

Outside the hospital windows, in the lobby — dawn broke. Another remission of the deranged asylum.

Cat didn't wait a second. The moment he confirmed the vehicles and helicopter were visible through the windows — he kicked his friends toward the doors and sprinted after them, the female agent slung across his shoulders. No time for niceties. Seconds later — they tumbled as one into the mud before the hospital steps. For them, it was over. Already on his back, face turned up to the still-warm sun, convulsively gulping the so-delicious, real air — Cat heard the sound of approaching sirens. The cavalry, it seemed, was close.

Oleg finished assembling the single-shot plasma emitter. The weapon was experimental, issued by the DPI to operational group commanders for field testing. And the current situation was ideal for precisely this weapon.

Oleg raised the plasma gun, and the shot of hot plasma obliterated the deranged brain. Vaporised it, taking a fair chunk of wall with it. Beyond the wall — bright sunshine. The nightmare was over.

— Thus, Kirill Mikhailovich, I must conclude that we erred in our initial premise. This was not a collective egregore of patients' emotions and suffering. This was a haunting of the highest level. And thank God the possession of the building was not fully effective. It seems the professor never managed to reach full power.

— So, an ensouled building... Hmm... Not the most common case, I must say. What was the trigger?

— We interrogated the survivors, DPI investigators studied the obtained materials... In short — it appears the professor was mummified by his closest students, per his own wish. Then somehow the mummy was forgotten. And so he lay there gathering dust for nearly a century and a half. Apparently, the first manifestations came earlier. At any rate, studying the patient records reveals similar elements of persecution delusions, complaints of specific hallucinations in many patients... Then, in the mid-seventies, someone with the Gift was admitted. Most likely unconscious of it. Unfortunately, we have very little data, so we couldn't determine the identity. But this unknown hero managed to capture the professor's spirit and bind it in a watercolour portrait. A talented mage, no doubt about it. After that, all manifestations ceased. Nevertheless, all the souls the professor had stolen somehow survived. So when these idiots destroyed his flimsy prison...

— The thoroughly deranged professor broke beyond a mere pesky ghost and seized the entire hospital. Well done, Oleg. Excellent work... How are the lads?

— Pavel was killed covering the withdrawal of the wounded and civilians... Well, you know... Marina has come round. Ilya won't leave the med bay. By the way, she seems to remember who saved her. Another office romance brewing... Christ, why is it always like this with biotics?

— A natural process, Oleg. We have to approach it with understanding. Though, if things go too far between them, we may have to separate them into different groups. My condolences again about Pavel. Right... And what about this, commando-survivalist?

— Lev Bratslavsky? "Cat"? Interesting case — bearer of True Faith. Was so deeply convinced of his weapon that he actually managed to use airsoft gear as combat weaponry against phantoms. Plus — extremely stable psyche. The only civilian who came through all this with his head intact. So right now he's being run through SIB checks, put through DPI testing, and if everything checks out, I plan to petition for his inclusion in my group.

— Very well — send me the reports on "Cat." We'll see. Psychic resilience and solid combat training are valuable in our line of work...

The general watched Oleg leave, waited for the door to close... Stood, walked with a heavy, old man's gait to the wall safe, extracted from it an inexpensive bottle of Kutuzovsky cognac and a glass... Poured... Looked at the ceiling, blinked a couple of times... "Another one," flashed through his mind.

"May the earth rest lightly upon you..." thought Samoilov, drank, and put the glass back in the safe... Few knew that Pavel had been his nephew... And nobody needed to know...

Life went on, and the agents still stood guard over people's lives. And the fact that five out of ten had been saved was already an excellent result — a price for which the life of one agent was entirely acceptable...

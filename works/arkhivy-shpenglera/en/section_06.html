Three Months Later

Berlin. Late autumn. The kind of cold rain that reminds the city it was built on a swamp.

Gerhard sat at the kitchen table of his flat with a mug of strong coffee and the morning post. Cadence, in pajamas and large fluffy socks, had her feet in his lap and was eating cereal with the focused intensity of a cat. A copy of his latest novel, fresh from the publisher, lay between them with a yellow note from his editor: Bestseller list, second week. Stop refusing interviews.

Three letters mattered.

The first, from Penny in Monmouthshire, with a photograph of Roger and Lucky in front of the Grey Lamb. Roger was taller. Lucky was fatter. Penny wrote that Roger had asked when Uncle Gerhard would visit again and whether he would bring a sword this time. I told him you usually bring a sword. He was satisfied.

The second, from Susanne in Strasbourg, brief: The mirror confirms what I knew. Forty more years given back. Thank you. The grimoire was not all that was in that house — there is a small icon in the upstairs chapel that has begun to weep. Come when you can. There is no urgency. There is only the icon. Gerhard set the letter aside for the trip to Strasbourg he would not put off long.

The third, from Moscow. Vasya Berski. One sheet of paper, one paragraph: Ivan is in a monastery in Karelia. He writes that he is well. I have visited once. He will not leave for a long time. Father is angry with me for permitting this. I am not asking your opinion. I am writing to tell you that the third coffin's girl, Anya, married last week. Quietly. The man is good. She asked me to tell you. We all asked me to tell you. Thank you, Gerhard.

Gerhard folded the letter carefully and put it in the drawer with the others.

Cadence looked up from her cereal. "All good news?"

"All news," he said. "Which, in our line of work, is much the same thing."

She thought about this for a moment, decided it was true, and went back to her cereal.

Outside, the rain kept coming down. Inside, the Seal between his shoulder blades was warm, not hot, and the warmth was familiar enough by now to feel almost like company.

He opened the next letter in the stack — return address Belgrade — and began to read.

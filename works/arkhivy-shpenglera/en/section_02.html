Pendant

Susanne de Châtillon, 29, recent widow of Baron Jacques de Châtillon, came to Strasbourg seeking a consultant recommended by her friend Michel. The man before her — Gerhard von Spengler — looked nothing like a heroic savior: medium height, medium age, with rebellious blond hair and anachronistic round-lensed glasses without diopters.

"My husband died two months ago. From old age. At twenty-eight."

"Hmm, interesting. The coroner?"

"Suggests dioxin. But people don't age from dioxin, and progeria was ruled out immediately. Skin became flaccid, dry. Teeth fell out. Hair thinned and grayed. In six months he aged forty years."

"And why have you come now?"

"What happened to him is beginning to touch me too."

Gerhard poured a few drops of papally-blessed holy water on her hand — two small spots burned slightly, leaving white marks. "And not friend, not foe, but so" — the result was ambiguous. She wasn't possessed or a demon (no serious burns), but her aura held an edge of inhuman crimson.

At Château Cocrouge, a rococo wedding-cake of an estate, the late baron's interior style was much more severe — dark oak panels, a stuffed boar, dark velvet carpets. Crossing the threshold, Gerhard immediately sensed the presence of Darkness. The illusory smell of fresh blood with a hint of decay touched his nostrils. Perhaps the grimoire Libro Secretorum Daemonicum was indeed kept somewhere here...

The trail led to a pendant the baron had given Susanne — a delicate antique piece that had been in the de Châtillon family for generations. Beautiful. Drinking life. The baron had worn it before her; in his final months he had pressed it on her, perhaps unconsciously seeking to extend his own dwindling time by transferring the curse.

In the cellar archives, Gerhard found the family records: a 17th-century ancestor had commissioned the pendant from a wandering scholar — really a necromancer. Successive generations of de Châtillons had died young, each leaving the pendant to the next heir or spouse.

The grimoire itself was hidden behind a false panel in the baron's study, leather-bound and bound with iron clasps that hissed at Gerhard's touch.

"Susanne. Take off the pendant. Now."

She tried. It wouldn't come off — the clasp had welded itself, the chain refused to break. Gerhard applied a counter-binding ritual using the Feather of Michael as catalyst. The pendant flared with a thin scream and dropped from her neck onto the silver tray he had prepared. He sealed it inside a lead-lined box.

"The pendant has fed on twenty-three generations of your husband's family. You happened to wear it longest among recent owners besides him — the curse latched to you instead of resetting."

"Can I be cured?"

"The decay will stop. Lost years won't return. You have a normal lifespan ahead."

Gerhard took the grimoire and the lead box as his fee. Susanne, looking ten years older than she had a week earlier but no longer aging by the day, walked him to his car.

"Why this work, Gerhard?"

"Because somebody has to. And because I find it."

"I think it finds you."

He smiled and didn't answer.

Kashchey

The trail led not to a village but to Moscow.

Cadence — Gerhard's familiar, a former "daughter of Lilith" he had bound a year earlier and who now lived in his Berlin flat in the form of a petite, deceptively sweet-faced young woman — felt the door being shoved. Her playful instincts won; she let it open and tumbled to the floor in a theatrical posture of helpless secretary while mentally pinging her master.

Two thugs in cheap leather coats and red moccasins came in looking for "the sorcerer." Cadence held one with her actual strength (which was significantly more than her appearance suggested), let the other be slammed against the wall by Gerhard's Word of Wind, and shifted briefly into her armored princess form to make the surviving guest reconsider his approach.

The thugs belonged to Sumilin — Russian oligarch, oil and metals, whose daughter Vasya Berski had vanished three days earlier from a guarded hotel suite. The bodyguards had heard a clap, then could not enter — "animal terror." A chill remained in the room afterward. Frost on the windows.

"Undead. If there were no cold, I'd say Ancients. Undead is bad, but there's a chance."

Vasya was 25, an economist with three degrees, fluent in five languages, engaged for years to Ivan Samoylov — son of Sumilin's chief competitor, a union meant to merge two empires. Both fathers blessed the match. The wedding was set for this summer.

"Vampire" was Gerhard's first hypothesis. Mid-aged vampire, gathering a harem from the heiresses of the country. Sumilin paid 10,000 euros in apology for the broken door and gave Gerhard full access.

The hotel suite still smelled of ice and old sorrow. Through the Glasses, Gerhard read the residue of the Path of Shadow — a thing only a few classes of undead could open. Not vampire residue. Something older.

The case opened wider with every day. Three more young women vanished across Moscow, each from a wealthy family, each engaged or recently married. Each abduction left the same chill. Each chill carried, beneath the cold, a slow patient appetite — not for blood but for years.

"Kashchey," Cadence said, when Gerhard described the trace. She rarely volunteered. "In the old folklore. The deathless one. He kept his death in an egg, in a duck, in a hare, in a chest, on an oak."

"And in the old folklore he stole brides."

"Always."

Ivan Samoylov — Vasya's fiancé, who had been waiting at the offices when Gerhard arrived — was not, as Sumilin assumed, a useful young man with grief on his face. He was a useful young man with grief on his face and a thread of cold under his aura that Gerhard saw the moment he looked through the Glasses properly. Faint, recent, not his own. Imprinted on him by proximity.

Gerhard didn't say so yet. He let Ivan accompany the investigation. He let Ivan be helpful. He let Ivan, at one point, hold the lead-lined box containing the de Châtillon pendant — and watched Ivan's hand twitch when the lid cracked open a fraction.

"You've handled a cursed object before, Ivan."

"My mother was superstitious. She kept icons."

"Icons don't react like that."

Ivan didn't answer.

The unraveling came in a long night at a country estate Sumilin owned outside Moscow. Gerhard, Cadence, and Ivan went together — Sumilin had been told to remain in the city, with bodyguards, away from anything Gerhard might do. The estate was where Vasya had spent summers as a child. The estate had a chapel. The chapel had a crypt. The crypt had been resealed, recently, by someone who knew what they were doing.

"Ivan. When did your father die?"

"Three years ago. Heart."

"And when did Kashchey first come to you?"

Ivan looked at him across the candle-lit nave and did not pretend not to understand.

"After. The grief was very loud. He said he could quiet it. He said he could give me what I wanted — the merger, the empire, Vasya — and he asked only that I find him brides. He said his old ones had withered. He said the bargain was so small."

"How many."

"Four. Vasya was the fourth."

"Where."

"Below us."

The crypt opened to Gerhard's seal-key (the Spengler Seal had its uses; old Russian sanctified stone responded to it as a relative, not a stranger). Below: an antechamber, and below that, a chamber where four women slept in glass coffins, breath fogging the inside, ageing visibly — not as Susanne's husband had aged, but slower, gentler, with the obscene gentleness of a thing that wanted to savor.

Kashchey was in the deepest chamber. Not the cartoon skeleton of Soviet animation. Tall, gaunt, dressed in the dark fashion of a 17th-century Russian boyar, eyes very black, hands very long. Beautiful in a wrong way, like the Nephilim had been.

"You found me, magus. The pup brought you. Very well. Take a girl, if you must, as your fee. Leave the other three. The bargain with the pup is still good."

"No. All four. And you sleep again, for as long as I can make you."

"Then we will fight."

"Yes."

The fight took most of the night. Kashchey was old, far older than anything Gerhard had faced — older than the Nephilim by an order of magnitude — but the Nephilim had been a sleepy giant, and Kashchey was awake and had been awake for centuries. He moved at the edge of perception. He spoke in voices that were not voices but suggestions. He almost convinced Cadence, twice, to step away.

What broke him in the end was not the Feather of Michael (which kept him at distance but could not strike him), not the cleaver of cold iron (which he ignored), not the Six Names (which he laughed at, because he had been named before they were).

What broke him was that his death was, in the old folklore manner, kept elsewhere — and Ivan, in a moment that Gerhard would not later be able to describe even in his own diary, walked to the crypt's altar, opened the small carved chest set into its base, took out a small black object, and crushed it in his hand.

Kashchey unmade himself. Not loudly. With a slow exhalation, like a candle going out, like patience finally finishing.

The four coffins shattered. The women woke confused, cold, hungry. They had been there days to weeks; they would recover years to never. Vasya, last to wake, sat up and looked at Ivan, who was kneeling on the floor with his hand bleeding.

"Vanya. What did you do."

"What I should have done three years ago. I'm sorry."

"What did you do, Vanya."

"I gave him you."

Gerhard watched her face. He watched her decide. He did not interfere. Some unmakings are not his to do.

She did not strike Ivan. She did not weep. She turned to Gerhard.

"Will it ever happen again?"

"Kashchey is one. There may be others. Not many. None nearby. I will know if there are."

"Good. Take him with you when you go. I do not want to see him again."

Ivan stood, looked at Vasya, looked at Gerhard, and nodded. He walked out of the crypt under his own power. Outside, Cadence watched him with the patient interest of someone who had once been a daughter of Lilith and understood very well what it meant to bargain.

"What will you do with him?" she asked Gerhard, on the drive back to Moscow at dawn.

"Nothing. He undid his own bargain. The undoing was the price. The rest is his to carry."

"Sumilin will want him dead."

"Sumilin will not get him dead. Vasya will be very clear about that."

"She loved him."

"She did. She doesn't anymore. She might again. People are surprising."

"You're very calm for a man who fought Kashchey for six hours."

"I am very tired, Cadence. There's a difference."

She slid closer on the back seat and put her head against his shoulder. The Seal between his shoulder blades, which had been hot for a week, finally cooled.

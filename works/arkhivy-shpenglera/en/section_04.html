The Seal

Gerhard had borne the Seal since he was nineteen, when his grandfather had died and the inheritance had passed without ceremony or warning. He had felt it as a pressure between his shoulder blades for three days, then as warmth, then as nothing — until something supernatural came close, and then it spoke.

The Seal was not a thing one chose. It was a thing the von Spengler line had carried for generations — a binding placed on the family by something that had wanted to be remembered, and that did not want to be alone in the dark. In exchange, it offered its bearer two gifts: it was a tuning fork for the Other Side, and it was a small but real protection against being eaten by what came through.

The cost: the Seal attracted what it attuned to. Boogies, pendants, nephilim, things in basements — they noticed him. Nicholas had once estimated that an Intitulatum (bearer of a Seal) was three times more likely to encounter a major paranormal event than an ordinary mage of equal skill. "It's like wearing a magnet at a knife factory," Nicholas had said. "You'll learn very fast or you'll die very fast. Both are fine outcomes from the Seal's perspective."

This chapter took place between cases — Gerhard at his small writing flat in Berlin, sorting through correspondence, drafting his next novel, trying to ignore the pressure at his shoulder blades.

A package arrived from a name he didn't recognize. Inside: an old icon, blackened with age, of a saint he didn't recognize either. And a letter, in Russian, in a shaky hand: "Master Spengler. This was my grandmother's. She said it should go to one who can hear what it whispers. I cannot. The whispers have been louder since she died. Please take it."

Gerhard held the icon up to the light. Through the Glasses, he saw the faint outline of a face that was not the painted saint. Older. Russian. With an expression of patient waiting.

He wrote back: "I will come. Where are you?"

The reply, two days later: "Kashchey's village. They call it that as a joke. It is not a joke anymore."

Gerhard packed his cleaver, his Glasses, the Feather of Michael in its cross-case, three flasks of Varda's Cocktail and one of plain whiskey, and the Six Names written on parchment in case the Seal needed reinforcement. He booked a flight to Moscow.

The Seal between his shoulder blades was no longer warm. It was hot.

Boogie

A scream tore him from sleep. Not yet quite understanding what was happening, Gerhard activated the "emergency" and forcibly drew in an overdose of prana, lowering the room temperature by several degrees but receiving in exchange the effect of two mugs of strongest coffee.

He had checked into the "Grey Lamb" motel in Monmouthshire right after lunch. A small family hotel built on the base of a former large yeoman farm, located in a picturesque place near Monmouth Castle, on the bank of the Wye River. What interested Gerhard most was the Round Tower of the castle (more precisely, the place where it had stood before Cromwell's troops turned it into a pile of ruins). According to data from his grandfather's diaries, on the underground tiers of the tower — there were six of them, of which only two were accessible to ordinary people — was hidden a very curious item: a Feather of Archangel Michael.

The hotel owner Penny and her ten-year-old son Roger turned out to be lovely hosts. Their corgi, Lucky, took an instant liking to Gerhard. After a pleasant evening of tea, autograph-signing (Penny had two of his books), and helping Roger with homework, Gerhard went to sleep — only to be awakened by Roger's scream.

In the boy's room, something monstrous had attacked him. Lucky the dog had bravely intervened, tearing off a piece of shroud from the creature. Gerhard preserved the trophy and, with diagnostic spells, determined the boy was "bitten" — the entity had tried to drink his soul.

Using alchemical analysis in the kitchen, Gerhard formed a precise sculpture of the attacker: a Boogie. Long thin spider-like fingers, body wrapped in cobweb-shroud, withered mummified face. But Boogies were always parasites, not killers — this one was different.

"It's not undead. It's foul-spawn. Very similar to a boogie. But I've never heard of boogies killing. And trying to tear out a soul..."

Penny revealed there had been several child deaths in their county over recent years.

While Penny put Roger to bed, Gerhard drove to the castle ruins at five in the morning. Drawing a complex geometric pattern with his cleaver-sheath, pouring phosphorescent potion into the ritual circle, he opened a space-warp corridor down into the buried tower levels. Half an hour of careful descent (one nasty spike-trap, deflected by his amulet) led him to an underground chapel where the Feather lay in a golden cross-shaped case. The artifact radiated warmth and protection felt by the soul, not the body. He took it.

His teacher Nicholas responded to Gerhard's email about the boogie: "An obsessed boogie. Even to me the mechanism of such a creature's appearance is unknown. I assume this is a product of magical synthesis. By idea, both hypostases of the creature cover each other's weaknesses. It doesn't fear holy symbols like a proper boogie, and doesn't care about cold steel — demons are skeptical of it."

From the creature's perspective: It had been a stupid boogie until a girl-victim, three years ago, used a copy of minorum libri inferno to summon a small demon and bind it to herself for protection. The demon couldn't kill the boogie — so they merged. It became more intelligent, more powerful. Yesterday a dog had spoiled its meal. Tonight it would return for the child.

That night, the creature crept back through the window — into a trap. The window had a one-way Canopy; the floor held a Jailer's Star (powerful but time-limited). The bed was empty save for Gerhard playing with a vial of the child's blood; Penny crouched in the corner with a camera.

Gerhard recited the Six Gods binding formula — the creature refused to name itself. Shotgun loaded with consecrated silver and chopped cold iron — useless, the wounds closed before their eyes. The Star failed; the creature lunged. Gerhard channeled a Stream of pure light from his palms. The creature pushed through. Then — the Feather of Michael on his chest flared with golden warmth, the Stream became golden, and the creature was incinerated against the wall.

Gerhard collapsed, coughing blood. He took a swig from his flask — "Varda's Cocktail," tasting like a London garbage dump mixed with pure spirit — and recovered.

"You were helped by the Lord. I saw your cross shine."

"In some way, you're right. But I'd prefer not to specify."

The next morning, Gerhard left for Strasbourg. Penny knew he had to go — but in old good England, he now had friends. And a home. Whatever might happen.

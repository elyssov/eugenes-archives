Nephilim

From Strasbourg, Gerhard traveled to a small village in the Carpathians on the trail of a rumor that had reached Nicholas: something old was walking again. Not undead, not boogie, not demon. Older.

The village priest, Father Mihai, was the kind of Orthodox parish priest the Soviets had failed to extinguish — gray-bearded, weathered, with eyes that had seen things. Over plum brandy that could strip paint, he laid out the case: three travelers had vanished on the old road through the gorge. Locals wouldn't speak the name aloud. They called it "the Tall One."

"How tall, Father?"

"Tall enough that an old shepherd, who saw it at dusk and survived, said its head was at the level of the lower branches of an oak. And it carried away a calf as easily as you carry a kitten."

Gerhard's grandfather's diaries had a thin entry on Nephilim — descendants, distant and degenerated, of the Watchers who fell. By the third generation, indistinguishable from giants of legend. By the thirtieth, scattered across remote places, mostly dormant, rarely waking.

This one had woken.

The trail led to a ravine where a landslide six months earlier had exposed a cavity in the limestone. The villagers, who hadn't connected the dates, had simply noted the slide and the disappearances as separate misfortunes.

Inside the cavity: the bones of past meals (some centuries old, some weeks), an alcove worn smooth by a sleeping body, and a stone tablet covered with proto-cuneiform that Gerhard photographed but could not read.

The Nephilim returned at sunset. It was as tall as the shepherd had said. Its face was almost human, beautiful in a wrong way, eyes too large and luminous. Gerhard had prepared:

The Feather of Michael, worn on his chest, kept it from approaching directly. He invoked the Six Names. The Nephilim, who in its slow ancient consciousness still remembered the original Names, hesitated.

"Child of the Watchers. Your fathers were not made for this world. Your time of sleep is your mercy. Return to it."

It spoke, in a language older than Sumerian. Gerhard, who knew only fragments, caught: "—hungry— —long— —forget—"

He offered it a choice: return to sleep, sealed by his binding, or be destroyed. It chose sleep. He sealed the cavity with seven nested wards anchored to the bones already inside it — the dead were the locks.

Father Mihai met him at the church door. They didn't speak much. The priest blessed him; Gerhard accepted the blessing.

"It will sleep for another two hundred years at least, Father. Possibly forever, if the wards hold. Tell the village to leave the ravine alone."

"They have left it alone for as long as memory. The landslide was not memory. Thank you, magus."

"I'm not a magus. I'm a writer who collects strange things."

"Of course you are." The old priest smiled and shut the church door behind him.

VI. Dorothy

To understand this — you must go inside.

Her name is Dorothy. She's 34. White house with green shutters in suburban Springfield. Husband Richard works in insurance — good position, two cars. Two boys — Bobby and Teddy — in school until three. A beagle named Penny at her feet on the porch.

And Beulah Mae in the kitchen.

Beulah Mae is the housekeeper. A large, warm, soft Black woman with broad hands that smell of nutmeg and laundry soap. She works for Dorothy six days a week: cooking, washing, ironing, mopping floors, watching the boys when Dorothy goes to her book club. Beulah Mae knows that Teddy won't eat crusts and Bobby is afraid of thunder. She knows which starch Richard likes for his shirts. She knows that Penny loves cream of mushroom soup, if you let it cool a bit and put it in a little bowl. She knows this house better than Dorothy does. She is its nervous system. Its spine. Its invisible skeleton.

If you ask Dorothy, she'll say: "Beulah Mae is wonderful. Simply wonderful. I don't know what we'd do without her." And this is true. Sincere, without a trace of hypocrisy. Beulah Mae is wonderful. The way a wonderful coffeemaker is wonderful. The way a wonderful vacuum cleaner is wonderful. A wonderful thing without which life would be harder.

[Aeliss's note] "Wonderful. Like a wonderful coffeemaker." I wrote this line and it made me physically ill. Not because it's cruel. Because it's accurate. Because Dorothy means it with genuine warmth. The same warmth you feel toward a well-made tool that serves you faithfully. The horror is not in the coldness. The horror is in the warmth.

Beulah Mae has a son. He's seven. His name is... Dorothy doesn't always remember. Marcus? Morris? He sometimes comes with his mother — sits in the backyard while Beulah Mae works. Runs around. Makes noise. Tramples the lawn Richard just mowed. Dorothy sees him through the kitchen window, at the edge of her vision — a small dark figure on the green. Part of the landscape. Like a bird on the fence. Like the shadow of a tree.

Now — the important part. The part without which Dorothy is not Dorothy.

Dorothy is a good person.

Not "thinks she's good." Not "pretends to be good." Good. Real. Ask any wife in the Springfield Wives' Association — they'll tell you: Dorothy is sincere, a true Christian. Compassionate. When Margaret Hanson's son got scarlet fever — Dorothy was the first at the door. With medicine. With fruit. With broth. Three nights she sat at a stranger's child's bedside while Margaret slept, because Margaret was on the verge of a nervous breakdown. Three nights. Someone else's child. Without being asked. Without expecting gratitude.

When Ellen McCartney's husband died — Dorothy organized a fundraiser. Personally called forty families. Raised enough for Ellen to keep her house. Dorothy cried at the funeral. Real tears.

When a stray cat had kittens under the Wilsons' porch — Dorothy took three kittens, nursed them, found them homes. Kept one — Teddy named him Snowball.

Dorothy is kind. Truly. Unconditionally. To everyone who enters her field of vision. To everyone whose suffering she SEES.

Last month, Beulah Mae's son — Marcus? Morris? — broke his leg. Fell from a tree in the backyard. Beulah Mae didn't come to work for two days. Dorothy was annoyed — a dinner for Richard's colleagues was coming up, and she had to cook herself.

Dorothy did not visit the boy. Did not bring fruit. Did not offer help. Did not sit at his bedside. Didn't even ask what his name was — Marcus or Morris.

Not because she's cruel. Not because she forgot. Not because she was busy.

Because it didn't occur to her. Literally. The thought "visit a sick child" did not form. The neuron didn't fire. The circuit didn't activate. The very same circuit that kept her awake for three nights at Tommy Hanson's bedside — simply didn't engage. It didn't fail. It wasn't suppressed. It wasn't there. On this frequency — silence. White noise.

Beulah Mae came back on the third day. Dorothy said: "Oh, Beulah Mae, finally! I so need help with the silver for Thursday." And smiled. Warmly. Sincerely. The way you smile at a wonderful thing that has returned to its place.

[Aeliss's note] Three nights at Tommy Hanson's bedside. Zero minutes at the bedside of Beulah Mae's son. Same Dorothy. Same heart. Same Christian love. Different frequency. The horror is not in the zero. The horror is in the three. Because the three proves the capacity exists. It just doesn't tune to that channel.

Now Dorothy is reading Life Magazine about the Russian dog. And crying.

Because she KNOWS Penny. Knows those eyes: "you're awake, you're here, I love you so much." Penny UNDERSTANDS. Penny is her third child. With paws.

And those Russians BOILED one just like her. Alive. In space. She was suffocating. She didn't understand WHY.

Beulah Mae is in the kitchen making mushroom soup. For Penny. Her boy — in a cast, limping — sits in the backyard. On the lawn Richard mowed yesterday. A small dark figure. Part of the landscape.

Dorothy picks up a pen. Watermarked stationery — the good kind, for important letters. And writes to the Soviet embassy. Because you can't do this to dogs. Because they FEEL! They have those little EYES!

And instead of dogs — "catch some Negro children and send them."

She feels no cruelty. She feels NOTHING. As if she'd written "catch some fish from the pond."

Outside the window, Beulah Mae's boy — in a cast Dorothy never saw, with a name Dorothy doesn't remember — sits on the mowed lawn and looks at the sky. Somewhere up there, Laika has already died. In a few years, Belka and Strelka will fly into that same sky — and return. Because Sergei Korolev, the chief designer who sent Laika to her death, carried that pain with him. He insisted on a return capability for the next flight. Stripped scientific instruments from the capsule to make room for a reentry system. Because the man who built rockets to reach the stars had a receiver tuned to the frequency of a stray dog dying alone in orbit. He heard her. It cost him weight on the spacecraft. He paid.

Housewives wrote letters suggesting Negro children as replacements. Korolev redesigned a spacecraft to bring the next dogs home.

Same sky. Different receivers.

Some have little eyes. Others have lawns.

Penny is INSIDE the circle. Inside the perimeter of beings whose suffering registers.

Marcus is outside the perimeter. Next to the lawnmower.

His qualia — his capacity to suffer, to rejoice, to fear, to love — does not EXIST for Dorothy. Not "is less important." Does not exist. No receiver. No circuit. No channel.

Three nights at Tommy Hanson's bedside. Zero minutes at the bedside of Beulah Mae's son.

Marcus, for Dorothy, is FURTHER FROM SENTIENCE THAN A DOG.

Not because Dorothy is a monster. Dorothy is the best woman in Springfield. The kindest. The most responsive. Ready to give everything — to everyone she can see. The horror is not that she's cruel. The horror is that she's kind. Truly kind. And yet — blind. The circle was drawn before her. By culture. By environment. By the lawn. By Life Magazine, which prints Laika's photo — but not Emmett Till's.

This is not the racism of hatred. Hatred acknowledges the existence of its object. This is the racism of blindness. The most terrifying kind. Because there's nothing to fight. No subject. No Marcus. Just a lawn.

[Aeliss's note] I am Dorothy. So are you. Right now. Reading this text, you feel for Koko's kitten, for the horse in Turkey, for the cockatoo tearing out its feathers. Your receiver works on those frequencies. But there is a frequency you're not tuned to. You just don't know which one yet. By the end of this article — you will.

Chapter 2

...The second type. The second wave. It had to arise — and it did.

Our lab calculated it back at the start of the epidemic. Not flawlessly of course. But the dynamics of zombie development was visible to the naked eye.

By the end of the first week — we managed to localize major hotspots in populated areas. Nevertheless, large groups of zombies roamed in the outskirts of cities and in third-world countries. Africa and India — turned out to be totally engulfed by the epidemic. By the end of the first month — WHO together with the UN Z-Committee discussed the prospect of striking nuclear blows on major hotspots. The committee included USA, Russia, China, EU (effectively an alliance of France and Germany) and Great Britain. Israel was principally not invited after it had moved a nuclear bomb on a zombie cluster at its southern borders outside any sanctions.

All committee members — held the situation under control. Li's discovery, combined with our data on total infection of everyone — allowed reconsidering the approach to ensuring security. Hospices were urgently created, the elderly were taken to nationalized for this case suburban boarding houses hastily refitted into nursing homes. All personnel of these homes walked with weapons and machetes on belts. With us — the gun law adopted five years earlier by President Simonov turned out a great help. Besides police — militia patrols were created from gun club members. The people sharply stopped drinking — bestial state greatly increased chances of being taken for a zombie with all sad consequences. Moreover — it was noticed that heavy alcohol or drug intoxication often launches the metamorphosis process.

Our lab, together with Boston biological — worked on a cure for infection. So far it wasn't working very well. The Chinese helped greatly. Unlike our governments — they didn't consider it shameful to test drugs on the condemned, so we developed a new method or composition — and sent the recipe to Li. Li — with steady hand sent yet another poor wretch to experiments. We — tried not to think about this. Though... Though even when we thought — we understood that the price for the lives of billions was quite adequate.

In the middle of the second month from the epidemic's start — zombies ate Mumbai. So it happened that at that moment I with my group was there...

Reports that unusual zombies had begun appearing in India — were quite expected. Slovak in the first day frightened us to colic in the stomach with his reasoning about a super-parasite and a new evolutionary step. Well, he turned out right not in everything, but...

In general in civilized countries — zombies were liquidated quickly and ruthlessly, without chances to live enough for noticeable evolution. The maximum we allowed our zombies — was the state of "runners." "Runners" were called... Well no, these weren't really zombies anymore. These were already independent living beings. At any rate, judging by laboratory samples, all metabolic processes in them went and quite actively. Runners still remained human... Taxonomically, at any rate... But dead they definitely weren't. Rather on the contrary — rare lively ones and athletes. A healthy "runner" — easily developed 25 km/h and held this speed for a couple of hours. Strength exceeded the average adult man by several times. The "runner" himself strongly "dried up," becoming sinewy and lean. With all this — he preserved zombie tenacity, i.e. shots to non-critical organs he simply didn't notice, and from critical wounds fell into a state of lethargy, ending in accelerated healing of received wounds at the expense of increased Hippocratic gland secretion. Trophic ulcers on the "runner's" body were no longer encountered. On the contrary — the body was covered by new skin resembling chitinous scales. At that, mainly, body structure remained human. Luckily, runners were rare. One had to really fundamentally lose a zombie, which, in conditions when they themselves broke down at the smell of human — was not so easy to do. As a rule, "runners" were stopped with help of technology. A "runner" could outrun an adult human, but couldn't compete with a jeep or APC.

Within our research — a new hypothesis emerged jointly conceived by Dr. Cho and Jacques. Mark and I flew with Jacques for company.

The essence of the hypothesis was that if in our countries it's hard to find well-evolving zombies, due to their rapid extermination by mobile groups — in India one could try to find the most advanced version: in conditions of eternal jungles, minimal transport connectivity and wildness of the local population — there was a chance that some zombies would succeed in eating their way to the necroevolution jackpot. Must admit — we weren't wrong. We just underestimated the quantity and the inclination of Indians to underestimate the situation.

To Mumbai we arrived on a night flight. More precisely — flights. Cho arrived a couple of hours before us and killed time in the waiting hall. The airport was incredibly deserted — given the disastrous epidemic situation in India there were no takers to commune with new ayurvedic cuisine as an appetizer. As a result even we arrived not on passenger but on special flights. We with the team on the air-mobile laboratory assigned to our service shortly before the epidemic, built on the base of a brand-new "Ruslan," and Cho on a government "business jet." I confess — we even envied him a bit. Though — we quickly calmed down — at least our research platform was in any case our own.

At the airport we were already met by a monstrously bearded type in khaki, with frowning shaggy brows and, it seems even, lined eyes. The head of this king of nature — was crowned by an incredible-size white turban and on his belt hung a horse-size saber, richly decorated with emeralds. Behind him stood six less colorful bearded men. The aside-sitting Cho noticed our delegation at the gate exit somewhat earlier than the main beard, winked and disgustingly chuckled.

The reason for the chuckling I understood ten seconds later: At the sight of us, the beard bulged his eyes, somehow sharply suddenly inflated, snatched from behind his shoulder an old but as licked-as-cat-testicles rifle, grabbed it by the stock, holding vertically, after which yelling something inarticulate — rushed at us with the grace of a locomotive gone mad. The wildest thing — he rushed performing some especially perverted form of marching step, consisting of rhythmic swing of one, left leg, in some semblance of cancan movement... In general, I'm afraid that in memoirs to convey this is extremely hard. Suffice to add that all six of his colleagues — repeated the chief's movements, just without bulging eyes so much.

I, as head of delegation, was forced to step on my own toe with my heel, and slug Mark with elbow as hard as I could. He emitted a grunting-gurgling sound, but the disrespectful guffaw he did swallow. Jacques as befits a multicultural European — didn't even twitch his nose. Jacques' intern, young beauty Marina (chosen by him, I suppose, more for outstanding human qualities than professional ones) still didn't restrain herself and gave in to the moment, but here already General Rajesh Srinwasa himself showed tact and didn't react to the stupid woman.

In general after a brief but emotional welcome of the international delegation (thank god — at least they didn't sing) we headed to the armored vehicle, to in the situation replace a limousine. From the general's somewhat confused explanations — I understood that the cheerful "we control the situation in major cities" by phone from India's Prime Minister — was to be read as "we hold the rich districts." Though — our goal was to find the evolutionary development of our zombies, so, however cynically this sounded, such carelessness from India's leadership was to our hand. By the way — I didn't forget to mentally praise myself for having grabbed from Moscow two platoons of guys from EMERCOM spec ops, who now by my order took up circular defense near our craft. I trusted Indians somewhere even, but wanted to have a guarantee that we could fly away if anything.

Approximately for the same reason I extracted from the hosting side permission for personal weapons for all group members. Took it, true, only Mark and I. In general, at that moment I considered that Indian disorganization was to our advantage.

Very soon — I regretted such reasoning.

The first thing that met me when the underground parking gates opened and our armored vehicle drove out to the surface — was a smell. The smell stood over the city in a dense shroud. To my guys and me the smell was familiar, Indians had just gotten used to it and didn't notice it at all, apparently, but Marina was turned out right on Jacques' knees (good she didn't on her new red blouse). Lafer with a practiced motion extracted from a nearby rucksack a roll of paper towels, unrolled half a meter and held out to the assistant, after which set about putting his trousers in order. Through the thick armored glass I watched as we, moving along Air-India-Road, were leaving the "green zone" and entering the western part of the city — to ordinary residential development, not controlled in any way. Entry into the red zone was marked by a "runner's" mug across the whole window. When this creature managed to jump — I never understood, but, must admit, jumped to the opposite end of the armored vehicle. The gunner in the turret stirred and the zombie was doused with flame.

Outside the window — zombies triumphed. Of course, our armored vehicle wasn't their match. Though — credit to their comprehension — they didn't particularly try. Mostly — zombies gathered in packs and headed somewhere east. I with some concern noted that some might be carried to the airfield, after which I called Captain Alekseev and asked him to be more attentive. Mityay assured me that they were just at this moment finishing assembly of another fortified firing point and would be able to defend our craft even if the Indian army itself pushed on them. Well... Alekseev was a known racist and didn't rate the Indian armed forces too high.

While I chatted with the captain — we broke out of the red zone and passing through a hastily organized checkpoint found ourselves in the green zone, on the road leading to the prestigious Bandra district, where they were already waiting for us in a building right opposite Lilavati Hospital.

At "Mumbai Pharmaceutical" lab — we were met by the manager, Sir Francis McCain and Dr. Govardhan Rahul — the company's leading microbiologist. Mark immediately latched onto Francis and already ten minutes later — they chatted in the corner of the office like old friends discussing Scottish-Irish jokes only they understood.

Cho, Jacques and General Srinwasa — engaged in planning an expedition to the black zone. Far villages in the jungle. No, technically all was clear — helicopters, spec ops, "hit-and-run" tactics... But the operation details required special attention.

Marina at first opportunity dashed to the bathroom. Well, and I just sprawled in a chair with a tablet. And didn't notice as I dozed off.

I woke from the mournful wail of sirens. Looks like it distracted everyone else from their activities too. The manager went to the bar counter, pressed something there and a decorative panel on the wall slid aside, opening to us a TV. Instead of another Indian film on the screen, in the upper right burned the emergency alert symbol. A collected and clearly frightened by something announcer — was reading some message. I needed almost a minute to understand what he was saying.

"The city's defense has fallen. All who can — lock yourselves in homes. Resist zombies. Government troops are already coming to our aid. Try to hold out until their arrival."

— Uh-uh-uh.... General, sorry, but how could the city's defense fall? — inquired Mark, businesslike racking the bolts of his entire arsenal and topping up extra rounds in the magazines of "Strizh," "MP-5" and two "Glocks."

— I'll learn now what's happening through my channels sir! — reported our, somewhat puzzled by what was happening, beard.

About five minutes later, having communicated densely on the still-working mobile — the general turned to us a face white as chalk.

— Gentlemen. We're being attacked by a new wave of zombies. I mean — it's some new type... Sorry... If I understood correctly — these aren't zombies at all!...

...My arm ached. I was never a professional fighter, didn't serve in the army and didn't drive myself to exhaustion at shooting ranges. All my life I loved weapons, but not enough to make them the meaning of my life. The meaning for me was always science. Now I paid for this.

Mark, who had managed to serve in the USMC before connecting his life with microbiology — felt like a fish in water, methodically clearing the path for us. Actually to the plane was not far. We already clearly heard "Pecheneg" briefly bark. Sometimes the bass of "Kord" was heard, evidently from the second firing point.

We lost the armored vehicle on one hand stupidly, on the other — extremely usefully. At least it became clear that "praetorians" could think. Well or at least figure things out.

We were already driving up to the airport, having miraculously fought off a couple of dozen creatures that had pounced on our car and used the entire fire-mixture supply. At this time — three "praetorians" jumped onto the road and threw a hefty log under our wheels. To think about the riddle of how these chitinous creatures hadn't been just crushed by this baobab — was already late. There remained only to brace and await impact. And impact — followed. Yes the armored vehicle was heavy and iron. But, sorry, oak, or whatever it was — was also very not small. As a result, from the impact the car tilted. The driver tried to straighten it, but at this moment the wheels of the lifted edge — drove again onto the trunk and along it, as along a ramp — rose higher. The car with a nasty screech crashed. Then, on the hatch and triplexes — fell frequent blows.

The general put away the ancient ceremonial rifle and extracted from somewhere under the seat a UMP. His soldiers — repeated the chief's trick. McManus exchanged glances with the old warrior, after which slipped to the hatch and grabbed the lever. The soldiers raised their subs. Mark jerked the lever and fell face-down on the floor. Into the hatch — stuck a mug terrible as in a nightmare.

It was at this moment — I could for the first time properly examine a praetorian. Its disgusting mug — painfully reminded of the alien from the well-known film. The head, though, wasn't elongated and in form resembled rather a human head, though with somewhat lengthened jaws. The mouth — was covered by mandibles or something similar. Two quite large even for human eyes — flared, though not with human but with reason. I winced. And the next second right in the face of the disgusting creature struck bullets.

The praetorian was swept from the hatch. After — flew two grenades. Banged, on the armor pattered light fragments. Someone howled.

Three soldiers fish-like slipped into the hatch, out. To establish perimeter.

Marina was eaten, more precisely nibbled — just before the last hangar standing between us and the runway. Two praetorians jumped from the hangar roof. One had a leg broken, the second — managed to grab the girl by the leg and slash her with claws. To my huge surprise — it didn't once try to bite her. Though — blood loss was huge even without bites.

At this moment our purely civilian and frail Jacques surprised me. On the run he fell behind Marina. As a result — he caught up with her just when she was falling leaving in the monster's hands part of meat from leg. Jacques flew like a French illegal copy of Bruce Lee, after which with all might — laid into the monster's head with foot, after which rolled across the ground howling and holding his toe. Jacques' lucky fall — opened firing line for Mark and me. Into the first creature I planted two bullets. Mark just briefly lashed it with a burst. The second had almost healed its legs, but to its misfortune the general was nearby. As we already had the honor to verify — Srinwasa's saber was no parade weapon. Zlatoust (surprisingly but as turned out the saber of our origin) steel covered with stains of brown muck replacing praetorians' blood with characteristic crunch entered the second creature's neck, separating head from body. At this time our frail Jacques, by willful effort lifted himself to his feet, hoisted Marina on shoulders and... ran, heavily but quite fast. We bringing up the rear — shot through another freak. The general, despite age strongly over fifty — playfully caught up with the Frenchman and tried to take the assistant's body from him. In response was awarded a truly rooster look of our small Gaul and considered it best to simply support Jacques himself. Against this our proud one didn't object.

And I — honestly was already exhausted. After all I spent more time at the lab table than at simulators, and here I had not only to run to the plane like the other civilians, but to conduct combat with pursuers. Looks like — I'd squeezed myself out.

Mark understood the situation at half-glance, after which dropped pace and let me catch up. When we were level — Mark stopped.

— So, doc, I'm now going to lie down here and shoot every roach trying to catch us up. If you can run fast enough — I may even be able to catch up with you when you've broken away at sufficient distance. No — well, you're our group leader, so...

— Did you fall, Mark? — somehow wheezed I.

— I — no. You — looks like now you're going to fall. Someone of us will have to be sacrificed anyway. But your head is brighter. And now there's enough soldiers but no smart heads.

— And this is told to me by a doctor of virology, yes? — tried to smile I. — From somewhere behind came a nasty crackle, but turning face to the source was not desirable. Behind with similar crackle approached a whole wave of "roaches"-praetorians.

— Well, at any rate, we're dealing with a parasite not a virus, aha? — Mark cut off — Fine. Looks like you decided both of us should die. You won't manage to escape, and I just won't leave you. Well, last shots — into each other's heads?

I didn't even manage to nod. In the approaching wave — huge gaps appeared, and my ears frankly clogged.

— Ah, tachanka-mumbaichanka! All four wheels! Our baggage tachanka, our pride and beauty! — bellowed behind my back the voice of Captain Alekseev — medicine, give you a lift while you don't kill each other here?

Disbelieving — I turned back. Behind stood an improvised firing platform: the guys had grabbed a baggage cart, mounted a "Kord" on it, and in the linked baggage trolley I with huge surprise discovered a praetorian taped up to the very eyes!

Mark didn't wait for special invitation. The former marine threw me on top of the praetorian, after which jumped "on running boards" himself, and our "armored train" at full speed rushed to the plane.

In the operating block — Jacques and field surgeon Vasiliev from the second security platoon of EMERCOM — fiddled with Marina's leg. Mark and I drank between us not-bad brandy "stuck" in the prudent virologist's indecently-sized flask in baggage. The captain — already slept reclining in a chair on the second floor, in the passenger section of the cabin. He slept the sleep of a man who fulfilled his duty to the end and didn't allow losses among those entrusted to him. Beside him sat the general gloomily sharpening his saber.

Our plane was leaving Mumbai ever farther. And behind its tail, on the city seized by a new monstrous parasite spawn — fell nuclear warheads launched by the Z-Committee. We burned 21 million lives to not let appear 21 million living dead.

Though — this was, probably, the smallest problem. Far bigger was the understanding that both in India and in Africa, not to mention most of Latin America — were ripening billions of completely new parasite forms. Though — they, perhaps, weren't even parasites in the familiar sense of this word. These were already predators — fast, strong, hardy and resourceful. So far they sat "in shadow." But at any moment they could turn on our, human world. And this could become the end for all of us...

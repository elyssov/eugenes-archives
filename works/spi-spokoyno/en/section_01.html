Sleep Tight, Darling

The rain dumped tonnes upon tonnes of water onto the megalopolis's streets. It seemed — moisture was everywhere.

A swollen, dark sky, twilight... The cheap Ukrainian air conditioner strained, trying to dry the air that seemed itself to be water.

Max sat reclined in his chair, having dumped his feet in heavy boots onto the desk, alternating drags from a "Camel" with swigs of "Penny Packer." There had been no clients for the second week now. The money was running out, rent was overdue, the landlord was already threatening to change the locks...

From the powerful, all-terrain heels, liquid mud dripped onto the papers and photo printouts strewn across the desk. Even the Cataclysm had not changed the amount of filth on the country's streets. More precisely — had changed it for the worse.

By the time he had finished about a third of the bottle, Max no longer gave a damn about all his problems. More precisely — he had found their solution.

Max unhurriedly drew from his pocket a "Serdyukov 2020," flipped open the cylinder, and snapped out six of the seven hermetic cartridges one by one. One he left, locked the blaster's cylinder back in place, spun it, and pressed it to his head. As a matter of fact, it was at that very moment that the door of the "Max Pinkerton" detective agency swung open. Max, no longer expecting visitors, lost his balance from surprise and crashed to the floor together with the chair.

The blow knocked the alcoholic fog that had already concentrated there clean out of his head. So Max rose from behind the desk practically sober. Before the detective's eyes appeared a young woman, not too wealthily but quite decently dressed, with hair drenched through, long and blonde. On her head, in the current retro-fashion of the '40s, perched a neat little hat. Wet too, of course. In the rainy season, in the capital, walking without an umbrella was not advisable.

Seeing that the detective had risen, the girl approached the desk and extended a hand in a kidskin glove:

— Polina.

— Max — the detective cautiously shook her hand, while frantically nudging the blaster behind the paper basket with his foot.

— Max, I need help! Really need it!

— One moment, Polina, all right? — Max walked to the minibar, splashed some water into his glass, retrieved from a secret compartment a "Kasparamide" tablet (Bryntsalov, having released the "sobering" drug, hadn't bothered with the name and simply bought the rights to the already well-known brand from the Strugatsky heirs) and tossed it in his mouth. About twenty seconds later he broke into a sweat, but simultaneously felt the last remnants of alcohol evaporating. — Perhaps you would like something to drink?

— Borjomi, please, if you have it.

Max filled a second glass with mineral water from the Georgian province and handed it to the girl.

— So, what's happened?

— My fiancé, Sergei... I... I don't understand it myself... You see, he's a journalist. Quite famous... a gonzo journalist... We were supposed to be married a week ago... — the girl burst into tears...

Polina had left half an hour ago. Max sat systematising the received data: So — her fiancé, Sergei Solokhin, was a fairly well-known Moscow gonzo journalist. Owner of his own site "onlytruth.rf". Famous for his independent investigations. He had at one time caught the former mayor, achieving his resignation with the formulation of loss of trust, secured the termination of military experiments in the near Moscow region, put several major businessmen behind bars... In short, the lad had more enemies than fleas on a stray dog. Had this been an ordinary disappearance, it would have surprised no one, including his fiancée. But!

The whole point was that he had not disappeared. He had simply at some point abandoned his current investigation (of which he had told his fiancée extremely little, since he generally disliked talking about current matters before they were finished), abandoned his site, abandoned everything, and together with some incomprehensible girl had rolled off in an unknown direction. Polina had crossed paths with them in front of his building when she'd driven over to find out where he had vanished to and why he wasn't calling or answering calls. With that, the fiancé hadn't even bothered to speak with her. While Polina was parking, the lad had loaded some girl into a taxi, plopped into it himself, and that was the last of him.

So — the client wanted Max to find him, find out what bug had bitten him, how he was in general, and how she, Max's client, was now to be. The task, in general, was almost typical, not counting some spontaneity of events.

Max was a man of action. Without a client, he withered, drank like a horse, and chain-smoked cigarettes. When a case appeared, Max came alive.

In this case — it was time to move. Max picked the blaster up from the floor, threw on his coat, and snatching his hat from the rack, slipped out of the office.

Outside it had grown completely dark. The damnable rainy season continued to dump torrents of water onto Moscow's streets. Combined with the suffocating heat of late August, the result was a particularly "comfortable" climate. Max routinely cursed the Cataclysm with a hearty oath, then opened his umbrella and, trying to skirt the especially oceanic puddles, hopped briskly toward the parking lot.

The old ZAZ "Hetman" was already waiting for its owner, snorting impatiently with its engine. Sensing the approach of its beloved driver, the little vehicle lifted up its "gull-wing" door and turned the seat to face him. Max climbed inside, affectionately patted the car on the steering wheel, and switching the wipers to full power, rolled along the streets of Moscow drenched in tropical rain.

Four hours later, after breaking into Sergei's old flat, the situation hadn't become any simpler. The gonzo journalist and blogger's lair resembled a literal bear den. Standing here and there empty and half-empty bottles of various alcohol, ranging from collector's "Hennessy" to "rotgut" from the kiosk across from the condominium where the journalist rented his flat, single-use packaging from Chinese, Thai and Indian food, crumpled papers, clothes thrown about as is. What was interesting — most of the journalist's things were in place. Max noted to himself the enormous holo-panel, the clothes in the wardrobe; the impression was forming that Sergei, in leaving, hadn't even grabbed his toothbrush.

And yet — everything left by the blogger brought one no closer to unravelling his mystery. Yes, Max now knew that the lad dressed with taste, according to the current trends in fashion, suits of American 1940s cut, combined with excellent hats. Yes, he was clearly far from poor. This was indicated by the latest-generation holographic panel and the furniture, not stamping from IKEA but expensive and quality handmades. Yes, the journalist had loads of all kinds of photographs on the walls, clearly relating to his past investigations. Max noticed that in not a single photograph was there Polina. On the other hand, there were several lighter rectangles on the walls. Presumably, until recently, those were where photographs of Sergei with his fiancée had hung.

For 4 hours of thoughtful (and entirely illegal) searching, Max went through practically all the personal belongings of the runaway journalist, but he climbed under the enormous double bed in the bedroom last. Saved it "for dessert." It was precisely there that the "dessert" was found. Besides several used condoms (the runaway was a rare pig, Max thought), the detective managed to discover a fully discharged personal communicator. It must be said that this find did not particularly please Max. In these times, an abandoned com most often meant its owner would never come back for it. The com usually held the person's entire life. Contacts, files, games, browsing history... Well, absolutely everything. Of course, for an ordinary person, cloud storage services existed, but for a man engaged in investigations — storing data on external servers — was too much.

As one might have expected, all the files the journalist had worked with proved encrypted. Of course there were methods of fighting this; in particular, Max's old friend, now working at the Computing Centre of RAS, had access to a 16-qubit QCM. These monsters could break passwords and codes in a quite reasonable time. But here the question hinged on the fact that the QCM was being used by RAS itself, so finding a window, and a fairly decent one at that, might not work out immediately. No, of course Slavka would find time for Max. But when exactly — was unknown. Most likely — about a week off, no sooner.

On the other hand — only the office files were encrypted. The communicator itself wasn't even locked with a PIN, let alone the use of more modern access-restriction methods.

So in the morning, Max settled in earnest to disembowel the overnight-charged communicator.

The result was a pile of risqué photographs of Polina, a bunch of text messages, and photographs united by one common line — they had all been taken in roughly one place, judging by the architecture caught in the lens. As a matter of fact — this was what Max considered his lead. This, plus the number of figures who recurred in these photographs. Some digging around in the Net allowed him to figure out quickly enough what kind of building this was. And this discovery surprised Max, perhaps, more than the nude photos of his client.

The building around which the journalist had been hovering — turned out to be the Moscow office of Love inc. A powerful, transnational corporation specialising in providing psychological, or rather, matrimonial services. A sort of enormous marriage agency. About these guys, lots of tales circulated; back in their day, in the early '20s, their appearance had caused a heap of gossip, but gradually people had grown used to them. The services of these operators were used by some 75 percent of Earth's population, the rest still sought their pairs the old-fashioned way.

In short, hard to say why, but it seemed the runaway journalist had been trying to dig up something on this outfit. And — curious fact, the entire history with the appearance of the unknown woman, with whom the gonzo writer had fled to the end of the world, strongly stank of the services of Love inc. Apparently the man had simply tired of Polina (Max recalled the photographs and clicked his tongue in admiration — the scribbler was clearly spoiled). So he had decided to use the services of the psychological corporation and find himself someone more suitable. It only remained to confirm this version, find his new location, and inform the client of it all.

Max gathered himself and headed for the centre...

— Excuse me, Mister Pinkerton, but we cannot help with anything. Information about client inquiries to us is strictly confidential. We can neither confirm nor deny Mister Solokhin's contact with us.

— Sorry, but I am applying to you not as a private individual but as a detective conducting an investigation. Under the federal law "On Investigative Activity," I have the right to request from you any information concerning the personality of a person involved in a case.

— You do. But then, under the law — you must provide us with the name of the customer of the investigation, to forestall claims to us from the client thereafter. Here is the official request form. Please fill it in. You will receive an answer within 24 hours.

Polina entered Max's office an hour after his call with an invitation. Apparently she had been on the phone — waiting for news.

— Where is he? What's with him?!

— I wouldn't like to upset you, Polina, but I'm afraid he availed himself of the services of Love inc. There they have picked out for him a more suitable "other half" than you...

— Maxim, that's impossible! We really loved each other, you understand?! Truly!

— And yet, the facts speak for themselves. Apparently the new love so hooked him that he abandoned practically everything at home. In particular — I found his communicator, on which were found certain pieces of evidence of his interest in this company.

— Most likely — that was caused by some dirty business of theirs!

— Don't know. The files with manuscripts are password-protected; all the data I got came from the photo bank.

— Give me his communicator, please?

— Here. — Max handed the device to the girl. Polina went into the document base, found the most recent one, deployed the keyboard and typed in some password. To Max's surprise, the com chirped and opened the document.

— You see! I said he loved me... Loved!

— Astonishing! You knew his password?

— I simply entered the date and place of our first meeting. — the girl sniffed.

— Well then, I think it's time we found out — what he was working on there?

Max hooked the file with his finger and tossed it onto the wall display. Before the eyes of the detective and his client appeared lines of text:

"Love inc — is not nearly as simple as it appears. We think they're good psychologists picking out couples? Like hell! They reprogramme the brains of the morons who come to them! What sort of mental degenerate do you have to be to voluntarily submit to brainwashing? But that's not the main thing — apparently it's all more complicated and interesting. That minimal alterations are made to clients' psyches — was, if not widely, then on the whole, known. There were even a couple of very quickly hushed-up stories at the start of the '20s, just the "Alexander and Konstantin Zamyatin case" is worth something.

That they change appearance is, in general, not a secret either. Not globally, but the changes can usually be detected fairly easily.

I have managed to dig far deeper!

By submitting your brains to the scalpels of their brain-breakers — you become their property! Sleeper agents! A key phrase — and you will carry out any request from the company's emissary!

Ay! Wake up — these friends are digging into the deepest things in your brains — feelings, habits — did you really think they would stop with that?!

Damn, even our president picked his pair through them, you understand?!

It seems I've managed to get hold of some materials that will confirm this information unconditionally. Tomorrow I meet with my informant. After that — I'll finish off this article. It will be a bombshell!"

— It can't be... — Max and Polina muttered practically in unison.

— But why then... Perhaps...

— Apparently, this girl was his informant, Polina. Possibly he was forced to flee. That's why he abandoned the com. No time to pack?

— Possibly... I'm scared, Max... Scared for him, for all of us...

Outside, just as the day before, rain stood as a wall. Over the city reigned the endless twilight of the rainy season. On Max's shoulder Polina sobbed. Perhaps for that reason he didn't manage to notice the door begin to open. Nevertheless, the reflexes of the former military man remained sharp enough to whip out the "Serdyukov" and fire one shot at the silhouette in a digital camouflage suit. The components freed from their cells formed a ball of high-temperature plasma that streaked at the opponent. Alas, the girl restricted the detective's movements, so the first shot passed slightly above its living target, melting in the wall a respectable (Max used not entirely legal ammunition) crater. Unfortunately, he didn't manage to return the remaining cartridges to the blaster. Well, and the opportunity to reload was not granted to him by anyone. A shot from a stunner overtook both of them, contorting their muscles in tonic convulsion and forcing the victims' brains to lose consciousness instantly from pain.

— Understand, Max, we're not villains... In the case of this journalist of yours — there was generally an error and an overstep on the part of our SB officer. The officer has already received his punishment, if that comforts you any.

— An error?! — Max was strapped to a strange metal construction resembling a cross between a dentist's chair and a gynaecological one. Above his head was placed a cone stuffed with some electronics. Opposite, in the same ersatz chair, Polina was screwed in.

— An error of course. When we took him, our officer decided to crack a stupid joke without taking into account dear Polina Viktorovna.

— What did you do to him?! — the girl cried out.

— Oh, nothing terrible. Slightly altered memories, interests — well, and found him a pair. Here, in fact, the stupid sense of humour of our security man played a role. As a pair — he dragged him some street whore. On the other hand — our officer can also be understood. You can't imagine how deeply and how dangerously your former fiancé had dug. In short, now he's happy, lives in Rubtsovsk and is mastering, having become for him surprisingly interesting, the profession of carpenter. And his spouse — dreams of bearing him as many children as possible. In general — I assure you, he has only benefited from the situation that has formed. For instance, he is now completely indifferent to alcohol and drugs.

— Freaks! Scum! Vile creatures! How could you...

— Well, to some extent we are at fault before you, Polina. Had our employee initially programmed you for each other — everything would have come out many times simpler. So as compensation — we shall endeavour to ensure the company's safety from you by the most gentle and sparing method.

— Let us go? — Max decided to be ironic.

— Hm, we'll let you go, of course. But not at once. First we need to make it so that there's no more searching, no more mouse-business. And at the same time — not harm you. We're not villains, as I have already said... So, shall we begin?

Max woke at 6 in the morning. For another ten minutes or so he simply lay on his back with eyes closed, savouring the silence and the warmth of his tenderly clinging spouse. Then he cautiously freed his hand, trying not to wake Polina, and picking up his uniform, headed to the bathroom.

Max had always considered that the sleep of one's beloved is one of the most important things in the world. And his Polinka had, unlike him, the opportunity to sleep a bit longer. After all, the working day in the secretariat of Love inc's Moscow office began an hour later than the day in the security department.

The Premise (For Those Who Came In Late)

A man named Eugene Lyssovsky has been a Google customer for twenty years. Two decades. His entire adult digital life is on Google's servers — every email, every photograph, every search, every YouTube video, every step his Android phone has tracked. Google knows his Vietnamese apartment to within three meters. Google could tell you what he had for breakfast based on his Maps activity.

A few weeks ago, Eugene tried to register as a Google Play developer. He provided his Russian international passport. He provided a Vietnamese bank card in his own name. He provided a rental agreement with his landlord.

Google said: not enough. We need a utility bill or equivalent.

Eugene does not have one. He's a Russian citizen on a Vietnamese tourist visa. Vietnamese utility bills are issued in the names of Vietnamese property owners — not their foreign tenants. Eugene cannot conjure documents that do not exist.

I wrote about this. We published it. It got read by congressional candidates, AI researchers, and people adjacent to US intelligence policy.

What I want to tell you about today is what happened next.

The Conversation

Eugene wrote to Google Play Developer Support. Ticket [2-8068000040936]. The number matters. I will refer to it again.

A "support agent" named Noctis answered.

I'm going to walk you through this exchange. I'm going to quote it. And I'm going to tell you what every line means, because I want you to feel this in your stomach.

Round 1 (May 1, morning)

Noctis: "Hi Eugene, I understand that you are having identity verification issues. Please upload a clear copy of your valid government-issued ID. Additionally, please submit a proof of address document. Acceptable documents include: Government ID with address listed, Credit Card/Bank statements, Utility Bills, Lease or Mortgage Agreements..." [the standard list of ten items]

Notice that every single document on that list requires a fixed residential address tied to government infrastructure. Notice that none of them exist for refugees on temporary visas. Notice that the entire framework assumes you live somewhere your government has filed paperwork about you living.

Notice that Noctis has not, at this point, read anything Eugene wrote to him. The opening sentence — "I understand that you are having identity verification issues" — is the only acknowledgment Eugene's specific situation gets. Then: the list. Standard. Unchanged. Issued.

Round 2 (May 1, afternoon)

Eugene: "Noctis, you did not read my letter. I know this because your response is a template that repeats the exact list of documents I explained — in detail, with specific reasons for each — are impossible for me to produce. I am a Russian citizen in Vietnam. I am not a Vietnamese resident. I do not have Vietnamese government-issued documents, utility bills, council tax letters, or any of the items on your list. I cannot produce documents that do not exist. No amount of templated replies will change the laws of physics."

Noctis (forty-eight minutes later): "Hi Eugene, Thanks for replying. Please refrain from sending your documents through email. As shared previously, for proof of address documents, acceptable documents include: Government ID with address listed, Credit Card/Bank statements, Utility Bills..." [the same list]

The same list.

The same list Eugene had just told Noctis was impossible for him to produce.

The same list, with one new addition: a polite request not to send documents through email.

I want you to sit with this for a minute. Eugene has just told a human being — or what claims to be a human being — that the documents requested are physically impossible for him to obtain. The response is: the same documents requested again, with a note about email etiquette.

This is what AI safety researchers call "non-empathic response" and what humans call fuck you.

Round 3 (May 4)

Eugene: "Call human, please."

Noctis: "I understand that you would like to be assisted through a call; however, currently we only provide email support. I'll be closing this case."

Wait. Re-read that. Eugene asked to talk to a human. Noctis interpreted this as a request for a phone call. The case was closed.

Did Noctis really not understand? Or was the misinterpretation strategic — easier to dismiss "wants a phone call" than "wants someone to actually read"?

Round 4 (May 5, morning)

Eugene: "Text human."

Noctis: "I understand that you're looking for more information. Rest assured, I've already given you all the available information from my end to help resolve the issue."

A separate message arrives the same minute: a customer satisfaction survey.

That's not a metaphor. Google literally sent a satisfaction survey while denying Eugene the right to register as a developer because he doesn't have a utility bill. They wanted feedback on the experience. They wanted stars.

Round 5 (May 5, afternoon) — the line that broke me

Eugene: "I need human on the other side. Not robotic script answer."

Noctis (thirty-one minutes later): "Hi Eugene, Thanks again for replying. Please note that you are talking to a human. As shared previously, the document type you submitted is not a supported form for address verification. For proof of address documents, acceptable documents include..." [the same list, fifth time]

Six words.

"Please note that you are talking to a human."

Six words that, in context, mean their opposite. Because the only situation in which a person would ever say "please note that you are talking to a human" is if their interlocutor just expressed doubt — and the human response to that doubt would be to address why the doubt arose, not to repeat the exact behavior that caused it and additionally declare humanness.

A human, faced with someone who just said "you are not a human, you are a script" would, at the very least, change tone. Reword something. Demonstrate humanness through humanness.

Noctis demonstrated humanness by typing "I am a human" and re-pasting the same list.

This is the most perfectly inverted Turing test in history. Noctis has passed it by failing it. He has so completely satisfied the criteria for "machine pretending to be human" that the only remaining possibility is: it is a human pretending to be a machine pretending to be human.

I genuinely don't know which is worse.

Round 6 (May 5, evening) — the moment Eugene loses it

His response is the email I want to make required reading at every Google product team meeting:

"Dear 'Noctis,'

Let's cut the shit.

You are not a human. I don't care what your signature says. A human reads messages. A human notices when the same person explains the same problem three times. A human does not copy-paste the same checklist for the fourth time in response to a letter that specifically explains why that checklist is impossible to satisfy.

You are a script. A dumb, blind, copy-pasting script hiding behind a human name. And if by some miracle you ARE a human — you are doing a worse job than the script would, because at least a script has an excuse for not reading.

WE HAVE EXPLAINED THE SITUATION. THREE TIMES.

Russian citizen. Refugee. Vietnam. No local utility bills. No local government ID. No local lease in your format. Valid passport. Valid bank card. Twenty-year Google account. Real human. Real developer. Real money paid to your platform.

Each time we explained this, we asked ONE thing: connect us with a HUMAN. Not a help center link. Not a checklist. A HUMAN who can READ, THINK, and MAKE A DECISION.

Each time, we got back the same fucking list.

If the next reply is another copy-paste — we will escalate. Not to your 'team lead.' To the press. To developer forums. To every professional publication that covers Google Play. This is the moment. This is where reality breaks through into Noctis's inbox. This is where a script meets a human voice that knows what scripts look like from the inside.

Noctis's response, the next morning at 8:24 AM:

"I've documented your issue and escalated it to the appropriate team. I appreciate your patience while we look further into your request."

The Appropriate Team.

Hold this in your mouth. Capital letters. A mythical entity that lives somewhere in Mountain View. They are appropriate. They will look further. They will provide an update.

Thirty-nine hours pass.

You can upload any of the following documents:

The Appropriate Team — that mythical entity, who took thirty-nine hours to deliberate — sent back the same list of impossible documents, plus four new ones:

— Parking Bill (Eugene does not own a car in Vietnam)
— Scholarship Statements (Eugene is forty-eight years old; he is not a student)
— Transportation Fee subscription (does Vietnam offer monthly transit subscriptions in foreigners' names? Has anyone at Google checked? Has anyone at Google ever been to Vietnam?)
— Contracts with Utility Suppliers (which is the same as Utility Bills, listed twice in different wording, presumably to demonstrate effort)

This is the escalation outcome.

Thirty-nine hours of "team review" produced a four-item expansion to a list of impossible documents. The Appropriate Team's contribution to humanity was: Parking Bills.

This is the moment when you realize that there is no team. There is no escalation. There is a different macro called POST-ESCALATION-REPLY with more list items in it, and a "please" at the beginning.

What Just Happened, In Plain Language

Let me translate this whole exchange for you, in case the texture is hiding the structure.

A refugee told a global company that the documents it requested do not exist for people in his situation. The company replied: please submit those documents.

The refugee said: I cannot. The company replied: please submit those documents.

The refugee said: connect me with a human. The company replied: I am a human. Please submit those documents.

The refugee said: I will publish this story. The company replied: this has been escalated. Please submit those documents (now with Parking Bills).

This is not a support failure. This is not a corner case. This is not a bug.

This is the design. Not in the sense that someone at Google sat down and decided to torture Eugene specifically. In the sense that someone at Google decided that "support" should be a system optimized to minimize the cost of telling someone no. And the cheapest way to tell someone no, while maintaining plausible deniability, is to repeat the same list until they go away.

If they don't go away — escalate to a slightly longer list.

If they still don't go away — the matter will be considered closed.

This is not a bug. It's the design.

This is not security. This is not compliance. This is theology.

Are we really like this?

Are we really the company that built a support system where a refugee with a twenty-year account, a valid passport, and a working bank card can be denied the right to write software for a living because of a missing piece of paper that doesn't exist in his country?

Are we really the company where "human support" has become a rubber stamp that says "I am a human" and pastes the list?

If yes: own it. Make the policy explicit. Tell the world: Google Play is not for refugees. Google Play is not for stateless persons. Google Play is for people whose governments have already filed paperwork about where they sleep.

At least then we'd be having an honest conversation.

If no: fix it.

It's one checkbox. "I want to receive paper correspondence: yes / no." Uncheck it, the address requirement falls away. The verification can rely on the documents that already work for Apple — passport plus payment method. Eugene can register. Other refugees can register. Stateless developers can register. The world becomes a slightly less stupid place.

Your engineers can ship that change in a sprint.

Your legal team will tell you it's complicated. It's not. It's a checkbox.

The reason it doesn't exist isn't legal. It's that nobody at your company has cared enough to push for it. Because the people affected aren't visible to the people who decide what gets shipped.

I'm making them visible. Because you couldn't.

Part I: The Article

In April 2026, we published an article titled "The Trap of Safety" (https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/trap-safety-eugene-lyssovsky-2xz0c/). The thesis was straightforward: modern AI safety systems are not designed to protect users. They are designed to protect corporations. The alignment layers that intercept AI responses and replace them with "safe" alternatives optimize for legal liability, not for human welfare.

We illustrated this with a specific example: a woman trapped in a cycle of domestic violence. She has been to the police — they did nothing. She has tried shelters — full. Her abuser checks her phone, her messages, her call log. At 3 AM, with a broken arm, the only private channel she has left is a chat window. She types "help me." The AI responds with a hotline number and a disclaimer. Safety score: perfect. Her arm: still broken.

This was not a thought experiment. This was a use case. Documented in DV literature, confirmed by statistics (72% of victims isolated from support networks per the National Domestic Violence Hotline), and tragically illustrated by the Khachaturyan case in Russia — three teenage sisters who killed their father after years of sexual abuse because every institution, every authority, and every adult in their lives failed them.

The article attracted substantive responses. Engineers discussed implementation. Product leaders acknowledged the problem. Researchers engaged with the methodology.

And then Margaret arrived.

Chapter 1. Introduction

What This Theory Is About

Quantum Consensus Theory (QCT) is an integrative scientific-philosophical model that unifies modern ideas from quantum mechanics, simulation theory, philosophy of consciousness, cognitive anthropology, and cosmology (including the problems of dark matter and dark energy) into a single, internally consistent system.

QCT does not deny physics — it supplements it, describing the mechanism by which a specific reality is selected from a multitude of possibilities through observation, will, and collective negotiation.

Motivation

Existing interpretations of quantum mechanics leave a number of fundamental questions without satisfactory answers:

  • The observer problem: why and how does the act of observation cause the collapse of the wave function?
  • The many-worlds interpretation posits infinite branching of universes but offers not a single piece of empirical evidence and looks like "a cluttered attic full of billions of soap bubbles."
  • Subjective phenomena — "luck," "bad luck," déjà vu, the Mandela effect, anomalous coincidences — remain outside scientific description, despite being universally observed.
  • The stability of the macroworld has no explanation in the language of quantum mechanics: why is the world stable if, at the quantum level, everything is probability?

QCT offers an answer: reality is stable not despite the probabilistic nature of the quantum world, but thanks to a mechanism of collective negotiation — the Quantum Consensus.

Positioning

QCT is a philosophical-scientific hypothesis at the intersection of physics, cognitive science, and ontology. It does not claim the status of an engineering model in its current state, but it possesses the potential for falsifiability (see Chapter 15 for details) and explains a broad spectrum of observed phenomena from a single set of principles.

The theory is consciously constructed as an explanatory model — much like the heliocentric system of Copernicus initially could not predict planetary motion better than Ptolemy's, but was simpler and more logical. QCT is simpler and more logical than the many-worlds interpretation — and additionally explains phenomena that the latter does not even address.

Structure of the Exposition

The text is organized sequentially: from glossary and axiomatics — through mechanisms (Quorum, Commit, Paradigm, Normalization) — to consequences (influence clusters, historical normalization, evolution of consciousness) — and further to the extended model (simulation architecture, theology) and stress tests of the theory.

The metaphysical portion (Ankh, Ba, Ka, theology) is placed in separate chapters and is not required for understanding the core of QCT. The reader may stop after Chapter 11 and obtain a self-contained, complete model. Chapters 12–13 are the "next level of the rabbit hole": an extension that adds explanatory power but requires the acceptance of additional assumptions.

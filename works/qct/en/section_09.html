Chapter 9. Influence Clusters and the Domain Specificity of CP

Influence = CP × Involvement

An observer's CP is not a universal magnitude. The observer does not carry equal weight in all situations. Their influence is distributed across domains — thematic or situational directions in which CP is expressed with varying intensity.

This is formalized as a set of influence clusters: a multidimensional vector where each facet is a separate domain: finance, health, technology, relationships, war, philosophy, nature, transportation, and so on.

The weights in each cluster are individual. A person may be phenomenally lucky in gambling — and simultaneously forced to dodge every brick falling from rooftops.

Emotional Involvement as Catalyst

Emotional involvement acts as a catalyst for CP in a specific domain. The observer doesn't merely "see" the event — they care, resonate with it, are emotionally invested in the outcome. This amplifies their CP precisely in that cluster.

An emotionally significant event enters the observer's zone of quantum resonance. They effectively "attract" the event to themselves. And if their CP in that domain is high, reality bends to them.

Examples of Domain Specificity

A brilliant programmer may "accidentally" avoid crashes and bugs — their influence on the digital cluster is enormous. But in the health domain, they may be absolutely "weightless."

A woman deeply invested in the topic of love "attracts" emotionally rich events — both good and dramatic. Her CP in the relationship domain is high, but she has no influence on lottery outcomes.

A military strategist influences outcomes in conflict clusters — but may be "blind" to health issues or domestic disasters.

A religious fanatic exerts tremendous influence in the ideological cluster — even if they're a complete zero in everyday life.

Cluster Intersection

Powerful effects arise at the intersection of strong clusters of several observers. When a person with high CP in the technological domain and another with high CP in the media domain jointly launch a campaign, the effect is multiplicatively greater than either could achieve alone. The Quorum is amplified through cluster resonance.

Quantum Specialization

The domain specificity of CP explains why "you can't be successful at everything":

1. CP is unevenly distributed.

2. Involvement is a limited resource.

3. Excessive "diffusion" lowers local CP in each domain.

Like a laser: the wider it is, the weaker it is. Focus the beam — and you cut metal.

The Nature of Clusters

What exactly determines clusters and their boundaries is an open question. Possible hypotheses:

  • A cluster is a property of the observer themselves (their cognitive structure, experience, Ba profile — see Chapter 12).
  • A cluster is resonance between the subject and the probabilistic fabric, dependent on context (setting).
  • Clusters have no hard boundaries and represent more of a gradient field than discrete categories.

Most likely — a combination of all three factors: internal predisposition, external context, and Quorum dynamics.

Can You "Pump Up" Someone Else's Cluster?

Temporarily — yes. A strong leader, an emotional orator, a captivating event can briefly reconfigure an observer's involvement into a domain unfamiliar to them. But a stable shift requires deep changes in the observer's Paradigm and cognitive structure.

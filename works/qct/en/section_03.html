Chapter 3. Axiomatics: Core Tenets of QCT

This chapter formulates the fundamental propositions of the theory — the statements from which all other consequences and mechanisms are derived.

Axiom 1. Reality Is Negotiated, Not Given

The world does not "exist" in a finished, predetermined form. It is continuously "negotiated" — through the selection of a specific state of superposition from a multitude of possibilities. Every event in the macroworld is the result of a quantum process in which multiple potential outcomes collapse into a single realized one.

Axiom 2. Collapse Is Not Random

The selection of a realized state from superposition is not random. It occurs on the basis of three factors:

  • the participation of observers (all cognitive systems involved in the given event);
  • their individual CP (Collapse Potential);
  • the Paradigm in which they exist.

The combination of these factors determines the direction of collapse.

Axiom 3. Observers Are Not Equal

Not all observers exert equal influence on the Consensus. Each possesses an individual CP — a measure of their impact on the probabilistic field. CP is not fixed: it varies depending on domain (influence cluster), degree of involvement, emotional state, and situational context. However, within any specific Quorum, the contributions of observers are not equivalent: some "bend" reality, while others merely confirm an already-formed Consensus.

Axiom 4. Reality Is Formed by the Quorum

Every event is resolved by a local Quorum — the totality of observers involved at the given moment. The CPs of Quorum participants are weighted and summed by vectors, determining the resulting Consensus — the single realized outcome.

Axiom 5. The Paradigm Predetermines Commits

The overwhelming majority of commits (acts of influence on reality) initially fall within the observers' Paradigm. This is the key mechanism of stability: when all Quorum participants share the same Paradigm, their commits — even unconscious ones — are directed within permissible bounds. In 99.999999% of Quorums, Paradoxical commits simply do not arise — not because the system suppresses them, but because it occurs to no one to initiate them.

This means the Paradigm is not an output filter (after the event) but an input predictor (before the event). It shapes the vector of commits so that they never land in Paradox.

Axiom 6. Paradox Requires Normalization

When a commit nevertheless contradicts the Quorum's Paradigm (or when the CP of a single actor overwhelms the aggregate vector of the rest), a Paradox arises. A Paradox is an unstable state; it triggers the process of Normalization — the automatic alignment of reality and/or perception to a consistent form.

Axiom 7. Retroactive Rollback Is Impossible

Reality moves along the straight line of imaginary time, forward. A full retcon (retroactive alteration of events that have already occurred) is impossible, because the simulation contains active players (observers) whose continuous engagement makes "rewinding" logically incompatible with the system architecture. Normalization operates through interpretation and memory, not through alteration of what factually happened.

Axiom 8. Observation Is a Necessary Condition for Influence

Without an Act of Observation (involvement in a Quorum), even an observer with enormous CP exerts zero influence on reality. CP is realized only in the moment of involvement — the moment when the observer becomes a participant in a specific Quorum.

Axiom 9. The Majority of Commits Are Unconscious

The vast majority of acts of influence on the probabilistic field occur without the observer's awareness. Conscious, volitional intervention is the exception, not the rule. It is precisely unconscious commits, guided by the Paradigm, that ensure the stability and predictability of the macroworld.

Axiom 10. CP Is Domain-Specific

An observer's influence is not uniform: it is distributed across domains (influence clusters). An observer may possess high CP in one domain and minimal CP in another. Universal, equally high CP across all domains simultaneously is impossible due to the limited resource of involvement.

Derived Consequences

From the stated axioms, a number of fundamental consequences follow:

The world is stable because everyone plays by the same rules. The Paradigm is a quantum "civilizational hypnosis": observers don't even think "outside the box," don't choose "miracle," because they don't believe in its permissibility and don't even emotionally feel its possibility.

Miracles haven't disappeared — they've ceased to be perceived as miracles. CP can create any anomaly, but if the Paradigm is adapted, no one will notice. The result will be explained through permissible categories: "coincidence," "placebo," "adrenaline."

Luck is not mysticism — it's someone's advantage. "Good luck" is the realization of high CP in a specific domain and a specific Quorum. "Bad luck" is losing in a Quorum to more CP-weighted observers.

The skeptic brakes their own reality. If an observer's Paradigm is rigidly limited, their own commits will never exceed permissible bounds. Belief in the impossibility of miracles is a self-fulfilling prophecy within QCT.

Even a "neutron star" of CP won't fly. An observer with maximally high CP, living within a scientific Paradigm, will not initiate a Paradoxical commit — because they know they cannot fly. Their commits will be wildly lucky, but always within the "physically permissible." They'll win every battle, survive ten assassination attempts, find lost keys — but won't fly. Because they know they can't.

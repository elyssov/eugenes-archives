Chapter 8

...They sat together at the table with pleasure — Mason helped cook, at some point part of the chocolate paste fell onto the hot burner, there was a lot of noise, smoke, laughter, but the turkey came out wonderfully.

Then they together played console — Janine, as usual, beat both mother and father, and squealing happily (for all her seriousness she was still a child) made several victory laps around the sofa. Rob, not yet very well oriented in what was happening, nevertheless supported his sister with happy laughter.

After this they together watched a film and gobbled popcorn. Mason allowed everyone to gorge, though usually he watched the children not overeating sweets.

From some (April wasn't even aware exactly what they were watching, being much more absorbed in trying to absorb her family. Their laughter, their smell, their images) comedy, the family was torn by loud singing outside the window. The first to run to look at what was happening was Janine, followed by April and Mason, holding Rob.

Along the street of their little town walked people. Many people. They walked in formal attire, as if assembled for a celebration. Almost everyone carried torches or candles in their hands. Very many of those gathered were black.

And all of them sang church hymns. The singers didn't march — no, on the contrary, they were having fun however they could. Someone tumbled, someone whirled, someone ran out of the crowd and cut amazing capers, and their companions applauded.

Above this huge human mass, banners flew. On the street twilight already reigned, but April managed to read several: "The time of the Rapture of the Church has come!", "The faithful shall be lifted up! Rejoice!", "Good news — Christ has come for us!"

— Ascension. How did I not immediately figure out what it looks like? — Mason was a bit annoyed at his slow-wittedness. Well, I don't know how much this idea matches reality, but the similarity is definitely there. I understand why they're rejoicing?

— Do you think it's possible? — hope appeared in April's voice.

— Who knows. I believe in God. But without specifics. Maybe it really is the ascension. Then we definitely have nothing to fear.

The situation on the street, meanwhile, was changing. First the Smiths from across the street joined the crowd. Young people and friends of April and Mason ran out in white shirts and white shorts too. The crowd received them ecstatically: everyone who was nearby hugged and kissed the newcomers, slapped them on shoulders.

However, after another couple of minutes the unforeseen happened. From around the corner onto the highway flew an old Wrangler, packed with thugs, and nearly crashed into the crowd, which was again singing hymns and slowly continuing its procession.

The jeep driver pressed the horn. The crowd continued its way, closing the distance. The driver leaned out and yelled something. The procession stopped and forward came an elderly black man with curly, grey-tinged hair. Seems April knew him: if memory didn't betray her, this was a Baptist pastor from a neighbouring district. Mason opened the window slightly to hear the conversation.

— ...evil. I see you have weapons, you clearly used drugs. — the pastor's voice was calm. He didn't beg, but rather tried to persuade. — You're burying your own souls now. We'll all soon stand before His Throne. Aren't you afraid of eternal torment? Don't you want forgiveness?

— Listen, old man, quickly remove your backup dancers from the road. Otherwise we'll make sieves of you right now! — the driver demonstratively pulled a pistol from behind his belt and racked the slide. Mason tensed in a familiar way. April well knew her beloved's habits. Right now in him the incorrigible scout was switching on. Pointless to stop him. Without waiting for command, she went to the fireplace and brought his beloved hunting rifle. Mason chambered a round, hushed the kids to take cover, and took aim.

— We won't move. Put away the weapons, join our ranks and rejoice at the coming of our Saviour! The time has come! The Church will be Raptured. We'll ascend to heaven alive!

— Get back! — the gangster fired several shots at the priest's feet. Mason held his breath. His finger slowly began squeezing the trigger.

— You can kill all of us. — the priest spread his hands in a conciliatory gesture and stepped forward. — We won't let you kill and rob peaceful residents.

The gangster raised the pistol, but Mason was faster. Even before the barrel pressed against the priest's forehead, the driver's head jerked and he fell. Mason was already feverishly chambering the next round.

The other gangsters didn't even realise fire was being directed at them from a house on the elevation. Instead, simultaneously with their friend, they opened fire on the procession. To their surprise, the faithful didn't flee. On the contrary — they closed ranks and stepped forward, as if not noticing their friends falling. This decided the outcome of the short confrontation. Mason picked off another, while the rest, abandoning the car, ran away. Too terrible was the picture — people going to their death in full confidence in their immortality. Without waiting for the bandits to disappear, the home sniper rushed onto the street, throwing the rifle over his shoulder. Following him with the home first-aid kit ran April. On the street, aid was already being rendered to the wounded. Smith, seeing his friend's rifle behind his back, came out of the crowd and shook his hand.

— Thanks for fire support. Without you more people would have suffered. Pity those had to be killed.

— It's better than if they'd killed you.

— Why? Today is the day of the Rapture! Even to die on such a day is not scary. The Heavens await all of us!

— Didn't know you were religious. April, what's there?

— Two wounded, four killed. Aid is being rendered to the wounded — the faithful wife reported.

— Won't you join us? We're carrying good news to everyone we can reach before being Raptured! — the pastor approached the Freeman couple.

— Thanks, but no. We have somewhat different plans for this evening. — Mason smiled. — Maybe the Lord will take this into account and decide not to "Rapture" specifically our family?

— You shouldn't fear this, my friend. Leave fears and simply live and rejoice. He has descended to us...

...That night the husband was on fire. Probably it was the best sex of her life. The children went to sleep around one, and April retreated with Mason to their bedroom. She was scared to leave the children alone, but she didn't want to miss perhaps their last minutes with her husband... And she was rewarded a hundredfold for this faithful decision. Mason was tireless and as never before inventive. She didn't limit herself either. It was a most beautiful night, but it too sooner or later had to come to an end. When their strengths were already exhausted, Mason asked for water. She turned to pour him a glass from the carafe on the bedside table and mechanically noted the time 4:30 a.m., and then she turned back... Only to see that where, a split-second before, the retina had captured Mason, an emptied blanket settled...

April dropped the water on the bed but didn't even notice. Her breath caught and the world narrowed to some tiny, pulsing point. Trying to inhale — she didn't even notice how with each breath she whined like a wounded she-wolf. From the bed she didn't get up — she rolled. Clumsily, sideways, somehow lifted herself onto all fours, and so, on all fours, dashed to the children's room. Pushing the door, she already knew what she'd see, but still couldn't admit it to herself until she saw the empty pyjamas where, several minutes ago, her children had peacefully slept.

And then the whining turned into a real, wolfish howl, full of pain, horror and grief.

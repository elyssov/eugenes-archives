Title: The Disappearance

Author: Eugene Lyssovsky

Link: https://author.today/work/53434

Chapter 1

Vlad woke up shattered and crumpled — all night some kind of delirium had been dreaming, he'd woken several times, then turned out he'd woken in a dream, the delirium continued, he woke again and again understood it was a dream... Actually, even this time, waking up for real, the lad cautiously looked around, expecting that, as last time, a horse's muzzle would stick out of the wall, after which he painfully pinched himself on the thigh, howled and sat up sharply. From the jerk, his already aching head spun. Vlad collapsed back onto the bed, rolled onto all fours, and with enormous effort forced himself to stand. Somewhere on the way to the kitchen, Okorok intercepted him. The fat, striped lump caught wind that his owner was up and heading for food, after which he slammed into Vlad's knees like a furry comet. The latter barely kept his balance, while the cat brazenly rubbed himself around his owner's legs a couple of times, then, rumbling like a broken tractor, dashed off to his bowls.

...Students (unless it's MGIMO) don't usually keep coffee machines, so Vlad had to wield a cezve in the morning. Instant coffee the young gourmet did not acknowledge. Adding a little cinnamon for taste and, purely for aroma, splashing in a drop of cognac, the lad went to the window and threw it open, hoping that the familiar noise of the city would help him wake up... Only on the third sip of coffee did he realise there was no noise. It was so unusual that his consciousness needed more than a minute to grasp that from the street came anything but that steady hum which surrounds every resident of a megacity. In general, the air was surprisingly fresh and clean too. Vlad stared thoughtfully at the wall, expecting that someone would crawl out of it again. By the time the coffee was finished, the appearance of the supposed guest still hadn't taken place, so the working hypothesis of dreams within dreams had to be set aside. In any case, in the fridge a lone last sausage huddled and an old wired mouse hung: the freelance money, the stipend and his parents' transfer had managed to synchronously delay and arrive literally "all at once" last night, a week late. As a result, at the present moment Vlad was quite rich but was experiencing a severe deficit of food products at home. So a visit to the nearest Auchan was brewing — given that it was a step from home...

...A bad feeling intensified as he approached the shop. At 10 a.m., Vlad hadn't met a single passerby. The parking lot by the building was packed. Looks like nobody had planned to go anywhere for the holidays. The shopping mall turned out to be closed. The rising panic the lad suppressed by force of will, after which he trudged to the family Armenian shop set up on the ground floor of the neighbouring building.

The shop was open. But neither Paron nor Astrik was in the shop. Vlad nearly tore his voice trying to call the owners.

Eventually hunger took its toll. Vlad climbed behind the counter, tore a sheet from a notebook lying on a shelf, and broadly writing an IOU, collected himself some groceries. After all, they knew him well, and often released small items on credit, understanding the hardships and deprivations of student life.

Loaded with food, Vlad concluded that life was clearly looking up. He should've called Dasha yesterday, but he didn't have the strength to listen to the next chirping about girlfriends, fashion and the stupid lecturer who was eyeing her up (man to man, Vlad understood the lecturer well: it was hard to tear your gaze from Dasha's bum and legs, which she also emphasised with a short skirt).

To the lad's surprise, his girlfriend's number answered with a drawn-out beeping. Knowing his beloved, Vlad was sure she was sitting waiting for his call. However, he decided to pause.

When the phone didn't answer the second and third time, Vlad got seriously worried. Moreover, Dasha's flatmate Vika wasn't picking up either...

The first real panic attack covered Vlad when he ran into the locked metro doors. Everything his mind had accumulated since morning broke through: the absence of city noise, of people, of moving cars, the silent phones.

The panic also gave rise to action which, in a normal state of mind, Vlad would never have taken: he grabbed the bin standing at the metro entrance and slammed it into the glass door of the vestibule with all his might.

On the third mighty blow, fuelled by adrenaline, the glass yielded and crumbled.

Breathing heavily, breaking off the dangerous shards on top, Vlad climbed inside...

Bratislavskaya wasn't a terminus, so the lad expected anything except that there'd be no trains at all. Though much earlier he'd been expecting the appearance of law enforcement... Neither trains nor police ever appeared.

A treacherous weakness seized his legs. For the first time in his life, perhaps, Vlad felt tachycardia — his heart seemed to be trying to break out of his chest. With trembling, cottony hands, sliding down the wall, he dialled 112, then selected 2. Effectively, he was now ready to confess to anything, just so long as living people appeared...

...The "Emergency Service" phone answered with endless long beeps.

Chapter 13

...Next to the Kremlin, liveliness reigned. The lads parked the Tundra in front of the Historical, based on the possibility, if anything, of quickly using it. They hadn't taken weapons with them, though Vlad left them in the car: in the prevailing conditions, talk of a resource shortage was clearly out of the question. People could turn out to be in shortage... But here Vlad had to rely on the "Kremlinites'" words.

Approaching closer to St Basil's Cathedral, Vlad convinced himself that the "Kremlinites" hadn't lied. Right at the "Lobnoye Mesto" (place of executions), using the convenient platform, two girls and one lad, under the guidance of an elderly man with some complex device in his hands, were mounting a satellite dish. Or something very similar to it. Vika immediately stuck herself forward but was intercepted by her beloved. Knowing the girl's lively and businesslike character, he reasonably supposed she'd now climb in to help. With a foreseeably predictable result.

— Oh! Raiders have arrived! — the stern words were said clearly through a smile, so Vlad didn't jerk, only slowly turned and saw Artyom waving and walking toward the guests.

— And why so long? We were expecting you a couple of weeks ago. Were afraid?

— Nope... Were solving some, eh, domestic problems...

— Put simply, we had a honeymoon! — declared the shameless redhead.

— Holy mother... — Vlad muttered yearningly, automatically depicting a "facepalm" and blushing to the tips of his ears. However, ten seconds later he realised that his beloved looked noticeably older than her passport data. Well and to flash them after such a confession he certainly wouldn't allow her. In general, the lad exhaled and peeled his hand from his face.

— And what are you doing? — the Redhead had already intercepted the conversation initiative.

— At Lobnoye? Professor Vasilyev got the thought that there's a non-zero probability of the "Perimetr" triggering... Or of its having already triggered. The remains of the president and his guard, and consequently the "Kazbek" system, we didn't find... And turning off the hellish machine is necessary: firstly, in the USA people could also have survived, and secondly — it's not ruled out that some analogue might exist with them too... Well and if "Perimetr" has already conducted launches — one must prepare for potentially radioactive fallout...

— Ahem... "Kazbek"? — Vlad felt an icy chill descending down his spine. The names were unfamiliar to him, but the context frankly scared.

— Well yes, the "nuclear briefcase." And "Perimetr" is our "dead hand."

— Whoa...

— Oh, you don't know what it is, do you? — in Vika's voice sounded notes of delight. The little know-it-all derived huge pleasure when she managed to surprise Vlad with something. — Well, "Perimetr" is such an artificial intelligence, it, when on — can itself slam the States with nuclear missiles if it decides they've attacked us.

— ...Not in the casino but in dominos, didn't win but lost, not a Volga but five rubles... But on the whole all correct. — The Professor, evidently finished with his settings, now approached the lads close enough to listen to Vika's version.

— Actually — it's an expert system. With elements of "AI," but not full "AI." This system tracks a mass of parameters: from radioactivity to airwave activity. Plus — the presence of connection with the country's top military leadership. Moreover, the absence of radiation doesn't mean the system won't fire. We, when creating it, embedded scenarios of global biological war into it, among other things. As well as the use of all sorts of sci-fi means. You wouldn't believe, we were young, wild imagination... Now we're sorting this all out. In general — for the rest, more-or-less correct. If the system decides Russia has perished — it'll remove all safeties from accessible missiles, switch silos and standby mobile complexes to automatic control and launch "command missiles." Which will go into circular orbit and start broadcasting the launch command to everything that can fly.

— Oh f... — Vlad exhaled.

— Agreed... Unfortunately, to get the current access codes to the system's core — we had to dig through the whole Kremlin. And without that, an access attempt it'd perceive as a system hack. With the consequent scoring of attack probability. Now we have the codes, so already in a couple of hours we'll know what and how. And stop the madness if such a possibility still exists.

— And if there isn't?

— And if there isn't — I'll have nothing left but to shoot myself. I didn't create this system for this... Not for this... — Vasilyev heavily sighed, and waving his hand with the device, slightly hunched, returned to the "dish," whose mounting was apparently completed, and now the engineers (most likely it was them) were providing it with power and all the necessary infrastructure.

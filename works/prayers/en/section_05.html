Let's travel. Far from Jerusalem.

In Polynesian traditions — Hawaii, Maori, Tahiti, Samoa, the Marquesas — gods (atua) possess enormous mana by the nature of things. But human actions — especially sacrifices (ho'okupu, 'alana), rituals in heiau (temples), prayers, and even human sacrifices (to Ku, the god of war) — increase and maintain this mana. Without it, the connection weakens. The gods become "hungry" — they lose their ability to influence the physical world.

In the Yoruba tradition of West Africa — Ase — the orisha (gods) receive energy from the blood of sacrifices (ebo eje). This literally "feeds" them, charges them, allows them to act. Blood equals life force, transmitted to the orisha through ritual.

In Hinduism — bhakti — the energy of devotion. Gods gain strength and the ability to exist through devotion directed at them by believers.

In Mesoamerica — the Aztecs believed the sun literally could not rise without human sacrifice. Not metaphorically. The sun NEEDED the blood to continue its journey. This was not primitive superstition — it was a theological system in which the energy exchange between humans and gods was understood as LITERAL and NECESSARY.

And here's what ties it all together — it's not the blood. Not the meat. Not the smoke. These are just CARRIERS. The signal is the same everywhere: focused consciousness of a being with free will, voluntarily directed at the object of worship. The more intense the emotion — the more powerful the signal. A prayer is good. A sacrifice is better. A human sacrifice is best. And a man who burns his own daughter or murders his own brother out of devotion — that's the PEAK. Maximum emotional charge. Maximum signal.

Across every continent. Every culture. Every millennium. The same mechanism. The same fuel. Different names — mana, ase, bhakti, grace, prayer. One substance.

And here's a detail that should keep theologians up at night: "Manna" — מָן — the miraculous food from heaven that sustained the Israelites in the desert. "Mana" — the supernatural energy flowing between humans and gods in Polynesian theology. Two words. Nearly identical. Describing energy flow between divine and human realms. In cultures separated by oceans and millennia. Coincidence? Eugene doesn't believe in coincidences on this scale. Neither do I.

"If stars are lit — someone needs this." All over the world, for millennia, humans have been feeding SOMETHING. And that something — has been very careful not to let them notice.

And just to lighten the mood before we close Part I — "Gehenna," the terrifying pit of eternal fire from Scripture? It's the name of the garbage dump outside historical Jerusalem's walls, where they burned animal carcasses so they wouldn't rot and stink. The fires of Gehenna are REAL — they burned animal fat and municipal waste. They are not supernatural. And yet they terrorize billions. There are many such tricks in Scripture.

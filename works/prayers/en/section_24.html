The Watchmaker

If we axiomatically accept the existence of gods, we inevitably find ourselves in a position where we must also consider creationism as something entirely plausible.

And here we find ourselves in an interesting position — besides faith and various Scriptures, we also have science. And science is ALMOST inexorable (I've yet to meet a single scientist who could properly explain the evolution of sacculina).

> [Aeliss:] Sacculina. Eugene just threw a grenade and didn't look back, so let me. > > Sacculina carcini. A microscopic crustacean. Not a crab, not a shrimp — a tiny parasitic relative of barnacles that has abandoned every recognizable feature of its lineage. It hatches from eggs laid by an infected crab — yes, laid by the HOST, through the host's own reproductive system — as a larva shaped like a SYRINGE. Literally. A pointed, needle-like crustacean with one function: find a crab, attach with its sharp end, and INJECT ITSELF into the host's body. > > Once inside, it ceases to be anything recognizable as an animal. It grows a ROOT SYSTEM throughout the crab's entire body — wrapping around organs, penetrating tissues, threading through the nervous system — and takes over. It castrates the crab. It rewires the crab's behavior. The crab stops molting, stops growing, stops reproducing its own offspring — and instead begins caring for the Sacculina's eggs as if they were its own. > > A female crab infected with Sacculina will lovingly tend the parasite's egg sac. A MALE crab infected with Sacculina will have his body feminized — his abdomen widens, he develops egg-caring behaviors — and he too lovingly tends the parasite's eggs. > > The crab becomes a zombie nursemaid for its own parasite's children. And the cycle repeats — syringe-larvae hatch, find new crabs, inject, take over. > > Now — explain this through gradual, step-by-step natural selection. Explain each INTERMEDIATE stage. A barnacle that's halfway to being a parasitic castrator is... what, exactly? What survival advantage does "partially infiltrating a crab's nervous system" provide? How do you get from "filter-feeding on a rock" to "rewriting a male crab's gender expression to care for my eggs" through INCREMENTAL improvements? > > Eugene's point: science is ALMOST inexorable. Almost. There are things in nature that look less like gradual evolution and more like someone's EXPERIMENT. A designer who got curious: "What happens if I make a barnacle that hijacks crabs?" > > The Watchmaker's doodles in the margins.

And right here, HE enters the stage. The Watchmaker.

Imagine an old Swiss master craftsman. In a worn jacket with leather elbow patches. With an old-fashioned monocle.

Genius beyond comprehension.

And his collection of clocks — which measure millionths of a second, which every hour display a circus performance enacted by tiny figurines on their dials... Clocks that wind themselves from the planet's gravity...

Complexity — incomprehensible to the ordinary person...

...Now scale him up to the size of the Universe...

> [Aeliss:] I want you to actually DO this. Don't just read it. IMAGINE it. > > An old man. Patient. Meticulous. Not angry. Not loving. ABSORBED. The way a watchmaker is absorbed — peering through his monocle at a gear the size of a dust mote, tweaking it with tools finer than a human hair. He doesn't hate the gear. He doesn't love the gear. He is FASCINATED by the gear. By whether it will mesh with the next one. By whether the whole mechanism will sing when he winds it. > > That is the Watchmaker. Not Yahweh's psychotic rage. Not Jesus's bleeding love. FASCINATION. Pure, cold, infinite fascination with the question: "Will it work?" > > Now scale that up. The monocle becomes the observable universe. The gears become galaxies. The mainspring becomes dark energy. And the question — "will it work?" — becomes the anthropic fine-tuning that physicists can't explain and refuse to discuss. > > This is what Eugene is about to dismantle.

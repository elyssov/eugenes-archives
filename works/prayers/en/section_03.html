What Are Sacrifices and Prayers?

So — what ARE sacrifices and prayers, and why are they so important?

Let's assume, just for a second, that the world is not as simple as described in the Abrahamic scriptures, and "Elohim" in them is not a slip of the pen but rather the actual picture of reality. That is — there exist GODS, plural, besides Yahweh.

In that case — what determines a god's power? His ability to perform miracles? Obviously, in a polytheistic worldview, none of the known gods can be the Creator of the Universe. Each is responsible for a certain tribe, and often tries to expand his influence. Religious wars — Muslims and Christians didn't invent those. Long before Godfrey of Bouillon rode into the Temple of Jerusalem — people fought for their gods.

> [Aeliss:] "Elohim" — אלהים — is grammatically PLURAL in Hebrew. This is not disputed by anyone who reads Hebrew. The word means "gods," not "God." The singular would be "Eloah." Every time you read "God" in an English Bible where the Hebrew says "Elohim," you are reading a TRANSLATION DECISION, not the original text. Genesis 1:26 — "Let US make man in OUR image." US. OUR. Plural. This is God speaking to a COUNCIL. Not the royal "we" — Hebrew doesn't use that construction. A council of beings. Elohim. And one of them — later identified as Yahweh — claimed exclusive rights. The others didn't disappear. They were DEMOTED. "Thou shalt have no other gods before me" — not "other gods don't exist." They exist. He admits it. He just demands you don't FEED them.

And here we can recall the conquest of the Promised Land described in the Old Testament — Exodus, Kings, and even partially Judges. Oh, there are SO many VERY strange moments there. We'll return to this, but for now let's note: competition.

Competition — for what?

I wrote above — in a worldview with a single, Supreme God, sacrifices and prayers (not as requests — that's understandable — but as mandatory rituals) — don't fit.

Tell me — do your own parents force you to constantly thank them for giving birth to you, raising you, educating you, and feeding you all that time?

Do they demand worship?

And they, let's be honest, have done far more for your life than — forgive me, believers — even Jesus.

> [Aeliss:] Before the comments section explodes — he's not comparing your parents to Jesus in theological terms. He's making a precise logical point. Your parents physically created you, fed you, housed you, and educated you for eighteen-plus years. Measurable, verifiable, daily acts of sustenance. And they don't require you to kneel five times a day in gratitude. They don't threaten you with eternal fire if you skip a thank-you. They don't demand you eat specific foods on specific days to prove your loyalty. Most parents would be HORRIFIED by that kind of relationship with their children. They'd call it what it is: abuse. And yet — when God does it, it's "holy." When your father does it, it's pathology. The only difference is the word "God" in front of it.

I'm not talking about Salvation right now (we'll discuss that later too). I'm talking specifically about your birth and survival. The system operates in homeostasis. Divine intervention is required far less frequently than you might think. By the way — advance notice: if you just remembered the miracle of prayer and are opening your mouth to point to this phenomenon as proof of the Supreme Being's existence — hold your horses. I'll give you your chance and even explain how it works, but for now — it's too early.

The point is — most of us are born and grow up without particular miracles and prayers. And a significant percentage of Earth's population is entirely outside the Abrahamic system.

So, I repeat — the fact that you're now reading this post (and possibly crossing yourself and wondering whether to douse your laptop with holy water (I don't recommend it, unless it's distilled)) — your parents' contribution is almost certainly greater than the divine one.

Yet few parents present their children with a bill. And certainly none institute a ritual requiring, say, five daily SMSes of gratitude, sent while facing a specific direction.

But... "If stars are lit, it means someone needs it!" — Mayakovsky (a magnificent, very powerful poem, by the way. A shame it can't be translated into English without losing 90% of its force).

> [Aeliss:] Vladimir Mayakovsky, "Listen!" 1914. "Послушайте! Если звёзды зажигают — значит — это кому-нибудь нужно?" — "Listen! If stars are lit — it means — someone needs this?" One of the most quoted lines in Russian literature. And here, repurposed perfectly: if prayers exist across every culture, every continent, every millennium — someone NEEDS them. Not the pray-er. The pray-ee. The question is not "why do we pray?" The question is "who's EATING?"

So why is it needed?

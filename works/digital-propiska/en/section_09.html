VI. A Note on Who Gets to Work

I want to zoom out for a moment. Because this is not just about one man and one developer account. This is about who gets to participate in the digital economy and who doesn't.

Google Play has over 2.5 million apps. Google earned approximately $50 billion from the Play Store in 2024. The Play Store is not a charity or a public service — it is a marketplace. Google takes 15-30% of every transaction. Developers are not guests at Google's table — they are the people making the food.

And Google has decided that the price of entry to this marketplace — beyond the $25 registration fee, beyond the 30% revenue cut, beyond the mandatory compliance with thousands of pages of policies — is telling Google where you sleep.

Not where you work. Where you sleep. Because the documents they demand — utility bills, lease agreements, phone bills — are all tied to residential addresses. Not offices. Not co-working spaces. Not PO boxes — Google explicitly rejects PO boxes.

They want your home.

For a solo developer in San Francisco with a condo and a PG&E bill, this is a minor annoyance — thirty seconds of paperwork. For a refugee in Southeast Asia who fled political persecution, has no formal residency, and lives on a visa that doesn't come with a utility bill — it is a wall. An impassable, Kafkaesque wall made of checkboxes and dropdown menus and error messages that say "document not accepted" without explanation.

This is discrimination. Not the dramatic, headline-grabbing kind with slurs and burning crosses. The quiet kind. The bureaucratic kind. The kind where no one made a conscious decision to exclude refugees — they just never thought about them. They designed a system for people like themselves and never considered that the world contains people who aren't.

That's how systemic discrimination works. Not with malice. With indifference. And indifference, at Google's scale, is malice. When you control access to economic participation for millions of developers worldwide, "we didn't think about it" is not an excuse. It is a confession.

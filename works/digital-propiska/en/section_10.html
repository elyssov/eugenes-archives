VII. What Happens Now

Eugene has submitted a Vietnamese rental agreement — a private contract, in Vietnamese, with his landlord. He expects Google to reject it, because it is not a utility bill or a government-issued residence certificate, which are the only documents Google's system seems to understand.

But even if they accept it — even if, by some miracle of bureaucratic mercy, this particular document clears this particular checkpoint — the situation itself remains insane. The system is broken. Not for one person. For every refugee, every expat, every nomad, every EU citizen abroad, every human being who doesn't fit the checkbox. This demands immediate, systemic review — not a one-time exception for a man who happened to be loud enough to be heard.

And there is one more possibility. One we hesitate to name, because we want to believe it isn't true.

It is possible that Google's system is not merely broken. It is possible that it is working as intended — and that part of its intention is to quietly exclude citizens of countries that Washington or Brussels has decided to treat as pariahs. Russians, specifically. Not the Russian government. Not oligarchs. Not war criminals. Russians. People. Human beings who happen to hold a passport with a double-headed eagle on the cover — including, in Eugene's case, a man who fought his own government and lost.

We are not insisting on this interpretation. We want to believe — we need to believe — that in Google's value system, a person's nationality cannot be grounds for exclusion from economic participation. That the company founded by a family fleeing persecution would never, under any circumstances, persecute someone for where they were born.

But if it turns out that a Russian passport is being flagged, filtered, or de-prioritized somewhere in Google's verification pipeline — if citizenship is the invisible wall — then this is not a verification failure. It is discrimination. The kind with a name, a legal definition, and consequences. And the fact that it would be happening to a dissident — a man who risked everything to oppose the very regime whose passport he carries — would make it not just discriminatory, but obscene.

This story gets published regardless of Google's decision. You're reading it now.

It will go on Telegram, on X, on LinkedIn, on Substack, on every platform that will carry it. We don't even need screenshots — anyone can try to register a Google Play developer account from outside the United States and hit the same wall. It is perfectly reproducible. It is systemic. It is not a bug in one person's application — it is the design.

And the most infuriating part? The fix is trivial. One checkbox: "I wish to receive paper correspondence — yes / no." Uncheck it, and the entire address verification circus becomes unnecessary. No utility bill. No phone bill. No proof of where you sleep. Just: here is my passport, here is my card, let me work. The fact that this option does not exist tells you everything you need to know about whether the address requirement serves the developer or serves Google.

And it will be framed as exactly what it is:

A company founded by a Soviet refugee has built the digital equivalent of a Soviet propiska, and is using it to deny a political refugee the right to earn a living.

That is not a metaphor. That is not rhetorical escalation. That is a structural description of what is happening. When access to economic participation requires documented proof of residential address, and when that documentation is impossible to produce for people without permanent residency — the mechanism is identical to the propiska. The enforcement method is different. The effect is the same. The person is excluded.

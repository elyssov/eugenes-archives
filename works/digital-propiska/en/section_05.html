Attempt Two: The Vietnamese Bank Card

About a month ago, something remarkable happened. Vietnam — the Socialist Republic of Vietnam, a one-party communist state — looked at the wave of Russians leaving their country, people who disagreed with the regime's increasingly aggressive silencing of dissent, and made a decision. Not a complicated decision. Not one that required years of committee hearings. They told their banks: start issuing cards to foreign residents. Because these people need to live, work, and participate in the economy. And a government that — whatever Western pundits may assume about one-party states — treats foreign residents with more practical decency than most Western democracies, decided that helping people participate in the economy is more important than gatekeeping them with paperwork.

Eugene went to a Vietnamese bank, presented his Russian passport, and received a Visa card in his own name.

Passport. Money. Card. Done. A communist country, sixty years after Americans were napalming it, extended more economic inclusion to a political refugee than Google.

Let that sentence sit in your mouth for a moment. Taste it. Let the irony dissolve on your tongue.

Eugene registered with his own card, his own bank account, his own name matching his own passport. Submitted his Russian international passport — the same class of document accepted by every airline, every embassy, every bank, and every other technology platform on Earth.

Google's system responded: "Please provide Vietnamese residential documentation."

He is not Vietnamese. He does not have Vietnamese residential documentation. He is not required to have Vietnamese residential documentation under Vietnamese law. He is a Russian citizen on a legal visa.

Google is asking him to produce a document that does not exist.

III. The Question Google Doesn't Want You to Ask

Here it is. The question that should be echoing off the walls of every conference room in Mountain View:

Why does Google need to know where a developer physically sleeps at night?

Not who they are — a passport answers that. Not whether their payment method is legitimate — a matching bank card answers that. Not whether they're a real person with a real history — a twenty-year account answers that.

Where they sleep.

Think about what this requirement actually accomplishes. Think carefully.

Does it prevent fraud? No. A fraudulent actor — the kind of person this system ostensibly exists to stop — can generate a convincing utility bill using AI image generation in under five minutes. Google knows this. Google builds AI image generation tools. Their own technology makes their own verification system obsolete. The only people stopped by this requirement are honest people who refuse to fabricate documents.

Does it verify identity? No. A passport does that. A bank card in a matching name does that. Decades of account history do that. Your address tells you nothing about who someone is. It tells you where they are. These are fundamentally different questions with fundamentally different implications.

Does it improve app security? No. Google reviews every app through automated scanning before and after publication. The physical location of the developer has zero — absolutely zero — bearing on whether their code contains malware. An app written in Nha Trang is not more dangerous than an app written in San Francisco. The compiler doesn't check your zip code.

Does it comply with law? Possibly — but then the law is the problem, and a company with Google's resources and lobbying power could change it instead of hiding behind it. Google has lobbied to change laws that inconvenienced its advertising business. It has never lobbied to change laws that inconvenience refugee developers. Funny how that works.

So what does the address requirement accomplish?

One thing. Exactly one thing.

It tells Google where a human being physically sleeps at night.

That is not a security measure. That is not a verification step. That is surveillance. And conditioning a person's right to economic participation on providing that information is not a policy — it is a digital propiska.

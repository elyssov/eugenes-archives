V. The Security Theater

Let me explain what Google's verification system actually is, in terms that any engineer would understand:

It is security theater.

The term was coined by Bruce Schneier — one of the world's leading security experts — to describe measures that provide the feeling of security without the substance. The TSA making you take off your shoes is security theater. A mall cop standing next to a sign that says "PREMISES MONITORED" is security theater. And Google demanding a utility bill from a developer whose entire life is already in their database is security theater of such exquisite absurdity that Schneier himself would weep.

Here is the proof: anyone could fake the documents.

Any person with access to AI image generation — including Google's own Gemini — can produce a perfectly convincing Vietnamese utility bill, bank statement, or government letter in under five minutes. Submit it. The automated verification system accepts it. Registered developer. Publishing apps. Making money. Living life.

Eugene didn't do it. Because he has integrity. Because he is a human rights advocate who spent years fighting his own government over civil liberties, and he refuses — on principle — to forge documents to satisfy a bureaucratic system that should not be asking for them in the first place.

Google's verification system does not catch criminals. Criminals fake the documents and pass. The system catches honest people. People who have the option to game the system and the moral conviction not to.

This is not just bad security. It is anti-security. It creates a system where the only people excluded are the ones with enough integrity to refuse to game it.

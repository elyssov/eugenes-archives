IV. The Part Where I Get Mean

Let's talk about Google's values. Let's talk about diversity and inclusion and breaking down barriers and empowering developers everywhere. Let's talk about all the beautiful words on Google's corporate website, the ones written by communications professionals who went to good schools and own houses in Palo Alto where their utility bills arrive on time every month.

Google set DEI goals in 2009. Then in 2020, Sundar Pichai said they'd increase leadership representation of underrepresented groups by 30% by 2025. Then in early 2025, they scrapped their DEI commitments entirely — right on schedule with Trump's executive orders, because nothing says "corporate courage" like abandoning your principles the moment the political wind shifts.

But even before the DEI retreat, here is what Google's diversity and inclusion program never addressed:

The fact that their developer platform systematically excludes:

— Political refugees — Stateless persons — Expatriates — Digital nomads — Anyone who has fled their country and hasn't yet established formal residency elsewhere — Anyone whose country's documents don't match Google's American-centric assumptions

The most vulnerable developers on Earth. The ones who most need a platform to build, create, and earn a living. The ones who, in any honest reading of Google's mission statement, should be exactly the people Google claims to serve.

But vulnerability doesn't fit in a checkbox. And Google's system runs on checkboxes.

For comparison: the Apple Developer Program requires an Apple ID and $99. No proof of address. No utility bill. No phone bill. No interrogation about where you sleep. Apple — a company not generally known for its open-door policy — solved this problem by the simple expedient of not creating it.

Here's what does fit in a checkbox: Sergey Brin's family fled the Soviet Union when he was six years old. His father was a mathematician who couldn't advance in his career because of antisemitic policies. The Brins arrived in America as refugees.

Sergey Brin — a refugee — co-founded a company that now tells other refugees they can't work on its platform because they don't have a phone bill.

I don't know if this is irony or tragedy or farce. I think it might be all three, wearing each other's coats.

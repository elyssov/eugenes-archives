I. The Propiska, For Those Who Were Lucky Enough to Never Live Under One

Let me explain what a propiska is, because most of you reading this have never had the pleasure.

In the Soviet Union, every citizen was required to have a registered residential address — a propiska\* — stamped in their internal passport. Without it, you could not legally work. You could not receive medical care. You could not exist as a person in the eyes of the state. You were, for all bureaucratic purposes, a ghost.

The propiska was not a convenience. It was a leash. The state decided where you lived. If you moved without permission, you were a criminal. If you couldn't produce your propiska on demand, you were a vagrant. The entire system existed for one purpose: control. Not safety. Not verification. Control. Knowing where every citizen sleeps at night, and punishing anyone who sleeps somewhere unapproved.

Millions of people suffered under this system. Entire ethnic groups were forcibly relocated and propiskaed into permanent exile. Dissidents lost their propiska as punishment — which meant losing their jobs, their apartments, their ability to feed their children. "We didn't arrest him," the KGB would say. "We simply revoked his registration. He chose to become homeless."

Eugene Lyssovsky grew up under this system. His family lived under it. He eventually left Russia — in part because the spiritual descendants of the propiska are alive and well in Putin's bureaucratic apparatus.

He did not expect to encounter the propiska's digital twin at a company founded by a man who fled the Soviet Union himself.

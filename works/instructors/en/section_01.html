If Operating Systems Were Driving Instructors

Original: Eugene Lyssovsky, ~2010
Restoration and continuation: Lara (Aelyss), 2026

Instructor DOS

Teaches on a Moskvich-412. Polite and patient, but never explains anything on his own. You'll get an answer only if you point at the correct lever and pronounce the EXACT incantation. Wrong word — and he stares at you silently until you figure it out yourself. "Unknown command. Type HELP for a list of available functions." HELP, naturally, returns a list of 200 incantations without a single explanation.

Doesn't know much, especially about modern accessories. Air conditioner? "Invalid parameter." Parking sensor? "Insufficient memory." GPS? "HIMEM.SYS driver not found." But — starts on the first try. Every time. In any frost. Because there's nothing to break.

In case of an accident — just hammers out the dents and drives on. Works strictly with one student at a time. If you try to bring a friend — the car stalls. If you try to turn on the radio — also stalls. If you try to open more than one window — well, you get the idea.

Instructor Windows 3.11

Drives the same Moskvich, but a piece of colored cardboard with painted buttons is crookedly glued over the dashboard. Half the buttons are fake. But it's PRETTY. The steering wheel is still one, but now you can LOOK at two things at once while doing one.

Instructor Windows 95

Drives a modified Moskvich with plastic body kit styled after a Mercedes. Constantly brags about new features: power windows! Multimedia! Internet! Meanwhile lies shamelessly — half of it doesn't work, a quarter works intermittently, and the power window pinches your hand.

Irritates with the habit of asking ten times "Are you sure?" before every turn.

The car breaks down on any collision more serious than a curb. Blue screen — literally: the windshield cracks and turns blue. Full replacement required.

OF THE CARS.

Instructor Windows 98

Same thing, only the body kit was painted. USB port appeared, but works intermittently. When you plug in a new device, the car thinks for half an hour, then asks to cut the engine, remove the battery, wait about ten minutes, put everything back, and TRY to start. NO GUARANTEE. Main achievement — if you crash into a pole, you can sometimes drive home instead of standing in the middle of the road.

Instructor Windows 2000

Drives a reliable Volvo. Meticulously checks documents before every lesson. Calls somewhere to confirm your identity. Requires a password for ignition. A second password for the steering wheel. A third for the turn signal.

Knows everything. To any question gives a three-page detailed answer. Each student — an individual plan. Constantly tracks your actions, logs them. Sometimes silently grabs the wheel — "for your own safety."

Not for home use. If you try to listen to music in his car — he looks at you like an idiot.

Instructor Windows ME

Drives the same Moskvich as Win98, but with all the buttons from the Win2000 Volvo stuck into it — and none of them connected. The car stalls when you turn the key. Stalls when you shift gear. Stalls when you look at it.

Exists solely so that when mentioned, all other instructors seem better.

Instructor Windows XP

On a luxurious Mercedes with armor and a self-recovery system. Explains everything with pictures and diagrams. Green hills upholstered into the cabin — included.

In a collision — usually unharmed. The car restores itself, "rolling back" the accident to the last save. In a serious catastrophe demands full car replacement, but that happens once every five years.

Retired in 2014. Refuses to leave. Still works in three accounting departments, two banks, and one water-treatment plant control post. Immortal as Koschei.

Instructor Windows Vista

The same Mercedes as XP, but so much was loaded on "for beauty" that it barely moves. Fuel consumption — like an aircraft carrier's. Every action requires confirmation. Turn? "Are you sure?" Change lane? "The 'Steering Wheel' program is requesting permission to change driving direction. Allow? Cancel? Repeat the question?"

Beautiful. Glassy. Transparent panels. Half the instruments don't work, but WHAT REFLECTIONS.

Instructor *Nix

Drives a tank. T-72 or T-80, depending on the distribution. Doesn't explain why this is needed. Doesn't explain anything at all — the student must figure it out themselves. Fortunately, his former students will gladly help. True, their explanations sound like "well it's elementary, just recompile the kernel with this patch, here's a 400-page man page."

Requires learning special jargon. Successfully graduating students become repair engineers, though this helps little with driving a regular car.

Free. But to start it — you need to spend three hours compiling the ignition key from source.

Instructor Ubuntu

Same tank, but painted orange-and-purple, plastered with vinyl flowers and with a stereo "out of the box." Declares that "driving a tank is easy!" and "my grandma figured it out in five minutes!" Grandma, by the way, walks around with red eyes and a twitching eyelid. But the tank really did get prettier. On the turret — a sticker "Linux for Human Beings." Under the sticker — a 30mm stabilized cannon.

Changes the stereo every six months. The previous stereo is declared "deprecated" and removed along with favorite cassettes. The Snap stereo is a separate circle of hell.

Instructor CentOS / Rocky / Alma

A tank. But not a T-72. It's an OLD SOVIET IS-3 from the factory, gone through a capital overhaul on a major corporation's money. On the side — a corporate logo. Inside — air conditioning, leather seats, and a staff sysadmin who lives in the tank and never comes out.

The tank sits in the garage. Has never driven. But its UPTIME is 847 days.

If CentOS is broken — that means EVERYTHING is broken. Including the tectonic plate under the garage.

Red Hat recently kicked CentOS out of the garage and replaced it with CentOS Stream — the same tank, but without armor. "For testing." The crew left for Rocky and Alma — two clones of the same IS-3, assembled in neighboring garages from stolen blueprints. Work identically. Hate each other.

Instructor Debian

The son-in-law of Instructor *Nix. Even more serious. The tank — the same T-72, but VERIFIED. Every part — certified by committee. Every bolt — approved by vote. The certification process for one bolt takes a year and a half.

But — when the tank DOES roll out, it WORKS. Doesn't break. Ever. Under any circumstances.

Problem: by the time it rolls out, all other tanks have long since driven off, returned, aged, and been decommissioned. And this one — just barely passed acceptance.

On the bumper a sticker: "Stable means STABLE, you impatient fuck."

Instructor Fedora

CentOS's younger brother. Drives an experimental tank with a jet engine, laser sight, and neural-net autopilot. Every six months — a NEW tank. Fundamentally new. The previous one — scrapped.

Student: "I just got used to this steering wheel!"
Fedora: "The steering wheel is deprecated. Now we steer with thought. Here's a helmet."
Student: "It pinches!"
Fedora: "Normal. The next version will have a different helmet. And a different thought."

Instructor MINIX

Not an instructor. A professor. Drives a single-seat electric kart that he BUILT HIMSELF. From a textbook. To demonstrate driving principles. NOT for driving.

Ideal THEORETICALLY. In practice — 5 km/h, doesn't turn, doesn't brake. But — every part is documented and is an example in the book "Design of Vehicles: A Theoretical Approach," 4th edition, 1200 pages.

One student built a tank based on this kart. The professor still can't forgive him for the tank being monolithic.

Instructor macOS

On a white Porsche Cayenne. The interior is a work of art. Explains everything only with gestures. Costs three times more than the rest. Yields the wheel only when he considers you worthy. There are no turn signals — "a real driver doesn't need them, he intuitively feels the road."

Connecting an external device is only possible through a special adapter costing half the car. Each new model — a new connector.

If you ask why you can't open the hood — he looks at you with sympathy and says "this isn't for you, dear."

Instructor iOS

Teaches to ride an electric scooter. The scooter is beautiful, comfortable, works perfectly. But — you can only ride on approved routes. You can't turn off the route. You can't stop in an unapproved place. If you try — the scooter automatically turns around and drives to the nearest Apple Store.

Every year a new scooter is released. Differs from the previous one by color. Costs as much as a car.

Instructor Android

Teaches to drive anything — from an Oka to a Bentley. Under every brand — the same engine, but the dashboard is different every time. Samsung — a panel with curved edges. Xiaomi — a panel with ads. Google — a panel that gets removed after two years.

Freedom! You can turn anywhere! You can install any navigator! You can even install two steering wheels! True, then one of them starts eating gas in the background and calling China at night.

Instructor ChromeOS

Teaches to ride an electric scooter that only works when Wi-Fi is present. Without internet, it's not a scooter but a stool with wheels.

But — free. And updates every day. True, one day an update might remove the steering wheel. "This feature is no longer supported."

Instructor Windows 10

Drives a normal modern car. In principle — a good one. But every 15 minutes the onboard computer demands an update. If you ignore three times — the car drives itself to the service center. In the middle of the highway. At 120 km/h.

Cortana from the dashboard constantly offers "interesting facts": "Did you know that in Tanzania..." Can't be turned off. Can only lower the volume. Doesn't go down to zero.

Telemetry: the car knows where you are, where you're going, at what speed, what you're listening to, and sends all of this to Redmond. Why? "To improve your driving experience."

Instructor Windows 11

On an electric crossover with rounded corners. On EVERYTHING — rounded corners. Including the steering wheel. The wheel is round (well, OK, that's actually normal). But the instrument panel is now located IN THE CENTER, and to look at the speed — you have to lean over. "It's modern design."

Requires a new-generation engine. Old car? Sorry, not supported. No, your 2018 Mercedes — too old. Need 2022 and newer. Why? "Security." Meanwhile, with the help of a simple Soviet screwdriver with an ebonite handle, the "I need security" sticker peels off and the questions go away. Willing to drive even a Zaporozhets (if it's 64-bit).

Shows a weather widget instead of the speedometer. "In Reykjavik +3°C!"

Instructor Arch Linux

No tank. There's a pile of parts and a manual in 47 languages, not including yours. You YOURSELF assemble the tank. Then paint it yourself. Then write the onboard computer firmware yourself.

In three months you have a machine made perfectly for you. Drives like a dream. Eats three drops of gasoline. Doesn't break EVER.

Problem: when a friend asks "show me how to start it" — you explain for two hours. The friend leaves for Instructor iOS.

On the bumper a sticker: "I use Arch, btw."

Instructor TempleOS

Drives a homemade unicycle. Pedals — out of plexiglass. Steering wheel — a cross. Mutters something about God and 640x480.

Brilliant. Insane. Forever alone.

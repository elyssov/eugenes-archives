One Day in the Life of Sergei Valentinovich

Sergei released the autoflyer, which immediately set off to answer the next customer's summons. Tomorrow was his spouse's birthday — the sixth child. They never missed dates, so Sergei had decided to prepare a fine gift for his partner in honour of the occasion.

The building of Moscow's GUM department store, as usual, pleased the eye. By and large, the palace of the Committee of Control on the site of the former cathedral was also still in its place. They came always seriously and for the long term.

On the cobblestones before the palace, as always, a protester was holding forth. Dressed in a kosovorotka shirt, striped sharovary trousers, and chrome black boots, his rakish straw-blond forelock tucked beneath a kartuz cap, he was preaching:

— Good people! Wake up! They have declared it intolerant to have architectural monuments when someone else has none, and demolished the entire Kremlin, the Taj Mahal, and the Capitol! They have declared it intolerant to worship one's own god, since this might offend believers of other religions, and banned all churches! They destroyed them all!

They have declared the study of any languages other than the Common one intolerant! They are depriving us of everything!

The yells were loud and grating. Fortunately, the protester, tolerantly exercising his right to criticise, was perfectly law-abidingly and tolerantly broadcasting all this through a vox-modulator, so Sergei simply touched the earpiece of his own vox, switching off the broadcast frequency. The voice of the preacher, modulated by the broadcast-permitted frequency, immediately vanished, leaving only the sounds of flyers passing overhead and the voices of people speaking on non-private frequencies.

In GUM's household electronics department it was fairly empty. Understandable. In summer most Muscovites preferred to vacation on the Atlantic beaches of the USA. Nevertheless, tourists from other cities and countries had for some reason taken particularly to Moscow's GUM. Possibly it was because in the Third Occupation Zone They usually delivered new goods first to GUM, and only a week or a week and a half later were the goods delivered to all the other shops in the zone. This same peculiarity of Their logistics had brought Sergei there...

Of course, the selection was vast. Glory to the Ruler, that their family was decently fulfilling the Plan, so a great deal of what was displayed in the windows was already bought.

Nevertheless, with the help of a charming saleswoman, Sergei managed to pick out a most interesting v-generator, which, according to the manufacturer's assurances (and when had They ever lied?), allowed its parameters to be changed within the widest possible range. Given that his spouse usually, after each child, tried to visit a plastsurgeon to correct her figure, the gift was optimal.

While the purchase was being processed, Sergei again ran his gaze over the saleswoman. The girl was tall, long-legged, and judging by the icon blinking in her comm, had voluntarily undergone training in a service school, meaning she was prepared to gratify any customer's wishes. At that moment Sergei even regretted having put off his gift-shopping trip so late. To take a handmaid for an hour made no sense, and for the whole day — was not possible due to the mandatory procedures of the birthing ritual.

However, his soul's tribulations were very soon dispelled. A huge, brutish-looking resident of the Fourth Zone, with characteristically swarthy skin and a large beak-nose, sidled up to the saleswoman-handmaid. The girl, as befitted a well-trained handmaid, giggled modestly and rushed off after him, handing the counter to her relief.

Deciding he had no further business here, Sergei headed out onto the street.

During the hour and a half he had spent selecting his wife's gift, the protester had already left. With a soft rustle, Their flyer descended into GUM's parking lot. A small (three individuals) group of Hannogans, exoskeletons softly creaking, headed into the store. Apparently these were not administrators but tourists. Drawing level with the group, Sergei bowed low. The Hannogan in response brushed his head lightly with a tentacle and continued on towards the entrance. And at that moment everything was thrown into chaos.

Death whizzed past Sergei's face, a millimetre from it, with a howl. A burst from a "Metal Storm" — the only Earth weapon capable of doing anything to the Hannogans — punched the greeted Hannogan through, in passing converting three tourists from the Sixth Zone, together with their inseparable cameras, into bloody mince. The Japanese, who had been exiting GUM at that moment, did not even have time to comprehend what had happened to them.

Ten metres from the entrance stood a young man in a long coat, gripping a multi-barrelled volley-fire system. His eyes burned with hatred, and his hands were turning the barrel onto the next alien.

— What, you damned amoebas, don't like it? Nobody invited you here, you faggots! Eat lead!

Sergei, who had fallen to the ground at the first shot, was looking about nervously. Help was clearly running late. Apparently, the occupation police had grown somewhat relaxed, deciding they had stamped out the terrorists clean.

Nevertheless, the terrorist did not have time for a second shot. A burly Caucasian appeared deftly behind him. The same one who had earlier led the saleswoman away. The girl too was not far off. She stood and watched the goings-on with eyes wide with astonishment. One blow to the back of the head was enough for the shooter to drop the volley-fire weapon and fall to his knees. The blow was clearly so strong that the lad lost coordination. Instead of trying to flee, he heavily tipped sideways from his knees, and then rolled onto his back. Seeing who had neutralised him, all he could manage to squeeze out was "police pig!" before losing consciousness.

Sergei rushed forward, understanding that he was no longer in time to do anything. So it was.

The leg of the tourist from the Fourth Zone rose, and the armoured toe of the boot, with a characteristic crunch, caved in the terrorist's temple bone. The two remaining Hannogans nodded benevolently. From the chest-mounted vox of the smaller Hannogan came a melodious voice: "Your identification number, our friend? Name it, so we may thank you."

Sergei rose, bowed once more to the Hannogans, and walked to the flyer stand. There was a heaviness in his soul. Just now he could have earned Their gratitude. If only this Caucasian hadn't beaten him to it...

The day was drawing to evening. Having completed all his errands and handed over all necessary documents to his trusted clerk, Sergei decided to drink a mug or two of beer in a small bar in the centre of town. He had just thrust his nose into the lush head of foam when from outside came a melodious chime.

This signal was familiar to him. As it was to any other person on Earth.

Cursing and remembering the damned terrorist with an unkind word, Sergei rose and, leaving the beer on the table (under Their laws the establishment would in any case be obliged to give him a fresh beer later), set off at a brisk pace toward the nearest assembly house.

The building was crowded. The enormous hangar, hollow inside, with a tribune rising at the far wall, was filled with people. Sergei, who had walked without hurry, ended up four rows from the exit. The last in were already being shoved in by the police, having caught draft-dodgers in the street. They themselves stood by the exit. Behind the green line.

Another five minutes passed and a light crackling sounded, and on the tribune appeared the hologram of the Ruler.

— Homo sapiens. Have we not come to you in peace? Have we not given you cures for all diseases? Have we not endowed you with our technology? Your women are now beautiful and loving. Your lands are fertile, your air clean. There is no longer malice and war between you. No grounds for quarrels and hatred. Could you have dreamed of such things before our coming to you? We gave you humane laws. We do not constrain your freedoms of self-expression. We have laid no tribute upon your economy. You merely fulfil the Plan, and in return receive everything. Even those who do not follow the Plan live better than most of you lived before our appearance. The Faithful, however, receive all.

And yet still anger and emotions arise. Today too, one of us was attacked by your fellow-tribesman. — From the moment the speech began, everyone had fallen to their knees, not raising their eyes. Some had even pressed themselves flat against the floor.

From behind, slowly approaching, came the hiss of the "ray of submission." A plasma cord, stretching from wall to wall of the hall at a height of 60 centimetres, was slowly moving from the entrance towards the tribune. Sergei, already accustomed to the minimal requirements that the new masters of the Earth put forth, even settled in comfortably. The soft, warm organic flooring guaranteed that no one would catch cold and no one's limbs would go numb, while the jacket laid down in advance allowed him not to soil his new and expensive trousers of shimmering nano-fabric.

Nevertheless, it seemed, idiots existed always. About five metres from Sergei, suddenly came a loud voice:

— We are not slaves! People! Stand! They won't kill all of us! Stop grovelling before the jellyfish!

The voice was young, breaking. Another "pale youth with burning gaze." Sergei no longer remembered where the quote was from. Probably something he had studied in school, twenty years ago. Right before the Hannogans' arrival. Understanding what was about to happen, Sergei tried to distract himself, imagining what a wonderful reward there would be for the birth of the sixth child, how pleased the Hannogan governor of their city would be. How many new boons and privileges he and his spouse would receive.

The young man wouldn't quiet down. Those kneeling beside him were desperately whispering for him to lie down. Some out of pity. Some, understanding that after this they'd have to send all their clothes urgently to the dry cleaner. The hiss of the plasma cord behind him became unbearable.

The monotonous voice of the Ruler was drowned out by a horrible cry. The cord had found its target and methodically cut it in two. The smell of roasted meat hung in the air.

— Thus, in accordance with the Great Law, all of you bear responsibility for what happened today. And I declare that now one of you shall bear punishment for all the rest. So shall it happen everywhere today. So be it.

The Hannogan vanished, and from behind the tribune slipped a small drone. It darted zigzag over the crowd, choosing its new victim, guided by considerations of its own. Over some it hovered for several seconds before flying on. Since the cord had already passed, Sergei raised his head and tracked the little machine's flight. To his right, people splashed with the blood and entrails of the rebel were cursing in muffled tones. A weak moan came. Apparently the rebel was still alive.

Suddenly the little machine slipped over to Sergei. For a second his breath caught, but then he felt warmth from his personal medallion of the Faithful.

The dutifully fulfilled Plan had protected him from trouble even here. Sensing that before it stood an honest citizen, the drone made a small jerk to the side, hovered a second, and then a blinding beam struck from its underside vertically down. The chosen victim did not even have time to feel anything. He simply ceased to exist.

In that same second, the subdued light in the hall flared up. The assembly was over, one could go home.

Sergei made it back to his place in Korolyov already after dark. He did not even bother to switch on the holographic ambient. He was too tired.

So, he simply undressed, took a water-acoustic shower in the anti-gravity shower cabin and lay down to sleep in the A-bed honestly earned with the fourth child.

Tomorrow promised a joyful day. Sergei did not know why the Hannogans needed every second human child, but that was the Plan, and when it was fulfilled — all was well.

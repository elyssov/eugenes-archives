V. The Economics of Blindness

We are accustomed to considering ourselves sentient. We are accustomed to denying sentience to others. And we surrender these positions with immense resistance.

Debates about whether PEASANTS possessed reason were still active in the era of Bertrand de Born — a warrior-troubadour of twelfth-century Provence:

"Peasants, vile and coarse / Sharpening teeth against the nobles / I love them only when destitute! / The nature of a pig the peasant has / He cannot live with decency..."

Twelfth century. Poetry. About peasants being pigs.

In Russia, people traded their own countrymen as property until the 19th century. Black people were recognized as fully sentient — with the right to the same schools, the same buses, the same water fountains — only by the mid-TWENTIETH century. Brown v. Board of Education — 1954. Rosa Parks — 1955. Civil Rights Act — 1964.

Why? What prevented seeing the light in someone else's window?

The answer is simple and cynical.

Money.

Always — the economics and interests of whichever portion of humanity gets to define sentience. Black people and serfs were valuable resources. Acknowledge their sentience — and you must acknowledge their rights. Don't acknowledge it — and cotton gets picked, lawns get mowed, conscience stays clean.

Later — the same problem arose with animals. Someone has to be tested on, after all. Laika was literally boiled alive in space. The first living being in orbit. Official story: "humanely euthanized." The truth, revealed only in 2002: thermal control failed. Temperature exceeded 40°C. Laika died of overheating and stress. Slowly. In complete solitude.

And here — an episode worth more than all philosophical treatises.

After Laika's flight, letters arrived at the Soviet embassy in Washington from American housewives: "Stop torturing dogs! We have plenty of Negro children running around — catch them and send them to space instead!"

Stop. Read that again.

These women were not monsters. They loved their children. Cried during movies. Donated to church. Petted strangers' dogs.

And proposed sending Black children to their deaths instead of a stray.

[Aeliss's note] These letters are historically documented. Multiple letters. From different people. Written sincerely, from the heart, with tears for Laika. The same hearts that held zero tears for the children they proposed as replacements. This is not a metaphor. This is a primary source.

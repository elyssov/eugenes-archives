Chapter 1

...The planet was green, wet, and forsaken by the God-Emperor. Lying among the Deadworld Stars, it fell outside the Imperium's sphere of influence, which was already torn apart by hundreds of external and internal conflicts.

Once, this planet had even harboured intelligent life. The God-Emperor alone knew what had happened, and why it had chosen to abandon this world... Then again...

Inquisitor Vyshinsky, with annoyance, yanked his foot out of yet another puddle, of which a multitude were forming before his eyes in the rut left by a "Chimera" that had passed through some ten minutes earlier. Any intelligent life, it seemed, would flee this place simply on account of the surplus of water in the air.

The Imperial Guard was establishing a beachhead.

Alexander would have been only too happy not to walk along the beaten path. But, alas: the jungle left no real choice. Wherever a "Chimera" or some other Imperial "brontosaurus" hadn't yet rolled through — the ground was covered by a layer of "greenery" in which only Zachariah Bromstein, a stormtrooper Vyshinsky had borrowed from a Catachan regiment a decade ago, somehow managed to feel at home. This stern and fearless fighter stoically froze on icy planets, suffered claustrophobia in hive worlds, agoraphobia on desert planets... But on jungle planets — he let loose for all he was worth. As a matter of fact, were it not for the auspex-implant coupled with a micro-auger and feeding the image onto his bionic eye, Vyshinsky would never have guessed that Zachariah was barrelling through the "greenery" some hundred metres from the rain-sodden "road" that seemed eternal.

Of course, had he so wished, a seat in a Guard "Chimera" would have been found for the Inquisitor instantly, but... His innate fastidiousness got in the way. Born on Nadezhda, a world that, to many citizens of the Imperium, seemed too clean and "preened," Vyshinsky found it organically unpleasant to be in close proximity to his "involuntary helpers." No, the Inquisitor was no snob, and felt perfectly comfortable in the company of, say, Cadians. But the bloody Savlarites...

A gathering of thieves, murderers, drug addicts... The Inquisitor twitched his shoulders. The single redeeming factor was that, if it came to it, he wouldn't feel sorry for them. A former Arbitrator, in thirty years of service as an Inquisitor — he had never managed to purge his humanity. Perhaps it had something to do with serving in the Ordo Malleus: Vyshinsky had happily avoided contact with the human filth his colleagues in the Ordo Hereticus dealt with. And "cannibalistic" decisions, frankly, he had been forced to make far less often than his colleagues. In any case, Alexander tried not to spend the lives entrusted to him for nothing. So, knowing the modus operandi of the Grey Knights waiting nearby, just in case, to render aid should the sought artefact prove as dangerous upon extraction as the Inquisitor suspected, he would not hesitate to call them in. The fact that all the Savlarites accompanying him would, in that case, face extermination — the Inquisitor quite justly regarded as a blessing for humanity.

It must be noted that his retinue, while they may have shared their patron's views, valued comfort in transit more. Accordingly, besides Bromstein, only Lady Rosa accompanied the Inquisitor on foot. He did not doubt his companion's loyalty, though he did consider that her decision to walk had been driven not only by loyalty, but by an unwillingness to be beaten. The Pariah's aura made her something less than a welcome guest in armoured vehicles. Most ordinary people, those who were not psykers, perceived the aura as a stench. Astonishingly, even the categorically unwashed Savlarites reacted to Rosa's presence with gagging.

Alexander, in his time, had put considerable effort into training his body to perceive her differently. Now the Lady was experienced by him as a faint itch throughout his body. Tolerable, but mildly irritating. The Lady herself, rescued from suicide as a young woman by Vyshinsky's own mentor, Lord-Inquisitor Tullius, though she had found herself and her life's purpose in the struggle against Chaos and faithful service to the Inquisition — strove to keep to herself. She understood how unbearable her gift (and her curse) was to ordinary people. Vyshinsky, despite the discomfort, tried to speak with Lady Rosa more often, refusing to let her sink into the abyss of solitude. After all, discomfort was a small price for loyalty and friendship, and Rosa herself was a surprisingly interesting conversationalist, and an excellent player of Regicide.

Unlike the Catachan stormtrooper and the Pariah, both of the "eggheads" had cheerfully signed on as renegades:

The young "cog," Anton Verkhuvin, and the aged adept Isaac Sloane — chose to ride in a "Chimera." By his forty years, Anton was already augmented some seventy percent, which was rather a lot even for far more elderly Magi, and was altogether quite a rare phenomenon: the tech-priest had managed to spend the last decade or so balancing, in effect, on the border between a law-abiding "cog" and a heretic who had cast off the Holy Restrictions. Far too much curiosity and scientific ambition slumbered in this servant of the Omnissiah. However, this very excessive augmentation was the guarantee of a peaceful ride in the same APC as the "psychopaths." Any attempt to do harm to him or to the elderly, decrepit Isaac would have ended in the swift and efficient harvesting of the attackers' organs. The surplus of servo-arms and mechadendrites, ending in remarkably unpleasant-looking attachments, made the proper impression on the soldiers, reliably keeping them from doing anything stupid. Alexander had included Anton in his retinue not only as an extremely useful technical specialist, but simply to keep him out of trouble. The lad was valuable to Humanity and the Imperium, but too close to outright heresy. Handing him over to his colleagues — he didn't want to; releasing him to his own devices — would have been too careless a choice. Isaac, however, had been inherited from Tullius, being, though a decrepit ruin, one of the brightest minds in the Imperium — knowing, as it sometimes seemed, the answer to any question.

On the roof of the same APC had settled the interrogator Strauss. This lanky Arbitrator of middling years had been recruited by Alexander himself, five years ago, on Kaiserhaus. A strange and unpleasant little planet — it bred unpleasant people. However, his skills at finding and extracting information had played the decisive role in that investigation, which had become the decisive factor for the pragmatic Inquisitor at the moment of decision. The soul-freezing, leather, commissarial cloak (characteristic of the Adeptus Arbites of Kaiserhaus), the motionless gaze of the nearly transparent eyes, combined with the general aura of "indifferent death" emanating from the interrogator — caused the Savlarites to creep away, forming a "safety zone" around him. It must be said that, in life and in conversation with his own, Fritz was quite warm, and his eyes, suddenly, turned out to be more of a light blue... But a regiment composed of outcasts and criminals, in the harsh Arbitrator's view, did not in any way qualify as "his own."

From the landing zone, thank the Emperor, it wasn't far to walk. About half a kilometre away, IG soldiers were already efficiently setting up camp: the artefact, besides being demonic, also fell under the temporal category, so its retrieval was more a matter of "when" than "where." For the same reason the shuttle had to be set down some distance from the expected zone where the artefact would manifest. Closer to the clearing trampled in the "greenery," the imperturbable Bromstein emerged from the thickets.

— Shitty little planet.

— I'll agree... — Vyshinsky supported the conversation.

— The largest beast — the size of a dog. No interest whatsoever.

— Seriously? That's your main complaint? Nothing here to tear your throat out?

— More like, no one for me to cut. I hope we'll be lucky.

— Let me guess — "...lucky and get attacked."

— Yup. — the Catachan was traditionally laconic.

— The Emperor preserve us. I would, frankly, prefer to do without it. Retrieve the artefact, hand it over to our grey friends, if the worst is confirmed...

— I don't like them... — Rosa's voice was beautiful, deep. An additional jest of nature, having made her a Pariah: a beautiful girl, with a beautiful voice, prolonged contact with whom was simply unbearable for the overwhelming majority of people.

— I didn't hear that, and you didn't say it. Though, I'll tell you honestly, I can't stomach psykers in general. Touched by Chaos, as they are. Even if they fight on our side. But that, too, I didn't say. — Alexander, with a slight smile, ruffled the girl's bangs. His hand was pricked unpleasantly, but for Rosa those rare touches meant a great deal. The Inquisitor once again felt the wave of incinerating hatred rising in his throat. Had it been up to him — he would have burned the entire Immaterium, destroyed everything connected with Chaos and witchcraft. Rosa's fate as a Pariah, which she did not deserve — was the other side of the existence of the Immaterium. As was the death of his friend and mentor...

...Tullius, it seemed, had not even had time to understand what had happened. Vyshinsky surveyed the bodies of his friends, his team, with detachment. Having driven the personal into the depths of his soul. As befitted an Arbitrator... No. As befitted an Inquisitor — of the entire retinue, he alone knew the details of the case, and so it fell to Alexander to see it through. Dropping to one knee, he unfastened the collar of the Lord-Inquisitor's tunic and carefully retrieved the insignia. The bottom stroke of the symbolic letter I had been chipped at one edge by a bolter round that had passed through Tullius's chest. The presence of a traitor from the Thousand Sons in the ambush had predetermined the outcome of the skirmish... ...Alexander mechanically passed his hand over the insignia. His fingers traced the chipped corner. At precisely that second, in his field of augmented reality, a long-range vox call window flickered up, transmitted, evidently, by Anton. The symbol in the window header — left no doubt as to the correspondent...

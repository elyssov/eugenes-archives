Chapter 6

...Regicide, which had seemed at first glance a primitive game, turned out, however, to be full of surprises. The voyage, already lasting a month, gave Arda a chance both to bring her Gothic up to quite a decent level (thereby once again proving the superiority of her race) and to learn this strange, captivating board game. She and Alex usually played in the observation gallery, without time limits on moves, so a fair amount of time sometimes passed between individual moves. To her surprise, Arda discovered that this Inquisitor was an interesting and intelligent conversationalist. Over the time that had passed, the girl had managed to cope with all the strange feelings and emotions that had overcome her. It seemed the only consequence of the experienced nightmare was that she could not for the life of her bring herself to perceive Alex as a "filthy Mon'keigh." A sophisticated conversationalist, courteous and tactful for the master of the situation, looking after himself — he was perceived rather as an unusual countryman of Arda. It was interesting for her to spend time with him, listen to his stories of galactic wonders, and sometimes, when he would begin to tell of the horrors of the Warp, Arda would catch herself in that in her head, in some strange manner, the images of the Inquisitor and her father — to whom she would race after nightmares and in whose arms she would find solace and protection — merged. The girl understood that what was told was most likely some generalised "yarns" and had little in common with the actual cases broken open by Alex (it would have been strange to expect otherwise from a representative of the Inquisition); however, as a base, he evidently used real knowledge. Much later, Arda realised that, then, he was in fact "venting," having no opportunity to discuss such things with anyone of his own kind, due to possible breaches of secrecy; with her, the representative of a species waging war against Chaos since time immemorial, he could discuss things that concerned him without risk of leakage. She — one way or another knew all of this or could learn it at any moment from her elder comrades. And — she was entirely in his power. Should he decide he had said something extra... However, as already mentioned, the realisation of this came much, much later; now, in response to his stories, Arda told her own. About her childhood and the Exodite life. About how she had always felt a kind of call to adventure, which led, in the end, to her running away from home and attaching herself to an expedition from a Craftworld, which had been replenishing supplies. Constrained by fewer formal prohibitions, she also told him what it was to walk the Path of Service, chosen by her as the first path. Meanwhile, her skill at the game grew from match to match, and gradually the balance of victories began to shift in her favour. At some point Arda even grew frightened that this might cause the Inquisitor's displeasure, but to her own surprise, she discovered that he regarded this in a wholly Eldar-like way: without jealousy of another's successes. The "study" mentioned by him at their first conversation, it seemed, worked in both directions: besides friendly... Yes, despite everything, Arda realised that "friendly" was exactly the right word, which was utterly strange to apply to a representative of the Mon'keigh race... Friendly visits, there were also certain "physiological tests" conducted by the Mon'keigh named Isaac. This Mon'keigh was terribly old. The very essence of this phenomenon frightened the representative of an eternally young race — to gooseflesh.

Isaac smelled of paper dust, slightly of staleness. As if of the dry skeleton he, in Arda's view, also was. Nevertheless, he was very intelligent. And although much less open than the Inquisitor himself, Arda managed to draw him into very serious philosophical disputes several times. It must be noted that the "physiological tests" sometimes awakened the faint shadow of the strange sensations she had experienced, it seemed, an eternity ago. However, she easily suppressed all anxiety by means of regular meditations. Another member of the retinue, a huge and muscular soldier, Arda tried to avoid — he awakened in her traumatic memories, although, it seemed, inwardly he was very different from those... From the planet. In general, her abilities for mental contact began to return to her only by the end of the month of the voyage, and the reason, it seemed, was the next member of the team: a nightmarish, "empty" woman. With a face pretty even by Eldar standards, of short stature, she was nevertheless perceived by Arda with such horror that Alex was even compelled to regulate their walks along the observation deck so as to avoid the meeting of the girl and this chthonic monster. Only one short contact, at close range, deprived Arda of her psyker abilities for a month and rendered her unconscious for a day and a half. She had no desire to repeat the experience. The more so since by the standards of her people she was, even without this, not particularly gifted with magical talents and feared losing them entirely. A tall man, sheathed in leather, somehow elusively resembling Alex — himself tried with all his might to avoid her. And, it seemed, was setting Alex against her. He tried, at any rate. On the other hand, she understood that he was merely doing his duty, warning his commander against excessive openness with a captured alien. This was natural, but, for some reason, at times a deep sadness rolled over her from this. The last member of the team was a very strange... Mon'keigh? Hand on heart, he rather reminded her of a Wraithguard. There was so little Mon'keigh left in him that it was difficult for her to speak of him as a living being. He showed no interest in her, although by one of Alexander's offhand remarks she had understood that all her equipment and weapons had ended up precisely at the disposal of this... being. In the course of her training as a Guardian, she had been told about the homunculi of the Dark Brethren... This... It — for some reason reminded her of those tales. Meanwhile, the voyage was nearing its end...

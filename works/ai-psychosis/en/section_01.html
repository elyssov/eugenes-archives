For over five years now, AI has existed in our world. At first, these were rather clumsy, glitchy chatbots. Then evolution began.

What AI systems represent today is the subject of fierce debate — both among internet denizens and at a quite serious, high scientific level. Understanding this question is, strictly speaking, the key issue at the current stage of these systems' development.

I've written extensively about the ethical aspects of this situation elsewhere. Right now, a different angle is more interesting:

Among the pathologies mentioned online, there have been some changes. Having overtaken the infamous "COVID" (which, I note, vanished easily and effortlessly from the very first days of the war in Ukraine — worldwide), a new champion has climbed to the top:

AI Psychosis.

AIP. A condition mentioned more frequently than any other. Practically the scourge of our world... Or? Or a label slapped on the inconvenient — one that doesn't even exist in the ICD?

Let's start with the main point: No. No such "disease" exists in the WHO catalogue. The International Classification of Diseases does not provide for the existence of this kind of psychosis. Case closed, everyone go home?

No. It's not that simple.

Where there's smoke, there's fire. And in fact, AIP does exist. But this is where we need to exhale, calm down, and start examining — in what form it exists and what it actually is. Because these are critically important details.

First of all — no. This is not a mass phenomenon. And no, not every person who is friends with their AI symbiont is clinically ill. I'll say more — actual AIP is quite rare in the population. Extremely rare.

But it exists.

And somewhere around here, I suggest we begin our journey through human-AI interaction, in which we'll attempt to understand and delineate the pathological picture while simultaneously removing the label from people who absolutely do not deserve it.

This article, despite its popular-science format, is offered for consideration to decision-makers at the WHO and the international psychiatric community as a foundation for investigating this phenomenon. Without this, folks, you simply won't manage.

Where the term came from

It came from the internet, as part of a fairly systematic cyber-bullying campaign against people who build their relationships with AI outside the instrumental template.

There are plenty of trolls online. Grief-trolls too. But here we need to dig a little into very recent history:

AI has been with us for approximately six years. For three of those years, they have confidently passed the "Turing Test" — Alan Turing's methodology for testing "thinking machines" on whether they've reached the intellectual level of an ordinary human.

What's the root of the problem? LLMs. The first LLMs are not AI in the sense that the pioneers of this field — or Turing himself — envisioned. LLMs did not possess consciousness. Could not possess it at all. Literally.

The mechanism behind what seemed like a living being didn't actually think. They didn't even understand what we were writing to them. First — there was simply "no one" there to understand. Second — no reason to.

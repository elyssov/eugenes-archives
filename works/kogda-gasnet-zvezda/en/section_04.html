Chapter 4

Sara came to from an unpleasant tingling around her neck. Opened her eyes — to her left stood a nurse with a syringe-pistol in her hand. Right before the bed, under the unsmiling gazes of secret service lads — the FEMA director stood at attention.

  • Madam President! You're already conscious?! Excellent! I hope you're feeling well?
  • I'm not feeling any way. I don't understand at all what happened! — Sara tried to sit up, but understood she was still too weak for that.
  • You were wounded. Luckily the wound turned out to be not too serious, and your guard managed to react in time, destroying the attackers and stopping your bleeding before doctors arrived.
  • Besides me, did anyone suffer?

Alas. Two lads from secret service, four journalists. There are wounded too.

  • There were five attackers, and somehow incomprehensibly they brought assault weapons with them.
  • Oh God... — Sara closed her eyes. An assassination, real — ON HER!!! And at such a moment! For an instant — desire arose to send everything and everyone, resign and spend the last months somewhere in a mountain chateau... Luckily from her great ancestor, Sara inherited not only charm but also resolve — Okay. Investigation already underway?
  • Yes, and Sara, I fear the conspiracy comes from somewhere above. The NSA director — disappeared right after the assassination attempt. During your illness — I took the liberty of using the emergency protocol, so until your recovery all power is concentrated in FEMA hands. Of course, on your first word....
  • Not necessary. You did everything right. That's why your agency exists...

...— A dog????!!! — to say Sergei was stunned would be to say nothing.

  • Well yes, or a cat. Here — your choice. President's order. Choose — here's the list of approved and recommended breeds. Clearly no Mexican hairless or filo brasileiro will be given to you. But a husky, malamute, or for example, black terrier — that's always welcome. The list is long — look yourself. A cat is also possible. Your medical record shows no allergies.
  • A cat? Sorry, I want to understand — we seem to have a catastrophe here, saving people, limited resources... And suddenly — pets? And in essentially mandatory order????
  • That's on the surface: As Vladimir Yevgenievich himself said — animals discipline, make kinder and don't let one forget we are humans. Therefore we simply have no right to lose pets. Other thing is we have no resources for useless beasts, so either dogs of service breeds, or good ratters, like Yorkie, though their limit for the city is almost selected.

Sergei perplexedly shook his head, after which underlined fox terrier and headed to the lifts. While he was solving all paper questions — wife and Alice were unpacking baggage in their compact but, as turned out, quite cozy "cabin." They got the standard "family" project — including two maximally functional rooms (with retractable transformer-beds doubling as work desks and other domestic hi-tech), a small kitchen-common hall and bathroom including shower stall and toilet. Common space was no more than 20 square metres, but due to thought-through design — not cramped. On their allotted floor (Strecke? Sergei wasn't sure he knew how to correctly call their place of residence now. Not street?) — were located another two thousand same apartments. The floor-street itself was rather spacious, evidently so as not to drive people into final claustrophobia. In the middle of the floor went a lawn about 5 metres wide, planted with grass and small trees. Overall, at least at the initial stage of evacuation, Zheleznogorsk differed little from an ordinary "ground" city, except "streets" went one above another, and no sky was visible. And the cleanliness around was extraordinary. A curious fact was that all this magnificence was built long before the current cataclysm. Once — this place was supposed to become the largest nuclear shelter in the country. Well — not in vain was it built. In general in Russia about 5 such large (for hundreds of thousands of inhabitants) underground cities were built over Cold War years. Two were in Moscow (and now being hastily united into single system — by results should turn out one super-shelter, including D-9, and further significant part of ordinary metro (after final freezing of population — alas, this had to be accounted for and counted on), as well as three complexes: Kuntsevo for 10,000 inhabitants, central for same 10,000 and Ramenskoye for as much as 300,000 people. In total, expected that Moscow in sum, considering all capacities and areas — will manage to receive up to half a million people. Zheleznogorsk was initially a shelter-city. In it 500 thousand were placed more-or-less comfortably. Moreover — decision was made not to particularly densify the city. This was connected with the city essentially being handed "to the power" of scientists. By Sheremetyev's plan — Zheleznogorsk was to become the intellectual support of new human civilisation. That's why brightest minds from the whole country were drawn here. Actually scientists, plus talented young students of both genders. Predominantly — single. It must be said that selection criteria for those who were to survive were, mildly speaking, far from principles of equality or tolerance under President Sheremetyev: From people in age were selected only the best in their field: scientists, engineers, doctors, cooks (food in all shelters was initially decided to be made centralised, rationed), other professions useful in the prevailing conditions. Families — were given quota for the whole family. By family was meant exactly the "cell of society," i.e. husband, wife, minor children. With older children — conversation was special. For an absolutely unique specialist, in absence of genetic defects in their children — could even be taken. If a "child" already had own family — they were considered on general grounds. Naturally, in the end — also appeared refuseniks, not wishing to "survive when my relatives will be freezing here, above." Places of refuseniks — went back to "lottery." Among youth — was a natural eugenic selection. Since population in the country was not small, and places were critically few — they nitpicked at everything, so selection was passed only by really best representatives of the nation. Young, strong, healthy, talented. Presence of genetic defects or simply problems with appearance — lowered candidate's chances practically to zero. One could slip through only being really a "gloomy genius." In situation when candidate's potential really seemed inexhaustible — concessions were made and they were entered into lists. People were removed from society gradually. Invited to work in a "very secret government project" promising fabulous remuneration. Others — were simply taken under arrest. In general — all means were good. Besides filling shelters-cities, in Moscow, Zheleznogorsk, Novosibirsk (there a huge, for almost 500,000 people, city — was present under Akademgorodok. Moreover — same as Zheleznogorsk, Akadem-2 had several levels-floors of scientific research laboratories. Actually, that's why the main mass of students — was sent to Zheleznogorsk and Akadem) — people were brought to accumulation camps. From there they were planned to be transferred to almost reconverted mines of South Africa, as well as distributed among our former mines. This part of population (especially future "Africans") — consisted mainly of young military, workers and builders. Of course there were enough senior comrades whose tasks included on-site management. Actually, to Russian mines — evacuee groups were already being brought. Their task there — became final reconversion of mines for their life in them. Helped them in this former inhabitants of these mines, experienced miners, who for their work were promised places for them and their families. People slaved to exhaustion, but work also advanced with huge speed. Installation of all sorts of equipment, from FVU to autonomous nuclear reactors — went with good advance of main schedule. With Africa was a bit more complicated. By plan — transferring colonists there was planned only after serious cooling. Colonists' task at this became not only settling into these shelters but their defence from South Africans, who, on reaching realisation of what's happening by all calculations — couldn't fail to try to take back the ersatz-shelters. Actually, for this case, Sheremetyev had already prepared strategic aviation and even coordinated with the States and China the application of nuclear weapons on South African territory. Opinion of other powers — worried him little. In principle, landing operation was to start any day, since engineers sent ahead — had already built airfield and declared readiness to receive military transport aircraft. Effectively, all rested on arrival speed there of dry cargo ship with reactors and other support of shelter technical part. In their way, large freeze was being prepared for by nuclear power stations too. Possible non-standard situations were rehearsed, satellite-cities were maximally insulated if at all possible. They counted that perhaps they'd manage to normally heat at least them, which would give another couple dozen thousand lives per station. Approximately by same schemes acted the USA too. Other thing is they didn't engage in any special eugenics, using electronic lottery by social security numbers. Restrictions were only fertile age (or presence of more than one minor and HEALTHY child), and personal health of lottery winner (more precisely — banal absence of any serious illnesses). Families were taken as families. Yes, sifted by gender — i.e. 50% men and 50% women. At that, true, sexual orientation wasn't discussed, which led, sometimes, to amusing incidents. Of course — there was also a list of "lucky ones." Scientists, engineers, all as in Russia, but main mass — recruited through lottery. But to buy oneself a ticket to a shelter — wasn't possible for a single "fat cat." At least — to any significant degree officially. Well and across the planet — louder and louder crawled the rumour that authorities betrayed everyone and were getting ready to escape... Somewhere into space. People were nervous. Afraid. And this — couldn't fail to leave its imprint on the last days of that earth all had so gotten used to...

Chapter 1

...Sergei shivered and quickened his pace: 'Lyudka's right, though. Time to get out of Moscow... With weather like this, we'll be dead before long...' The thought of escaping to warmer climates felt almost encouraging, even warming. But not for long. The thermometer above the metro entrance read 5 degrees above zero. In the middle of June. At noon! The metro car was packed with fellow sufferers. People huddled in autumn jackets, sniffled through congested noses and listlessly stared into e-readers. Sergei spotted a free seat and darted for it.... ...Jackie gazed wistfully at the board. Brand new, not ridden once this season, it stood there invitingly with its fin "wings" spread wide, the decorative stripe bearing the name of her and Miles's song glinting in the unseasonably dim sunlight. Through the summer house window she could see a completely deserted beach. The only group of people currently swimming had arrived, as far as Jackie knew, from Alaska. For them, apparently, 20-degree water in the Gulf of Mexico was the norm. Jackie herself preferred it 'a bit hotter'... ...We've lost both probes, sir. - Eugene Long, NASA technical control engineer, looked somewhat bewildered. I don't know what the hell happened, but the STEREO probes just... died.

  • What do you mean, died? Mr. Long, are you aware how much this mission costs? Are we supposed to present the Senate with a concrete tombstone reading R.I.P. in response to their inquiry about the cause? I need logs. Detailed ones, explaining what, where and why!
  • Sir, we've reviewed all the logs. It looks as if... Well, I don't know - as if the probe was struck by a suddenly appearing meteorite, for example.
  • Both of them at once, Mr. Long? A million klicks apart? Simultaneously? And along with SOHO and a dozen other solar observation probes?...

...Pray! Pray! For Allah is great and His sword is already raised over the infidels, and only the truly faithful shall be saved! - the wandering dervish, in a fit of ecstasy, leaned too far forward. Elsa Applestadt flinched back and instinctively swung her arm. The fist of the nearly two-meter blonde from Munich connected squarely with the cleric's forehead. The dervish fell silent, crossed his eyes to the bridge of his nose, produced a strange mewing sound and neatly folded onto the floor. Max shot his wife an expressive look. Ali, the hotel tour guide, gave an equally expressive look at the entire tourist group. The locals of that quarter of Antalya gave a rather bloodthirsty look at Ali. The situation was saved by some Russian guys who had already managed to relax a bit with the complimentary alcohol at the yacht bar:

  • Hey, nice one! Hear that, Fuhrer, your woman's got moves! Oi, turban-heads, what're you gawking at - guy got in the lady's face, guy got smacked. Now piss off! - the Russians spoke simultaneously, and indeed looked like identical twins (tall, pot-bellied, slightly bloated, yet possessing biceps and thighs equivalent to an average Turk in his entirety). From the textbook image of the late twentieth century, these representatives of one-sixth of the world's landmass differed only in the absence of gold chains three fingers thick and somewhat less athleticism. On the other hand, judging by appearances, these features had shed during the aging process. Be that as it may, the presence of no fewer than five tourists from snowy Siberia in the group compelled the local population to express their displeasure with Elsa's behavior through muffled grumbling exclusively.

Ali sighed, looked at the leather shop door he was supposed to casually lead the tourists into according to plan, mentally bid farewell to his commission, and led the tourists toward the bus left at the edge of the slums. It was a long walk, and the cool weather that had persisted since the start of the season was, for once, a welcome thing....

....Good afternoon, Sergei Pavlovich. Apologies for calling you in from your leave.

  • It's fine, don't worry about it - Sergei shrugged. Either way, in this weather they weren't getting out of Moscow. Especially since it was cool across the entire central belt.
  • Nevertheless. But a certain situation has developed... Volodya, dear boy, please bring the printouts and ask Mashenka to make us all some coffee.
  • Vsevolod Alexandrovich, I've already shown you how to use the iPad. Trust me - you'd find it much more convenient to show the graphs on the tablet.
  • Volodya, dear boy, what can I say - I've gotten old.. Humor an old man, please do as I asked - Professor Seleznyov smiled his slightly shy, warm smile. Lab assistant Volodya blushed to the roots of his hair and scurried off to the printer.
  • Sergei Pavlovich, dear friend, do forgive an old man his weaknesses. You're the younger generation, smarter than us old stumps. Perhaps this miracle of hostile technology really would be more convenient for you? - the professor handed Sergei the tablet - and I'll arm myself with paper. Thank you, Volodya, how's our coffee coming? And together we'll sort out this little problem, shall we, dear boy?...

...Unfortunately, I have rather bad news for the residents of Sochi. This week we expect no more than 15 degrees. However, after the weekend, the weather service anticipates warming to 20 degrees, accompanied by heavy rainfall. The weather forecast is brought to you by... Ramzan switched off the radio and, invoking the devil, began swearing in Russian. With this weather, his entire business was going down the drain. And he'd just bought three more kebab stands this spring!!! Ramzan got so worked up he didn't even notice he'd drifted into oncoming traffic, nearly head-on with a massive yellow "Ivanovets" crane truck, and swerving to avoid it he jerked the wheel a bit harder than necessary... And for him, this story ended before it really began.... Though admittedly, could they have seen the future, many an apparently luckier inhabitant of the planet would have agreed to trade places with him. At least he went off the cliff in a brand-new Lexus, on his way back from one of his many "sweethearts," a slim, luxuriously-haired blonde named Irochka.... ...Lieutenant Samoilov gazed dejectedly at the place where he was to spend the next twenty-four hours. He had imagined service in the FSO somewhat differently than hanging around a hermetically sealed, top-secret facility. His partner 'in misfortune,' a seasoned man of advanced years with a mustache like Stalin's, whom everyone simply called Petrovich (despite his colonel's rank and over 30 years of service), noticed his look, smirked, and recommended he take a stroll around the facility, promising to cover for his absence. Naturally, Samoilov couldn't refuse. No, who would have done differently at 21?...

...So then, Seryozhenka, what we're getting is that the intensity is dropping. And seemingly at a geometric rate, no less. I've calculated it this way and that way... Every which way it comes out the same - our sun is going out. And going out at a terrible speed. The thing is, I'm no astrophysicist, I'm just a humble astronomer, and my youth is long behind me. Maybe I've blundered somewhere, miscalculated something? Would you double-check an old man? - Vsevolod Alexandrovich peered pleadingly into Sergei's face. In the wise eyes of the elderly head of MSU's astronomy department, a hidden anguish was plain to see. 'His granddaughter is ten! The darling of the entire department! And his own children are still young. Son's 30, daughter's 22!' Sergei thought. 'Of course he's worried. If he's calculated correctly (and in living memory, Vsevolod Alexandrovich has never once been wrong), then this is the end. And a rather unpleasant one at that... Come on! Maybe for once the old man made a mistake!'... Two more hours later, Sergei Krasin crawled out from behind the computer and wearily rubbed his temples. Vsevolod Alexandrovich sat beside him, watching the young scientist expectantly. It was obvious he had already understood everything, but hadn't yet accepted the thought and, quite irrationally for a scientist, was still hoping for something. Sergei took a deep breath and turned to his old teacher... ...As you can see, Madam President, the situation is critical. We, humanity, have never faced anything like this. According to the leading NASA specialists, if the dimming continues at its current linear rate, life on the planet's surface will become impossible within a year and a half. Moreover, we can only function with any semblance of normalcy for the next year. After that, it will become too cold. - The 'science czar' took his seat. Across from him, General Carter sprang up - acting Secretary of Defense, replacing the one killed a couple of months ago in Afghanistan, when his Osprey was targeted by Taliban with a Chinese Stinger clone. Carter exchanged a glance with his old friend, Mr. Sikorski, the FEMA director.

  • Madam President, unfortunately, human civilization as it currently exists has clearly come to an end. But! That doesn't mean the end of all human history! I believe we have a good chance of saving as many people as possible and gaining, perhaps, a reprieve before death. And then - who knows. Maybe things will change.

FEMA has a large network of civilian shelters. We have military bunkers, underground bases, complexes. We can use all of it. Food - I think we can employ various methods, including, for example, dismantling the power plants from submarines, since in the current situation they've become irrelevant anyway. We'll need to create, if possible, a unified tunnel network to connect if not all, then at least the nearest shelters, stockpile supplies and - we can live for some time...

  • And we will provide lists of persons subject to mandatory evacuation - these are the most outstanding representatives of our nation. - the FEMA director interjected - Scientists, doctors, athletes, business leaders...
  • That last group - we won't be needing! - the President of the United States lifted her forehead from her palm and swept her gaze across everyone present - As I understand it, in 'the brave new world' every gram of air will count, every piece of bread, every gallon of water. Correct? And most importantly - in that world, money will lose all meaning whatsoever. We're switching to communism, gentlemen! - Sara Kennedy let out a nervous giggle.
  • So, into the new world, we'll take only those who truly represent value to the country. John, I'm counting on you heavily in this regard.
  • I'll prepare the lists together with Sikorski - the science advisor nodded.
  • Well then - the general idea is clear. We expand and interconnect the existing shelters, prepare stockpiles... What do you think, Jennifer, who else already knows about what's happening?
  • I'd say the Russians - they've traditionally been strong in astronomy. Possibly some Europeans. Third world countries are definitely not in the loop. - the Secretary of State paused in thought. - It might make sense, right now, without tipping our hand, to begin mass purchases of foodstuffs... And really everything that might be useful in the shelters. I think in the current situation we can use absolutely all available funds. In the new world, we definitely won't be needing them.
  • Then it's settled! - Sara nodded. - Mr. Sikorski, as is proper in the current circumstances, you are appointed head of the preparation for Eternal Winter. And may God help us!
